## Mitochondrial Biogenesis & Molecular Adaptation

### Holloszy 1967 — Foundational: Exercise Doubles Mitochondrial Capacity
**"Biochemical adaptations in muscle: effects of exercise on mitochondrial oxygen uptake and respiratory enzyme activity in skeletal muscle"**
*J Biol Chem. 242(9):2278-2282*

- Strenuous treadmill running in rats doubled mitochondrial capacity to oxidize pyruvate
- Succinate dehydrogenase, cytochrome c reductase, succinate oxidase, and cytochrome oxidase all increased ~2-fold
- Total mitochondrial protein increased ~60%
- Mild exercise (30 min swimming) was insufficient; progressive overload over 12 weeks was needed
- The increase in mitochondrial capacity explains reduced lactate at submaximal exercise — lower Pi and ADP slow glycolysis
- **Why it matters for WKO5:** This is the molecular basis for why FTP improves with training. Mitochondrial biogenesis shifts the lactate threshold rightward on the power-duration curve.

### Akimoto et al. 2005 — PGC-1alpha / p38 MAPK Pathway
**"Exercise stimulates PGC-1alpha transcription in skeletal muscle through activation of the p38 MAPK pathway"**
*J Biol Chem. 280(20):19587-19593*

- Running activates p38 MAPK → ATF2 → PGC-1alpha transcription
- Single bout: transient PGC-1alpha mRNA increase. 4 weeks training: sustained 50% protein increase
- Training induced fiber-type switching from type IIb (fast glycolytic) → type IIa (fast oxidative), with 250% increase in MHC IIa protein
- Blocking p38 or ATF2 eliminates the effect
- **Why it matters:** PGC-1alpha is the "master regulator" of mitochondrial biogenesis. Each training session triggers this cascade; accumulated sessions drive structural adaptation. This is why CTL correlates with fitness.

### Wright et al. 2007 — Calcium / CaMK Pathway to Mitochondrial Biogenesis
**"Calcium induces increases in PGC-1alpha and mitochondrial biogenesis by a pathway leading to p38 MAPK activation"**
*J Biol Chem. 282(26):18793-18799*

- Signaling chain: Ca²⁺ release from sarcoplasmic reticulum → CaMK → p38 MAPK → ATF-2 → PGC-1alpha → mitochondrial biogenesis
- This is distinct from the AMPK energy-depletion pathway — two independent routes to adaptation
- Every pedal stroke releases calcium, triggering this cascade
- **Why it matters:** Training volume matters because the calcium signal is cumulative — more contractions = more signaling. Both volume (calcium pathway) and intensity (AMPK pathway) drive adaptation through different molecular routes.

### Leick et al. 2010 — Adaptation Gene Expression Peaks 10-24h Post-Exercise
**"Endurance exercise induces mRNA expression of oxidative enzymes in human skeletal muscle late in recovery"**
*Scand J Med Sci Sports. 2010*

- After 90-min cycling at 70% VO2max, key oxidative enzyme mRNA upregulated 100-300% at 10-24 hours
- No mRNA changes in first 0-8 hours post-exercise
- PGC-1alpha and GLUT4 mRNA elevated 100-200% at 10-24h
- No protein changes at these timepoints — protein accumulation requires repeated bouts
- **Why it matters:** The 10-24h delay has implications for training frequency and recovery. Interrupting recovery too early may blunt the response. Supports adequate recovery between hard sessions.

### Howarth et al. 2004 — TCA Cycle Efficiency Improves with Training
**"Effect of endurance training on muscle TCA cycle metabolism during exercise in humans"**
*J Appl Physiol. 97:579-584*

- 7 weeks of training at ~80% VO2peak: TTE at 80% VO2peak more than doubled (42 → 91 min)
- TCA cycle intermediate pool expansion was attenuated ~45-50% post-training, yet endurance improved
- Resting glycogen increased 58%; muscle lactate accumulation reduced ~70%
- Citrate synthase activity increased 32%
- **Why it matters:** FTP is not limited by TCA cycle pool size but by mitochondrial density and efficiency. Validates Sweet Spot (~80% VO2peak) training for building metabolic machinery.

## Cardiovascular & Oxygen Delivery

### Gledhill et al. 1994 — Stroke Volume Doesn't Plateau in Trained Athletes
**"Endurance athletes' stroke volume does not plateau: major advantage is diastolic function"**
*Med Sci Sports Exerc. 1994*

- Untrained: stroke volume plateaus at ~120 bpm. Trained cyclists: stroke volume increases progressively to max HR with no plateau
- Major advantage is enhanced diastolic (filling) function, not systolic (emptying)
- At 190 bpm, ventricular filling rate 66-71% greater in trained vs untrained; emptying rate only 15-20% greater
- Trained subjects had larger blood volumes (4994 vs 4457 mL), higher VO2max (68.6 vs 44.1 mL/kg/min)
- **Why it matters:** Explains why trained athletes sustain higher power at any given HR. Each heartbeat delivers more blood. This is the cardiovascular basis for FTP and VO2max improvements.

### Montero et al. 2015 — VO2peak Gains Are Primarily Haematological
**"Haematological rather than skeletal muscle adaptations contribute to the increase in peak oxygen uptake induced by moderate endurance training"**
*J Physiol. 593(20):4677-4688*

- 6 weeks of moderate training (~65% Wpeak): VO2peak increased 9%, Wpeak increased 16%
- After phlebotomy to reset red blood cell volume to pre-training levels, VO2peak reverted to baseline
- Despite +18% capillary-to-fiber ratio and +43% mitochondrial volume density, these peripheral adaptations alone were insufficient to increase VO2peak
- **Why it matters:** For newer cyclists, moderate-intensity training below FTP drives VO2max gains via blood volume expansion. For well-trained athletes with maximized haematological adaptations, peripheral adaptations (FTP/VO2max ratio) become relatively more important.

### Montero & Lundby 2019 — Red Blood Cell Volume Regulation
**"Regulation of red blood cell volume with exercise training"**
*Comprehensive Physiology. 9(1):149-164*

- Endurance champions may have 40% more RBCV than controls; RBCV can increase up to 10% with months of training
- RBCV increases are the main factor leading to VO2max changes
- Plasma volume expansion occurs first (within sessions), triggering hematocrit drop, which stimulates EPO production and RBCV expansion
- Multi-month timeline for full RBCV adaptation
- **Why it matters:** Early in a training block, athletes may feel sluggish despite improving fitness (PV expansion before RBCV catches up). Meaningful VO2max-driven improvements at 3-8 min durations on the PD curve require sustained training over months.

### Detraining & Blood Volume
**"Effects of detraining on cardiovascular responses to exercise: role of blood volume"**
*J Appl Physiol.*

- Blood volume reduction is a primary mediator of cardiovascular deconditioning during detraining
- Loss of plasma volume and red cell mass reduces stroke volume, cardiac output, and VO2max rapidly
- **Why it matters:** Explains why power metrics (FTP, VO2max power) decline during detraining. Blood volume is one of the fastest-declining adaptations, matching the CTL decay rates in WKO5's PMC.

## Critical Power & VO2 Kinetics

### Vanhatalo et al. 2011 — VO2 Slow Component and CP/W'
**"Muscle fiber recruitment and the slow component of O2 uptake: constant work rate vs. all-out sprint exercise"**
*Am J Physiol Regul Integr Comp Physiol. 300:R700-R707*

- VO2 slow component arises from loss of efficiency within already-recruited fibers, not just progressive fiber recruitment
- During all-out test: iEMG declined 26% (fiber de-recruitment) yet VO2 remained at VO2max
- CP (critical power) estimated from final 30s of 3-min all-out test; W' from power-time integral above CP
- CP/VO2max ratio inversely correlated with W' (r = -0.89): athletes with high FTP/VO2max ratio tend to have smaller anaerobic reserve
- **Why it matters:** Explains why efforts above CP/FTP are disproportionately costly. The hyperbolic relationship above CP on the power-duration curve is governed by W' depletion plus the VO2 slow component driving VO2 toward VO2max.

## Endurance Determinants & Fiber Type

### Coyle et al. — Determinants of Endurance in Well-Trained Cyclists
**"Determinants of endurance in well-trained cyclists"**
*J Appl Physiol. 64(6)*

- 14 competitive cyclists with similar VO2max (4.6-5.0 L/min) showed vastly different endurance
- Key differentiator: lactate threshold location — Group H reached LT at 81.5% VO2max vs Group L at 65.8%
- TTE at 88% VO2max was 2x longer in Group H (60.8 vs 29.1 min)
- 92% of performance variance explained by %VO2max at LT + muscle capillary density
- Group H had more Type I fibers (66.7% vs 46.9%)
- **Why it matters:** Foundational paper for power-based training. VO2max alone doesn't determine endurance. FTP as % of VO2max power is the critical variable — exactly what WKO5's PD modeling captures.

### Coyle et al. 1991 — Fiber Type and Elite Performance
**"Physiological and biomechanical factors associated with elite endurance cycling performance"**
*Med Sci Sports Exerc. 23(1):93-107*

- Sprinters: steep PD curve (high Pmax relative to FTP) = more fast-twitch
- Time trialists: flatter curve = more slow-twitch
- **Why it matters:** Basis for WKO5's fiber type estimation from the PD curve shape.

## AMPK & Energy Sensing

### Kjobsted et al. 2018 — AMPK Comprehensive Review
**"AMPK in skeletal muscle function and metabolism"**
*FASEB J. 32:1741-1777*

- AMPK is the master cellular energy sensor, activated when AMP/ATP and ADP/ATP ratios rise during exercise
- Activation is intensity- and duration-dependent:
  - Short high-intensity → alpha2-beta2-gamma3 complexes (via LKB1)
  - Long low-intensity → alpha1 complexes (via CaMKK-beta / Ca²⁺)
- AMPK promotes glucose uptake (GLUT4), fat oxidation (ACC2 phosphorylation), and mitochondrial biogenesis (PGC-1alpha)
- AMPK inhibits mTORC1, suppressing protein synthesis during energy stress — important for concurrent strength/endurance training
- Glycogen inhibits AMPK via beta-subunit GBD — low glycogen enhances AMPK signaling ("train low" mechanism)
- Trained muscle shows reduced AMPK activation at same absolute/relative intensity = improved metabolic homeostasis
- **Why it matters:** Explains zone-specific adaptations at the molecular level. Glycogen-AMPK interaction explains train-low benefits. AMPK-mTOR antagonism explains concurrent training interference.

### Wadley et al. 2006 — AMPK Responds to Absolute Power, Not Relative Intensity
**"Effect of exercise intensity and hypoxia on skeletal muscle AMPK signaling and substrate metabolism in humans"**
*Am J Physiol Endocrinol Metab. 290:E694-E702*

- AMPK activation determined by absolute power output (watts), not relative intensity (%VO2peak)
- Glycogen use, lactate, and catecholamines scale with relative intensity
- **Why it matters:** Supports using absolute power targets (watts) for prescribing training rather than HR or RPE alone. At altitude where absolute power drops, adaptation signals may be blunted despite high relative effort.

## Glycogen, Substrate Metabolism & Adaptation Signaling

### Yeo et al. 2010 — Low Glycogen Amplifies AMPK During HIT
**"Acute signalling responses to intense endurance training commenced with low or normal muscle glycogen"**
*Exp Physiol. 95(2):351-358*

- Trained cyclists doing HIT (8x5 min max) with low vs normal glycogen
- AMPK phosphorylation significantly greater in low glycogen despite 12% lower absolute power (270 vs 308 W)
- **Why it matters:** Molecular rationale for "train low, compete high." Coaches should expect lower wattages during glycogen-depleted sessions — this doesn't reflect reduced fitness.

### Baar & McGee 2008 — Optimizing Adaptations by Manipulating Glycogen
**"Optimizing training adaptations by manipulating glycogen"**
*Eur J Sport Sci. 2008*

- Low muscle glycogen amplifies AMPK and p38 MAPK, driving PGC-1alpha and mitochondrial biogenesis
- Power/velocity at lactate threshold is a better performance predictor than VO2max for elite athletes
- Proposes "train low/compete high": glycogen-depleted base training for mitochondrial adaptations, glycogen-loaded for competition

### Hawley & Burke 2010 — CHO Availability and Training Adaptation
**"Carbohydrate availability and training adaptation: effects on cell metabolism"**
*Exerc Sport Sci Rev. 2010*

- Training with low glycogen enhances citrate synthase, COX-IV, fat oxidation enzymes via AMPK/PGC-1alpha
- Multiple strategies: twice-daily training, fasted training, withholding CHO during sessions, restricting CHO during recovery
- However, metabolic adaptations do NOT consistently translate to improved time-trial performance
- NRF-1, NRF-2, and PGC-1alpha are key transcription regulators

### Hetlelid et al. 2015 — Well-Trained Athletes Burn 3x More Fat During HIT
**"Rethinking the role of fat oxidation: substrate utilisation during high-intensity interval training"**
*BMJ Open Sport Exerc Med. 2015*

- Well-trained runners oxidized nearly 3x more fat than recreationally trained during HIT (0.57 vs 0.20 g/min)
- Fat oxidation rates correlated strongly with VO2max (r=0.86)
- Well-trained athletes worked at 89-96% VO2max during intervals with lower RER (0.88 vs 0.95)
- **Why it matters:** Ability to oxidize fat at high intensities is a hallmark of fitness. Well-trained athletes spare glycogen even during intervals, enabling higher sustainable power.

### Rauch et al. 2022 — Fat Oxidation Peaks at 63% VO2max
**"Feeding tolerance, glucose availability, and whole-body total carbohydrate and fat oxidation in male endurance and ultra-endurance runners"**
*Front Physiol. 12:773054*

- Fat oxidation peaked at 63% VO2max (Fatmax), ceased at 94% VO2max (Fatmin)
- 43% of participants achieved MFO rates >1.0 g/min on a standard mixed diet (not keto)
- **Why it matters:** Maps onto WKO5 zones: Zone 2/Endurance maximizes fat oxidation; Threshold+ relies entirely on CHO.

## Testing Protocols & Measurement Validity

### Jamnick et al. 2018 — GXT Protocol Affects LT and VO2peak Validity
**"Manipulating graded exercise test variables affects the validity of the lactate threshold and VO2peak"**
*PLOS ONE. 13(7):e0199794*

- 17 trained cyclists tested across five GXT protocols (1, 3, 4, 7, 10-min stages)
- 14 different LT calculation methods compared — results varied by ~95W range in one subject (MLSS was 302W)
- Modified Dmax method from 4-min stage GXT best predicted MLSS (mean difference 1.1W, ICC=0.96)
- VO2peak from shorter stages was higher than longer stages
- **Why it matters:** Lab-derived VO2max and LT are protocol-dependent. WKO5's field-based PD modeling from actual ride data may be more ecologically valid. Coaches should be cautious comparing lab values across different protocols.

### Pinot & Grappe 2014 — MAP Determination from Field Data
**"Determination of maximal aerobic power on the field in cycling"**
*J Sci Cycling. 3(1):26-31*

- Professional cyclists: MAP = 476W (7.02 W/kg); Elite: 433W (6.70 W/kg)
- MAP corresponds to power sustainable for ~3.8-4.4 min — aligns with WKO5's modeled peak aerobic power
- Aerobic Endurance Index (AEI) from slope of %MAP vs Log(t) — analogous to WKO5's "stamina" metric
- Professionals had 9.9% higher MAP but 13.5% shorter T_MAP — ceiling power and time-at-ceiling are somewhat independent
- %MAP-Log(t) equation: %MAP = -9.67 log(t) + 113.2 (R=0.99)
- **Why it matters:** Directly validates WKO5's Record Power Profile methodology. Provides reference values for elite/pro cyclists.

### Ramp Protocol Effects on Peak Power
**"Effects of step duration in incremental ramp protocols on peak power and maximal oxygen consumption"**

- Step duration affects measured peak power but VO2max is protocol-independent
- Shorter steps yield higher peak power values
- **Why it matters:** Comparing peak power across different test protocols requires caution. VO2max is robust to protocol variation but the power at which it occurs is not.

## Training Stress & Recovery Models

### Tucker et al. 2016 — EPOC After Different Exercise Types
**"Excess postexercise oxygen consumption after high-intensity and sprint interval exercise, and continuous steady-state exercise"**
*J Strength Cond Res. 2016*

- Sprint intervals: greatest EPOC (22.0 L / 110 kcal) and post-exercise fat oxidation
- Steady-state: greatest total energy expenditure (348 kcal vs 271 for sprint)
- EPOC is modest (~60-110 kcal over 3 hours) regardless of modality
- **Why it matters:** Training zone prescription should target specific physiological adaptations (FTP, VO2max, anaerobic capacity) rather than caloric considerations. EPOC alone doesn't explain body composition changes from interval training.

### Hemodynamic Adaptations to Exercise
*Am J Cardiol.*

- Central (stroke volume, cardiac output) and peripheral (capillary density, O2 extraction) adaptations together determine the ceiling of aerobic power
- High-volume endurance work drives capillarization; high-intensity intervals challenge maximal cardiac output
