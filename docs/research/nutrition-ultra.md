# Nutrition for Ultra-Endurance Cycling: Research Synthesis

## 1. Caloric Deficit Management

### The Inevitable Deficit

Ultra-endurance cycling creates an energy expenditure that is physiologically impossible to match with intake. This is the central nutritional challenge.

**Energy expenditure estimates (cycling):**
- Moderate intensity (~60% VO2max, ~0.65 IF): approximately 600-900 kcal/hr depending on body mass, efficiency, terrain
- A 75kg rider at 200W averages roughly 720 kcal/hr
- 600km brevet (20-24 hrs): 12,000-18,000 kcal total expenditure
- 1200km brevet (60-90 hrs): 30,000-50,000 kcal total expenditure

**Maximal realistic intake:**
- Gastric emptying and intestinal absorption limit carbohydrate delivery to roughly 90-120 g/hr (360-480 kcal/hr from carbs) under optimal conditions
- Adding fat and protein from solid food, practical ceiling is approximately 400-600 kcal/hr during sustained riding
- Most ultra cyclists actually achieve 200-400 kcal/hr averaged over the full event, including stops

**Deficit thresholds:**
- Cumulative deficits of 4,000-8,000 kcal are routinely observed in 24hr events (Enqvist et al., 2010; Knechtle et al., 2005)
- The body tolerates this by drawing on endogenous glycogen (~2,000 kcal when fully loaded) and adipose tissue (essentially unlimited for events at this intensity — a lean 75kg rider at 10% body fat carries ~67,500 kcal as fat)
- The performance limiter is not total energy per se but **carbohydrate availability** for the brain and for higher-intensity efforts (climbs, headwinds)

**Energy Availability (EA) research:**
- EA = (Energy Intake - Exercise Energy Expenditure) / Fat-Free Mass
- In the Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S) framework, EA below 30 kcal/kg FFM/day is considered the threshold for physiological impairment (Mountjoy et al., 2018, IOC Consensus Statement)
- Below 25 kcal/kg FFM/day, hormonal and metabolic disruption becomes significant
- During a 24hr race, EA is typically deeply negative; the body relies on stored substrate. This is tolerated acutely but has recovery implications
- Key distinction: **acute** low EA during a single event is different from **chronic** low EA. A single ultra event does not cause RED-S, but insufficient recovery nutrition afterward can compound problems

**Practical threshold for performance:**
- The critical factor is hepatic glycogen. The liver stores approximately 80-100g of glycogen and releases glucose at 5-8 g/hr at rest, faster during exercise. When liver glycogen is depleted, blood glucose drops, and CNS function degrades (the "bonk")
- Maintaining carbohydrate intake of at least 40-60 g/hr appears to be the minimum to prevent severe hypoglycemia during prolonged moderate-intensity cycling (Coggan & Coyle, 1987; Jeukendrup, 2014)

**Duration-specific considerations:**
- 6-8 hrs: Deficit is modest (2,000-3,000 kcal). Muscle glycogen still partially available. Standard sports nutrition works.
- 12-16 hrs: Deficit grows to 3,000-6,000 kcal. Muscle glycogen is largely depleted; rider is increasingly reliant on fat oxidation and exogenous carbs. This is where nutrition strategy becomes critical.
- 24+ hrs: Deficit is 5,000-10,000+ kcal. Fully fat-adapted metabolism at lower intensities. Exogenous carbs serve primarily to maintain blood glucose and enable any intensity above fat-max. Solid food becomes psychologically and gastrointestinally necessary.
- 48-72 hrs: Cumulative deficit may reach 15,000-25,000 kcal. Body composition measurably changes (1-3 kg fat loss typical). The risk shifts from acute performance limitation to systemic stress — immune suppression, mood disturbance, impaired wound healing.

## 2. Carbohydrate Intake Sustainability

### The 90g/hr Question

**Current high-performance guidelines:**
- Jeukendrup (2014) established the dose-response for carbohydrate and performance: up to 90g/hr of multiple transportable carbohydrates (glucose:fructose in approximately 2:1 or 1:0.8 ratio) maximizes exogenous carbohydrate oxidation
- More recent work (notably from Asker Jeukendrup's group and the work supporting marathon and Ironman fueling) has pushed this to 100-120 g/hr for elite athletes in events up to 8-10 hours
- These rates use SGLT1 (glucose/galactose transporter, ~60 g/hr max) and GLUT5 (fructose transporter, ~30-40 g/hr max) simultaneously

**The ultra-duration problem:**
- Nearly all research validating 90+ g/hr has been conducted in events lasting 2-5 hours, occasionally up to Ironman distance (~8-12 hrs for age groupers)
- Evidence for sustained 90 g/hr beyond 12 hours is almost entirely anecdotal or observational

**Gut fatigue and the time-course of GI tolerance:**
- Gut training (practicing high carb intake during training) improves tolerance significantly (Cox et al., 2010; Jeukendrup, 2017). SGLT1 expression is upregulated with repeated high-carb exposure over 2-4 weeks
- However, prolonged sympathetic nervous system activation during exercise redirects blood flow away from the splanchnic circulation. Over 12+ hours, intestinal permeability increases (demonstrated by elevated lactulose/rhamnose ratios and plasma I-FABP, a marker of enterocyte damage — Lambert et al., 2008; van Wijck et al., 2012)
- GI symptoms (nausea, bloating, reflux, diarrhea) increase progressively with duration. In RAAM (Race Across America) riders, GI distress prevalence reaches 60-80% (Knechtle et al., 2020 review)
- Anecdotal consensus from ultra-cycling communities: intake rates that feel comfortable at hour 4 often become intolerable by hour 16-20

**Practical sustainable rates by duration:**

| Duration | Sustainable Carb Rate | Notes |
|----------|----------------------|-------|
| 0-6 hrs | 80-120 g/hr | Standard sports nutrition, well-supported |
| 6-12 hrs | 60-90 g/hr | Gut fatigue begins; solid food becomes attractive |
| 12-24 hrs | 40-70 g/hr | Highly individual; palatability fatigue significant |
| 24-48 hrs | 30-60 g/hr | Includes stops; nausea management critical |
| 48-72 hrs | 30-50 g/hr | Real meals at stops; gels often completely rejected |

**Key finding:** The decline in practical intake rate with duration is not primarily an absorption limitation — it is a combination of gut mucosal damage, palatability fatigue (inability to tolerate sweet foods), reduced gastric motility from sympathetic activation, and nausea from sleep deprivation. The gut's ability to absorb remains if food can be tolerated.

**Strategies to maintain intake:**
- Alternate sweet and savory foods (see Section 4)
- Use isotonic or slightly hypotonic carb drinks rather than hypertonic gels when nausea is present
- Real food (rice, potatoes, bread) is often better tolerated than sports-specific products beyond 12 hours
- Small, frequent feeding (every 15-20 min) rather than large boluses
- Reduce intensity temporarily if GI distress is severe — even a 5-10% power reduction significantly improves splanchnic blood flow

## 3. Fat Oxidation and the Crossover Concept

### Fat as the Primary Fuel for Ultras

**The crossover concept (Brooks & Mercier, 1994):**
- At low exercise intensities, fat oxidation provides the majority of energy
- As intensity increases, there is a "crossover point" where carbohydrate becomes the dominant fuel
- Above this intensity, carb dependence rises steeply

**Fat-max and crossover intensity:**
- Fat-max (intensity at which fat oxidation is highest in absolute terms) typically occurs at 45-65% VO2max in trained individuals (Achten & Jeukendrup, 2003)
- The crossover point (where carb and fat contribute equally) is typically at 50-70% VO2max
- In well-trained endurance athletes, fat-max can be higher (55-70% VO2max), and maximal fat oxidation rates are higher (0.5-1.0 g/min vs 0.3-0.5 g/min in untrained) (Venables et al., 2005)
- At fat-max, a well-trained cyclist might oxidize 0.6-1.0 g fat/min = 36-60 g/hr = 324-540 kcal/hr from fat alone

**Training status effects:**
- Endurance training increases: mitochondrial density, capillary density, intramyocellular lipid stores (IMCL), fatty acid transport proteins (FAT/CD36, FABPpm), and enzymes of beta-oxidation
- A well-trained ultra cyclist may derive 60-80% of energy from fat at typical brevet intensity (0.55-0.70 IF)
- This is profoundly important: **at proper ultra pacing, fat provides the majority of fuel, and exogenous carbohydrate primarily serves to maintain blood glucose and spare hepatic glycogen**

**Implications for ultra pacing:**
- Most brevets are ridden at 0.55-0.70 IF (Intensity Factor). This is near or below the crossover point for trained riders
- At this intensity, fat oxidation may contribute 400-600 kcal/hr in a well-trained cyclist
- Combined with 40-60 g/hr carbohydrate intake (160-240 kcal/hr), the effective energy deficit is substantially reduced
- The critical implication: **pacing slightly lower (e.g., 0.60 vs 0.70 IF) dramatically shifts fuel partitioning toward fat, reducing carbohydrate requirements and extending endurance**

**Duration effects on substrate utilization:**
- As exercise continues beyond 2-3 hours and glycogen depletes, fat oxidation increases even at a fixed intensity (the substrate shift)
- After 6+ hours at moderate intensity, even riders with average fat oxidation capacity are predominantly fat-fueled
- This is why the "bonk" in an ultra is qualitatively different from a road race bonk: it is primarily a blood glucose / CNS issue, not a total energy crisis

**Quantitative model for ultra fueling:**
- At 200W average (typical for a strong 600km brevet rider), total energy expenditure is roughly 720 kcal/hr
- At 0.62 IF, fat oxidation may provide ~450 kcal/hr (62%)
- Carb oxidation: ~270 kcal/hr needed
- Endogenous (muscle + liver glycogen): contributes early but depletes
- Required exogenous carbs: 50-65 g/hr to maintain steady state (after glycogen depletion)
- This aligns well with the 40-70 g/hr practical range for sustained ultra intake

## 4. Solid Food Requirements

### When Gels and Liquids Are Not Enough

**The physiology of solid food need:**
- Liquid and semi-liquid carbohydrate sources (gels, drinks, chews) are absorbed faster but provide no satiety
- Ghrelin (hunger hormone) is suppressed by gastric distension and by macronutrient diversity. Liquid-only nutrition fails to suppress ghrelin adequately over long durations
- Psychological need for "real food" becomes overwhelming after 8-12 hours in most riders
- Solid food also provides fat and protein, which liquid strategies largely lack

**Palatability fatigue:**
- Flavor fatigue is well-documented in military nutrition research (Hirsch et al., 2005) and in ultramarathon contexts
- Sweet fatigue is the dominant pattern: after 8-16 hours of gels and sweet drinks, riders develop aversion to sweetness, sometimes progressing to nausea at the sight/smell of sweet products
- This is hypothesized to involve downregulation of sweet taste receptor sensitivity and conditioned taste aversion
- Savory food becomes highly craved, often around 12-16 hours

**Sweet-savory cycling strategy:**
- Successful ultra finishers typically alternate between sweet and savory foods in rough 4-8 hour blocks
- Hours 0-8: Standard sports nutrition (gels, bars, sweet drinks) well tolerated
- Hours 8-16: Mixed approach. Sweet intake declining. Introduction of savory: sandwiches, wraps, rice balls, boiled potatoes with salt
- Hours 16-24: Predominantly savory for many. Real meals at stops. Gels only for short bursts of intensity (climbs)
- Hours 24+: Highly individual. Some riders return to tolerating sweet foods after a sleep break and a meal. Others remain savory-dominant.

**Effective solid foods for ultras (drawn from brevet, RAAM, and bikepacking communities):**
- Rice-based: onigiri/rice balls, rice cakes with nut butter, sushi rice wraps (see Allen Lim's "Feed Zone" recipes, widely adopted in ultra cycling)
- Potato-based: boiled baby potatoes with salt, potato wedges
- Bread-based: PB&J, cheese sandwiches, wraps with savory filling
- Ethnic staples: Japanese convenience store onigiri (extremely popular in Japanese brevets), French baguette with cheese/ham (standard Paris-Brest-Paris food), Turkish flatbread
- Calorie-dense options for carry: nut butters, cheese, salami
- Warm food: soup, ramen, congee (especially valued in cold or night conditions — provides warmth, salt, fluid, and calories simultaneously)

**Gastric emptying of solid food during exercise:**
- Solid food empties from the stomach more slowly than liquid (1-4 hrs for a mixed solid meal vs 20-60 min for liquid carbs)
- This is actually beneficial in ultras: slower, steadier energy delivery, greater satiety, reduced insulin spikes
- The key risk is eating too much solid food before a high-intensity effort (major climb), causing GI distress
- Practical rule: solid food on flat or gentle terrain; switch to liquid/gel before and during significant climbs where intensity rises above fat-max

## 5. Sodium and Electrolyte Needs (12-60 Hours)

### Cumulative Losses and Replacement

**Sweat sodium concentration:**
- Highly individual: ranges from 20-80 mmol/L (460-1,840 mg sodium per liter of sweat)
- Average is approximately 40-50 mmol/L (~1,000-1,150 mg Na/L)
- Can be measured via sweat patch testing (e.g., Precision Hydration, Gatorade Sweat Patch)
- Sweat rate during cycling: 0.5-1.5 L/hr depending on intensity, temperature, humidity, acclimatization

**Cumulative sodium loss estimates:**

| Duration | Sweat Volume (moderate conditions) | Sodium Loss (average sweater) |
|----------|-----------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| 6 hrs | 3-6 L | 3,000-6,000 mg |
| 12 hrs | 6-12 L | 6,000-12,000 mg |
| 24 hrs | 10-20 L | 10,000-20,000 mg |
| 48 hrs | 15-30 L | 15,000-30,000 mg |

Note: these are riding-only estimates. Total event sweat includes rest stops where sweating decreases.

**Hyponatremia risk:**
- Hyponatremia (serum Na < 135 mmol/L) is a serious risk in ultra-endurance events
- Caused primarily by **overdrinking** relative to sodium intake, not by sodium loss alone (Noakes et al., 2005; Hew-Butler et al., 2015)
- Risk factors: slow pace (more time, more opportunity to overdrink), high fluid availability, warm conditions, female sex, NSAIDs (which impair renal free water clearance), low body mass
- Exercise-associated hyponatremia (EAH) prevalence: 5-15% in ultramarathons (asymptomatic), with symptomatic cases at 1-3% (Hoffman et al., 2013)
- Less studied in ultra-cycling specifically, but the same physiology applies
- Prevention: **drink to thirst**, do not force fluid intake on a schedule, include sodium with fluid

**Hypernatremia risk:**
- Less common but occurs in hot conditions with inadequate fluid intake
- More relevant in desert/heat ultras
- Also occurs during sleep deprivation when thirst perception is blunted

**Sodium replacement strategy:**
- Minimum 500-700 mg Na/hr during active riding in moderate conditions
- In heat: 700-1,500 mg Na/hr depending on sweat rate and sweat sodium concentration
- Sources: electrolyte drinks (typically 300-500 mg Na per 500ml serving), salt capsules (200-400 mg per cap), salted food (a single packet of pretzels may contain 400-600 mg Na)
- Salt loading before the event: consuming extra sodium in the 24-48 hours before an ultra can expand plasma volume by 3-5% (Sims et al., 2007), which improves thermoregulation and delays cardiovascular drift

**Other electrolytes:**
- Potassium: lost in sweat at approximately 5-10 mmol/L. Bananas, potatoes, and most real food provide adequate replacement. Supplementation rarely necessary.
- Magnesium: sweat losses are modest (~0.5-1.5 mmol/L). Depletion is more of a chronic training concern. Supplementation during the event is of unclear benefit.
- Calcium: losses modest. Not a primary concern during events.

**Cramping — current evidence:**
- The electrolyte depletion theory of cramping (that cramps are caused by sodium/potassium loss) has been largely supplanted by the neuromuscular fatigue theory (Schwellnus et al., 2009)
- Cramps in ultras are most associated with: excessive intensity relative to training, fatigue, and possibly altered motor neuron excitability
- Sodium supplementation has not been convincingly shown to prevent exercise-associated muscle cramps in controlled trials (Schwellnus et al., 2011)
- However, many ultra riders report empirically that salt intake helps. This may be partly because salt is co-consumed with fluid and calories, addressing multiple deficits simultaneously
- Pickle juice / vinegar has been shown to reduce cramp duration, likely via oropharyngeal TRP channel activation (a neural reflex), not systemic electrolyte correction (Miller et al., 2010)

## 6. Sleep Deprivation and Appetite/Digestion

### The Multi-Day Nutrition Wreck

**Sleep deprivation effects on metabolism:**
- Even a single night of sleep deprivation increases insulin resistance by 20-30% (Donga et al., 2010)
- Glucose tolerance is impaired: blood glucose responses to a given carbohydrate load are higher and more prolonged
- This means the same gel that was efficiently metabolized at hour 6 may cause a higher and more unstable blood glucose response at hour 30 after a night without sleep
- Cortisol is elevated, promoting gluconeogenesis but also muscle protein breakdown and impaired immune function

**Appetite dysregulation:**
- Sleep deprivation increases ghrelin and decreases leptin (Spiegel et al., 2004), which should increase appetite
- However, in ultra-endurance events, the simultaneous sympathetic activation and GI stress often suppresses appetite despite hormonal hunger signals
- The result: riders feel nauseated but simultaneously know they need to eat. This is one of the most miserable aspects of multi-day ultras
- Appetite often partially recovers after even a brief (20-90 min) sleep break. Many experienced ultra riders use a deliberate sleep-then-eat strategy: nap first, then eat a real meal upon waking

**Blood sugar instability:**
- Sleep-deprived riders are more prone to reactive hypoglycemia (overshooting insulin response to carbohydrate intake, causing a blood sugar crash 30-60 minutes later)
- Strategy: favor lower glycemic index foods and mixed macronutrient meals during overnight/sleep-deprived periods
- Avoid large boluses of pure sugar on an empty stomach during the early morning hours (2-5 AM), when insulin sensitivity is at its circadian nadir

**Practical implications for multi-day events:**
- Plan a real meal (warm, savory, mixed macros) before and after any sleep break
- Keep riding food simpler during the overnight hours — familiar, easy to digest, not too sweet
- Caffeine management becomes intertwined with nutrition timing (see Section 9)
- Riders who nap for even 15-20 minutes report improved appetite and GI tolerance for the subsequent hours

## 7. Gastrointestinal Distress

### The Most Common DNF Cause in Ultras

**Prevalence:**
- GI symptoms affect 30-50% of ultra-endurance athletes in events under 12 hours, rising to 60-90% in events over 24 hours (de Oliveira et al., 2014; Pfeiffer et al., 2012)
- In RAAM, GI distress is reported as the primary reason for reduced pace or withdrawal in a majority of entrants
- Upper GI symptoms (nausea, vomiting, reflux, bloating) are more common than lower GI (diarrhea, cramping) in cycling specifically (running causes more lower GI issues due to mechanical jostling)

**Causes — a multi-factorial model:**

1. **Splanchnic hypoperfusion:** During exercise, blood flow is redirected to working muscles. At 70% VO2max, gut blood flow may be reduced by 60-80% (van Wijck et al., 2012). This causes ischemia of the intestinal mucosa.
2. **Duration-dependent mucosal damage:** Prolonged reduction in gut blood flow leads to increased intestinal permeability ("leaky gut"), measured by plasma I-FABP elevation. This worsens with every additional hour of exercise.
3. **Mechanical stress:** Less relevant in cycling than running, but prolonged aero position can compress the abdomen.
4. **Dehydration:** Even mild dehydration (2-3% body mass) significantly worsens GI symptoms by further reducing splanchnic blood flow and slowing gastric emptying (Rehrer et al., 2001).
5. **Heat:** Heat stress independently worsens gut permeability and GI symptoms (Snipe et al., 2018). See Section 8.
6. **High carbohydrate concentration:** Hypertonic solutions in the stomach cause osmotic fluid shifts and bloating. This is why gels without water are a common nausea trigger.
7. **Fat and fiber:** Slow gastric emptying. In shorter events, riders avoid these; in ultras, solid food is necessary, so this becomes a balancing act.
8. **NSAIDs:** Ibuprofen and similar drugs markedly increase intestinal permeability and GI bleeding during exercise (van Wijck et al., 2012). **Avoid NSAIDs during ultra events.**
9. **Sleep deprivation:** Impairs gut motility and increases nausea independent of other factors.

**Prevention strategies:**
- **Gut training:** 2-4 weeks of practicing high carb intake during training rides. Increases transporter expression and tolerance (Jeukendrup, 2017)
- **Pacing:** Keeping intensity below 70-75% VO2max preserves splanchnic blood flow. This is the single most protective factor.
- **Hydration maintenance:** Small, frequent sips rather than large boluses
- **Avoid hypertonic intake:** Always consume gels with water. Use isotonic or hypotonic drinks.
- **Avoid NSAIDs entirely**
- **Reduce intake temporarily if symptoms begin:** It is better to eat less for 30-60 minutes while symptoms resolve than to force intake and trigger vomiting (which creates a much larger caloric deficit than the pause)
- **Prokinetic agents:** Some ultra riders use low-dose ginger (anti-emetic), or in extreme cases, prescription prokinetics (domperidone, metoclopramide). Evidence is limited in exercise contexts.
- **Probiotics:** Some evidence for reduced GI symptoms with multi-strain probiotic supplementation in the weeks before competition (Jager et al., 2019, ISSN position stand), though effect sizes are modest.

## 8. Temperature Effects on Nutrition

### Heat

**Increased caloric expenditure:**
- Heat itself increases metabolic rate modestly (2-5%), but the primary effect is cardiovascular: increased heart rate for the same power output (cardiac drift), meaning the rider is effectively working at a higher relative intensity
- This shifts fuel partitioning toward carbohydrate, increasing carb requirements

**Increased fluid needs:**
- Sweat rates can reach 1.5-2.5 L/hr in extreme heat
- A 24-hour event in 35C+ conditions may require 20-40L of fluid replacement
- Sodium losses scale proportionally

**GI consequences of heat:**
- Heat stress directly increases intestinal permeability (Snipe et al., 2018) — gut damage from heat alone, independent of exercise
- Gastric emptying is slowed in hyperthermia
- GI distress prevalence is significantly higher in hot-weather ultras
- **Combined effect:** Heat + exercise + duration is the worst-case scenario for GI function

**Practical adjustments for heat:**
- Increase sodium to 1,000-1,500 mg/hr
- Increase fluid intake (drink to thirst, but thirst may lag in heat)
- Shift calorie intake toward liquid/semi-liquid forms (easier to consume when core temp is elevated)
- Pre-cooling and cooling strategies (ice, cold fluid) can reduce GI distress indirectly by reducing core temp and sympathetic drive
- Reduce intensity in heat to maintain the same metabolic rate, preserving splanchnic blood flow

### Cold

**Increased caloric expenditure:**
- Shivering thermogenesis can increase metabolic rate by 2-5x resting (Haman et al., 2002)
- Even mild cold stress without shivering increases metabolic rate 10-30% through non-shivering thermogenesis
- Cold overnight sections on mountain brevets can add 100-300 kcal/hr to energy expenditure through thermoregulation
- Substrate utilization during cold shifts toward carbohydrate and intramuscular triglyceride (Haman et al., 2002), increasing carb demand

**Reduced thirst and dehydration risk:**
- Cold blunts thirst perception (Kenefick et al., 2008)
- Riders underdrink in cold, leading to insidious dehydration
- Cold-induced diuresis (increased urine output) compounds this

**Practical adjustments for cold:**
- Increase caloric intake by 10-20% during cold sections
- Actively remind yourself to drink (set timer if necessary)
- Warm food and drinks are extremely valuable: hot soup, warm rice dishes, hot tea/coffee. Both for calories and for core temperature maintenance.
- Carry easily accessible high-calorie foods that don't freeze or become unpalatable in cold (nut butters, cheese, chocolate)

## 9. Caffeine Strategy for Multi-Day Events

### Pharmacology in Ultra Context

**Caffeine basics:**
- Ergogenic dose: 3-6 mg/kg body mass (Goldstein et al., 2010, ISSN position stand)
- For a 75kg rider: 225-450 mg
- Half-life: 3-7 hours (highly individual, affected by genetics — CYP1A2 polymorphism — and habituation)
- Peak plasma concentration: 30-60 min after ingestion

**The ultra caffeine problem:**
- In a 3-hour road race, you take caffeine before/during and it works. Simple.
- In a 60-hour brevet, caffeine must serve dual purposes: performance enhancement and **sleep management**
- Using caffeine continuously depletes its effectiveness through adenosine receptor upregulation (tolerance)

**Multi-day caffeine strategy (synthesized from ultra-cycling practice and sleep research):**

1. **Pre-event withdrawal (controversial):** Some practitioners recommend reducing caffeine intake for 1-4 days before the event to resensitize adenosine receptors. This is physiologically sound but practically miserable and may impair final training/preparation. Withdrawal headaches peak at 24-48 hours.

2. **Early hours (0-12):** Use caffeine at moderate doses (100-200 mg every 3-4 hours) if habituated. Do not exceed normal daily intake. The goal is maintenance, not stimulation.

3. **First night (hours 12-24ish):** This is where strategic caffeine use begins. 100-200 mg as drowsiness onset. Expect reduced effectiveness in the 2-5 AM window regardless of caffeine dose, because circadian pressure is at its peak.

4. **Second day (hours 24-36):** Resume moderate caffeine. The combination of sleep debt and adenosine buildup means caffeine is partially effective but cannot fully compensate.

5. **Second night (hours 36-60):** Caffeine has diminished returns. Many RAAM and PBP riders report that caffeine "stops working" by the second night. Higher doses (300-400 mg) may partially help but increase side effects (tachycardia, anxiety, GI distress, jitteriness).

6. **After sleep breaks:** A short nap (20-90 min) clears some adenosine. Caffeine taken immediately upon waking from a nap is more effective than the same dose after prolonged wakefulness. This is the "nap-a-latte" or "coffee nap" strategy: consume caffeine, then nap for 20 minutes, then wake as caffeine peaks.

**Caffeine and GI interaction:**
- Caffeine stimulates gastric acid secretion and colonic motility
- In sleep-deprived riders with already compromised GI function, high caffeine doses can worsen nausea and cause urgent diarrhea
- Coffee is more GI-stimulating than caffeine pills (due to chlorogenic acids and other compounds)
- For late-event caffeine, pills or caffeinated gels may be better tolerated than coffee

**Specific numbers for modeling:**
- Caffeine clearance: assume 5-hour half-life for an average metabolizer
- After 200mg dose: ~100mg circulating at 5hr, ~50mg at 10hr
- Cumulative 24-hour caffeine intake should probably stay under 800-1,000 mg to avoid significant side effects
- For a 48-hour event: budget approximately 1,200-1,600 mg total caffeine, distributed to weight toward nighttime hours

## 10. Practical Fueling Plans

### 600km Brevet (Approximately 20-27 Hours)

**Assumptions:** 75kg trained rider, average 25 km/hr moving speed, 24 hours total time including stops, moderate climate, 2-3 control stops with food available.

**Pre-event (24-48 hrs before):**
- Carb loading: 8-10 g/kg/day = 600-750g carbs/day
- Sodium loading: add 1,500-2,000 mg Na/day to normal diet
- Extra hydration: aim for pale urine
- Last large meal: 3-4 hours before start. 150-200g carbs, moderate protein, low fat/fiber.

**Phase 1: Hours 0-8 (~200 km)**
- Intake target: 70-90 g/hr carbs (280-360 kcal/hr from carbs)
- Fluid: 500-750 mL/hr
- Sodium: 500-700 mg/hr
- Foods: Mix of gels, bars, bananas, rice cakes, electrolyte drink
- Real food at first control stop (approx. 4-5 hrs in): sandwich, pasta, whatever is available — eat a modest amount, don't gorge

**Phase 2: Hours 8-16 (~200 km)**
- Intake target: 50-70 g/hr carbs + solid food for satiety
- Begin sweet-to-savory transition
- Control stop meal: substantial — rice, pasta, soup, bread with cheese/meat
- On-bike food: wraps, onigiri, boiled potatoes, savory pastries
- Caffeine: begin if nighttime approaches. 100-200 mg.
- Fluid and sodium: maintain rates

**Phase 3: Hours 16-24 (~200 km, overnight for many riders)**
- Intake target: 40-60 g/hr carbs, emphasis on tolerability
- Savory dominance for many riders
- Warm food and drink at any opportunity
- Caffeine: 100-200 mg every 3-4 hours. Coffee-nap if drowsy.
- GI issues most likely in this phase — reduce intake temporarily if nausea occurs rather than forcing food
- Last control stop: eat whatever sounds appealing; appetite may be suppressed

**Total estimated intake: 10,000-14,000 kcal over 24 hours**
**Total estimated expenditure: 14,000-18,000 kcal**
**Expected deficit: 4,000-6,000 kcal (manageable)**

### 1200km Brevet (Approximately 55-90 Hours)

**Assumptions:** Same rider, 75-90 hours total with ~4-8 hours of sleep in 2-3 naps, multiple staffed control stops with full meals.

**The 1200km is fundamentally different from the 600km:** it is a multi-day event with sleep breaks, and nutrition strategy must include recovery meals.

**Pre-event:** Same as 600km.

**Day 1 (Hours 0-16, ~300 km):**
- Standard 600km Phase 1-2 strategy
- Eat well at control stops — these are banked calories
- Do not skip meals to save time. Eating costs 15-20 minutes at a control; not eating costs hours later when you bonk.
- Target: 55-80 g/hr carbs while riding

**Night 1 (Hours 16-24):**
- Eat a full dinner at a control stop before nighttime riding
- Caffeine strategy begins
- On-bike intake reduces to 40-60 g/hr
- Target: arrive at a sleep stop having eaten well

**Sleep Break 1 (typically 1-3 hours):**
- Eat a real meal before sleeping (even if not hungry) — the stomach processes food during sleep
- Set alarm. Upon waking: coffee/caffeine, small easily digestible meal (toast, rice, banana), then ride

**Day 2 (Hours 24-40, ~300 km):**
- Appetite often partially recovers after sleep
- Real meals at controls critical
- On-bike: 40-70 g/hr, mixed sweet and savory
- GI function may be better or worse than night 1 depending on individual response
- Hydration discipline — it is easy to underdrink on day 2

**Night 2 (Hours 40-55):**
- Hardest period of the event
- Caffeine less effective (see Section 9)
- Nausea and appetite suppression peak
- Accept lower intake rates (30-50 g/hr) and focus on tolerability
- Warm soup is often the only thing that sounds appealing
- Second sleep break: even 20-90 minutes transforms the remaining hours

**Day 3 (Hours 55-75+, ~300 km):**
- If a good sleep break was achieved, appetite and GI function improve meaningfully
- Riders often report a "second wind" for eating
- Eat aggressively at controls — the body is in significant deficit
- Return to moderate carb intake (50-70 g/hr on-bike)
- Caffeine useful for final push

**Total estimated intake: 25,000-35,000 kcal over 75 hours**
**Total estimated expenditure: 35,000-50,000 kcal**
**Expected deficit: 10,000-20,000 kcal (equivalent to 1.5-3 kg of fat)**

### What Finishers Actually Eat — Observational Data

**Paris-Brest-Paris 1200km (the most documented brevet):**
- Control stops serve full French meals: pasta, rice, meat, bread, cheese, soup, pastries, fruit, coffee
- Most finishers eat 5-8 full or partial meals at controls plus on-bike food
- Crêpes (sweet and savory) are a PBP staple
- Many riders carry: sandwiches, rice cakes, dried fruit/nuts, gels (fewer than you'd think), salt tablets, caffeine pills
- Japanese PBP participants are known for carrying onigiri, umeboshi (pickled plum for nausea), and instant miso soup packets

**Race Across America (RAAM, ~4,800 km):**
- Support crew prepares food, so options are broader
- Successful finishers consume an estimated 8,000-10,000 kcal/day (Knechtle et al., 2005)
- Common foods: PB&J, burritos, pizza, soup, mashed potatoes, protein shakes, fruit smoothies, rice, noodles
- Multiple RAAM crew chiefs report that riders eat less than half of what is offered, and food preferences change unpredictably

**Transcontinental Race (unsupported, ~4,000 km):**
- Riders rely on gas stations, bakeries, restaurants
- Successful finishers report eating "everything in sight" at restaurants — full meals multiple times per day
- Gas station staples: sandwiches, pastries, chocolate milk, Coca-Cola (widely used in European ultras for its combination of sugar, caffeine, and carbonation that can settle the stomach), chips
- Many riders report that Coca-Cola becomes a craved item by day 2-3, likely due to its precise combination of sugar, carbonation (which can reduce nausea), caffeine, and palatability

## Key Ultra-Specific Principles (vs. Racing)

1. **Fat is your friend.** In racing, you try to sustain the highest carb-fueled intensity possible. In ultras, you pace to maximize fat oxidation, and use carbs strategically for the brain and for intensity surges.

2. **The gut is the limiter, not the engine.** Most DNFs in ultras are GI, not musculoskeletal. Protecting GI function (pacing, hydration, avoiding NSAIDs, gut training) is the highest-return investment.

3. **Palatability matters more than macros.** The theoretical best food is irrelevant if you cannot stand to put it in your mouth at hour 30. Carry variety. Savory options are non-negotiable for events over 12 hours.

4. **Eating is a skill that degrades with fatigue.** Practice eating during long training rides. Practice eating when sleep-deprived. Practice eating foods you would not normally consider "sports nutrition."

5. **Sleep enables eating.** Even a 20-minute nap can restore appetite and GI function more effectively than any pharmaceutical intervention. In multi-day events, the sleep-eat-ride cycle is as important as the ride-eat-ride cycle.

6. **The deficit is inevitable. Manage it, don't fight it.** Trying to match expenditure with intake in a 1200km causes GI disaster. Accept a deficit of 4,000-8,000 kcal per 24 hours, minimize it through steady intake, and recover afterward.
