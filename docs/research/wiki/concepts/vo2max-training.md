## 1. Definition and Measurement

### What VO2max Is

VO2max is the maximum volume of oxygen per minute that an athlete can capture, transport, and utilize during exercise. It is expressed as mL/kg/min (milliliters of oxygen per kilogram of body weight per minute) and represents the ceiling of aerobic energy production. [R]

VO2max is determined by two sides of the same equation:
- **Central factors** (supply): cardiac output, stroke volume, blood volume, hemoglobin mass [R]
- **Peripheral factors** (demand): mitochondrial density, capillary density, muscle fiber oxidative capacity [R]

The strongest predictors of VO2max are central -- cardiac output, blood volume, and hemoglobin (R ~0.8). In one study, phlebotomy erased VO2peak improvements back to baseline despite 40% mitochondrial gains, confirming that central factors dominate. (WD-53) [R]

### Typical VO2max Values

| Population | VO2max (mL/kg/min) | Source |
|-----------|-------------------|--------|
| Average untrained | 35-40 | Santos (TP) [R] |
| Recreational athlete | 40-55 | Santos (TP) [R] |
| Well-trained amateur | 55-65 | Composite [R] |
| Elite cyclist | 65-80 | Santos (TP) [R] |
| World-class (highest recorded) | 80-97 | Santos (TP) [R] |
| Female athletes (general) | 5-15% lower than male peers | Santos (TP) [R] |

### How VO2max Is Measured

- **Lab test (gold standard)**: Incremental ramp test with expired gas analysis (ventilatory test). Only way to get a true mL/kg/min number. [R]
- **Field proxy**: Maximum sustainable effort for 5-8 minutes gives a good indication of pace/power at VO2max (Simone, TP). The power or pace achieved approximates your "VO2max power." [E]
- **Cooper test**: VO2max = (35.97 x miles_in_12min) - 11.29. Originally for military screening; low precision for trained athletes. [R]
- **WKO5 power-duration model**: Extracts VO2max-associated power from the PD curve without a dedicated test. Requires clean data with maximal efforts in the 3-8 minute range within the last 30-90 days. [E]

### Trainability and Genetic Ceiling

- Age and genetics account for at least 50% of VO2max. [R]
- VO2max is considerably less trainable than lactate threshold (FTP). Improvements are often highly related to body weight changes. (Santos, TP) [R]
- Athletes who continue HIIT into older age see VO2max decline at ~0.5%/year vs. 1-1.5%/year for those doing only LSD. (Friel, TP) [R]
- Early in training, cross-stimulation is high -- sprint and threshold work both raise VO2max. This cross-stimulation disappears with training maturity. (WD-61) [R]
- "Genetic plateau" is a diagnosis of exclusion -- rule out recovery, nutrition, sleep, life stress first. (WD-61) [E]

## 2. The "Raises the Roof" Principle

### Key Concept

VO2max sets the ceiling for FTP. FTP cannot rise indefinitely from threshold training alone -- eventually VO2max must increase to make room for further FTP gains. This is the "raises the roof" metaphor. (TMT-60) [E]

### The FTP-VO2max Relationship

- LT/FTP is expressed as a percentage of VO2max. Typical trained cyclists operate at 70-85% of VO2max at threshold. [R]
- Improving FTP means either raising VO2max (higher ceiling) or increasing the fraction of VO2max sustainable at threshold (higher utilization). [R]
- If FTP has plateaued despite consistent threshold training, the signal is to introduce VO2max work. (TMT-60) [E]
- TTE (Time to Exhaustion) stagnation at FTP specifically signals need for VO2max work, not more threshold training. (TMT-60) [E]
- A higher VO2max also increases lactate threshold, plasma volume, muscle strength, lactate tolerance, glycogen storage, and stroke volume. (Simone, TP) [R]

### Decision Tree (TMT-60)

1. **FTP plateau detected** (FTP unchanged after threshold block + rest + retest)
2. **Response**: Introduce VO2max block
3. **Mechanism**: Raise VO2max ceiling so FTP has room to grow
4. **Retest**: After VO2max block + rest, FTP often jumps without additional threshold work

## 3. Interval Protocols

### Overview

There is no single "right" VO2max interval method. Different formats are tools in a toolbox. (WD-55) [E]

"VO2max power" is not a fixed number -- it can be achieved at a range of powers depending on protocol, cadence, and pacing strategy. (WD-55) [R]

### Protocol Comparison Table

| Protocol | Format | Work Duration | Rest | Total VO2max Time | Key Advantage | Source |
|----------|--------|--------------|------|-------------------|---------------|--------|
| Classic long | 4-5 x 4-5 min @ 105-120% FTP | 4-5 min | 3-5 min easy | ~20 min | Maximizes cardiac strain, peak HR | WD-55, TMT-45 [E] |
| Ronnestad 30/15 | 3 sets x 13 reps: 30s @ 94% MAP / 15s @ 77% MAP | 30 sec | 15 sec (3 min between sets) | High | +4.7% 20-min TT power in 3 weeks; more time >90% VO2max | Ronnestad 2020 [R] |
| Billat 30/30 | 30s @ 100% vVO2max / 30s @ 50% vVO2max | 30 sec | 30 sec | Very high | O2 consumption lag keeps VO2 elevated during rest; 2x time at VO2max vs. continuous | Billat 2000 [R] |
| Variable intensity | 6 x 5 min @ 85% MAP w/ 3x30s surges to 100% MAP | 5 min | 2.5 min | 6:50 >90% VO2max | 43% more time >90% VO2max than constant; same RPE | Bossi 2019 [R] |
| Decreasing intervals | 3:00/2:00/1:00/0:45/0:30 (high) with proportional rest, to exhaustion | Decreasing | Proportional | 5:12 >90% VO2max | Best of long + short: quick VO2max attainment + extended time | Vaccari 2020 [R] |
| Micro-burst 40/20 | 3 x 10 min sets of 40s on / 20s off | 40 sec | 20 sec (3 min between sets) | High | Accumulates more time at high absolute power (~6-min best); high muscle activation | Bobo (TP) [E] |
| 80/20 anaerobic | 6-10 x 2.5 min @ VO2max / 5 min easy | 2.5 min | 5 min | Moderate | Longer intervals maximize cardiac strain; 20-30s O2 lag adds bonus time | Fitzgerald (TP) [E] |
| Fast start | 1.5 min @ 100% MAP + 3.5 min @ 85% MAP | 5 min total | 2.5-3 min | High | More time in upper pace/power zones for same RPE | Ronnestad 2020 [R] |

### Specific Numbers and Thresholds

| Parameter | Value | Source |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| VO2max interval total time | ~20 min at intensity; formats 8x3, 5x5, 3x8 all viable | WD-55 [E] |
| Target RPE for VO2max intervals | 9-9.5/10 | TMT-49 [E] |
| Minimum weekly dose | 1 session/week | TMT-45 [E] |
| Typical recovery between VO2max sessions | 36-48 hours | Santos (TP) [R] |
| VO2max block trigger | FTP unchanged after threshold block + rest + retest | TMT-60 [E] |
| Re-test VO2max proxy | Every 4-6 weeks during a VO2max-focused block (5-min peak power) | Simone (TP) [E] |
| Improvement signal | >5% increase in 5-min peak power or gradual RPE/HR decrease at same power | Simone (TP) [E] |
| Rest week frequency during VO2max blocks | Every 2-3 weeks | TMT-55 [E] |
| Rest week minimum after VO2max block | 7+ days | TMT-55 [E] |
| Cadence for VO2max intervals | High cadence (100-120 RPM) causes less muscular fatigue | WD-55 [R+E] |

### Protocol Selection Guidance

| Athlete Profile | Recommended Protocol(s) | Rationale |
|----------------|------------------------|-----------|
| New to VO2max work | Classic 4-5 x 4-5 min | Simple, well-studied, teaches pacing at intensity |
| Plateau from classic intervals | Ronnestad 30/15 or micro-bursts | Novel stimulus; accumulates more time at high power |
| Time-crunched (<8 hr/week) | 40/20 micro-bursts or 30/15 | High stimulus density in short sessions |
| Ultra-endurance base, first VO2max block | Classic long intervals | Massive volume base amplifies VO2max response (AMA) [E] |
| Masters (50+) | Start conservative: 5x30s, build to 5x1min, then 5x2min over months | Low dose first; injury prevention paramount (Friel, TP) [E] |
| Cyclocross / crit racer | Variable intensity or decreasing intervals | Mimics race-specific surging pattern |

## 4. Progression Models

### Building Into VO2max Work

The Short-to-Long (STL) approach is effective for transitioning into high-intensity work after base. (Bobo, TP) [E]

1. **Accumulation phase**: Build aerobic engine with tempo, sweet spot, endurance volume
2. **Transmutation phase**: Introduce VO2max intervals starting with longer intervals (~8 min) to bank time in higher zones
3. **Focused VO2max block**: Concentrate VO2max sessions (2-3 weeks), followed by restoration

### Within-Block Progression

| Week | Example Progression | Notes |
|------|-------------------|-------|
| 1 | 4 x 3 min @ 110% FTP / 3 min rest | Conservative start; assess response |
| 2 | 5 x 3 min @ 110% FTP / 3 min rest | Add 1 rep |
| 3 | 4 x 4 min @ 108% FTP / 3 min rest | Extend duration |
| 4 | Rest week (7+ days easy) | Critical for adaptation |
| 5 | 5 x 4 min @ 110% FTP / 3 min rest | Combine volume + intensity |
| 6 | 4 x 5 min @ 108% FTP / 4 min rest | Near-maximal protocol |
| 7 | Rest + retest | Assess 5-min peak power |

**Progressive overload before protocol change**: Exhaust simple duration/rep progression before switching to a different VO2max format. Most athletes change protocols too often and never fully capture adaptation from one approach. (TMT-52) [E]

### Sporadic Dosing

VO2max work does not require a dedicated block. Sporadic dosing can work: one coached athlete did 3 VO2max sessions in month 1, 6 in month 2 alongside other training, resulting in massive FTP gains. (TMT-60) [E]

## 5. When to Do VO2max Blocks

### Timing Within the Season

| Season Phase | VO2max Prescription | Rationale |
|-------------|-------------------|-----------|
| Off-season / early base | Not primary; 0-1 sessions/week optional | Build aerobic base + volume first |
| Late base / pre-build | Introduce 1/week | Begin raising ceiling before race season |
| Build phase | 1-2/week (focused block of 2-3 weeks) | Primary adaptation window |
| Race season | 1/week maintenance or sporadic dosing | Maintain ceiling; race provides stimulus |
| FTP plateau (any phase) | Introduce VO2max block immediately | FTP decision tree trigger (TMT-60) |

### The Volume-Before-Intensity Principle

More riding volume before VO2max blocks produces better responses. Ultra-endurance athletes who never do VO2max work see massive gains from a single block after building a big volume base. (AMA, TMT-43) [E]

Well-trained athletes see less adaptation per unit of high-intensity training vs. untrained; endurance volume provides adaptations that intensity alone cannot replicate. (AMA) [R]

### Anti-Panic Protocol (TMT-71)

If behind schedule:
- **8+ weeks to event**: Add volume first for 3-4 weeks, THEN start intensity progression
- **<4 weeks to event**: Pivot goals (finish, enjoy, treat as training race)
- **NEVER**: Double VO2max intensity immediately to "catch up"

## 6. Ceiling Effects and Individual Variation

### Diminishing Returns

- VO2max improvements follow a logarithmic growth curve. (WD-61) [R]
- ~25% improvement over baseline is a common long-term ceiling. (WD-61) [E]
- Early growth curve stimuli are non-specific (sprint, threshold, VO2max work all raise VO2max early). Cross-stimulation disappears with maturity. (WD-61) [R]
- Faster protocols may get to plateau sooner, not a higher plateau. (WD-61) [O]
- FTP work eventually stops raising VO2max -- model plateau detection. (WD-52) [R]
- HIF-1alpha suppression in elites: PHD2 2.6x higher, FIH 3.5x higher, Sirtuin6 5x higher. Capillary density hits a ceiling. (WD-52) [R]

### Individual Variation

- IPD meta-analysis (~350 athletes, 13 studies): individual variation (-20% to +30%) dwarfs any training distribution effect (~1-2%). (Persp-38) [R]
- Some athletes respond strongly to VO2max training; others see minimal VO2max change but still improve performance through economy, neuromuscular efficiency, weight change. (Baddick, TP) [R]
- VO2max is only half the equation -- the other half is efficiency. Two athletes with the same VO2max can perform very differently. (Baddick, TP) [R]
- Even when VO2max does not change, performance improvements still occur through improved running/cycling economy, muscle recruitment, and experience. (Baddick, TP) [R]

### The 30/15 Reanalysis (WD-55) -- Critical

The widely cited Ronnestad 30/15 study requires careful interpretation:
- The study abstract states "no group difference in change of VO2max" (p = 0.49). [R]
- The 4.7% improvement in 20-min TT power is most parsimoniously explained by W' (anaerobic capacity) improvement, not VO2max. [R]
- CX racing (massive intermittent effort dose, similar to 30/15) never improves VO2max in trained athletes. [E]
- **Conclusion**: 30/15 intervals are a good training tool but should not be marketed as uniquely superior for VO2max improvement. [E]

## 7. Recovery Considerations

### VO2max Block Recovery

| Parameter | Value | Source |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| Rest week frequency | Every 2-3 weeks during VO2max blocks | TMT-55 [E] |
| Minimum rest week duration | 7+ days after VO2max-focused block | TMT-55 [E] |
| Rest week ride IF | < 0.50 | TMT-55 [E] |
| Between-session recovery | 36-48 hours (Santos, TP) | [R] |
| Indoor training multiplier | 1.1-1.2x outdoor TSS equivalent | TMT-51 [E] |

### Warning Signs During VO2max Blocks

- HRV sustained depression >7 days [E]
- Performance regression despite maintained load [E]
- Persistent low motivation (most reliable single indicator) [E]
- Illness frequency >1x per 6-8 weeks [E]
- Athlete requests rest -- **ALWAYS grant it** (TMT-55) [E]
- Failed VO2max session + failed retest 2-3 days later = immediate rest week [E]

### Masters Athletes (50+)

- VO2max declines inevitably with age; HIIT slows this to ~0.5%/year vs 1-1.5%/year without. (Friel, TP) [R]
- VO2max is the best predictor of endurance performance in older athletes (LT and economy decline less). (Friel, TP) [R]
- Dose: Start very conservative (5 x 30s, 1x/week or 1x/9 days). Build gradually over months. (Friel, TP) [E]
- Density: Space VO2max sessions further apart than younger athletes (every 7-9 days vs every 3-4 days). [E]
- Injury risk is higher; patience is essential. (Friel, TP) [E]

## 8. Common Mistakes

1. **"30/15s are the best VO2max training"** -- No VO2max superiority demonstrated; gains likely reflect W' improvement. (WD-55) [R]
2. **Neglecting VO2max entirely** -- FTP-only training hits a ceiling. VO2max work is essential even in a minimal plan. (TMT-45, TMT-60) [E]
3. **Skipping volume base before VO2max block** -- Volume base amplifies VO2max response; ultra-endurance athletes who build massive base see outsized gains. (AMA) [E]
4. **Continuing threshold when FTP is plateaued** -- This only extends TTE, not FTP. Introduce VO2max instead. (TMT-60) [E]
5. **Not resting enough during VO2max blocks** -- Rest week every 2-3 weeks; 7+ days after a VO2max block. (TMT-55) [E]
6. **Using heart rate to pace short VO2max intervals** -- HR is a lagging indicator; takes 1-3 minutes to register effort. Use power/pace and RPE instead. (Simone, TP) [E]
7. **Changing protocols too often** -- Exhaust rep/duration progression before switching formats. (TMT-52) [E]
8. **Stacking 1-3% improvement claims from multiple studies** -- Arrow-gains fallacy. Cannot compound gains from separate interventions. (WD-61) [E]
9. **Panic training after time off** -- Doubling VO2max intensity to "catch up" leads to fatigue, not fitness. (TMT-71) [E]
10. **Low-carb during VO2max blocks** -- High-intensity work requires glycogen; under-fueling impairs both performance and adaptation. (WD-54, Persp-41) [R]

## 11. Conflicts and Nuances

| Conventional Claim | Evidence-Based Position | Evidence Level |
|-------------------|----------------------|----------------|
| "30/15s are the best VO2max protocol" | No VO2max superiority; gains are likely W' (anaerobic capacity) | WD-55 [R] |
| "You must train at VO2max to improve VO2max" | Early in training, many intensities raise VO2max; cross-stimulation fades with maturity | WD-61 [R] |
| "More mitochondria = better performance" | 40% mitochondrial increase did NOT correlate with VO2peak improvement | WD-53 [R] |
| "VO2max is not trainable" | It is trainable, but less so than LT; genetics set ~50% of ceiling; training fills the remaining potential | Baddick (TP) [R] |
| "Higher VO2max always means faster" | VO2max is only half the equation; economy/efficiency is the other half | Baddick (TP) [R] |
| "Zone 2 doesn't affect VO2max" | Volume base amplifies subsequent VO2max block response; PGC-1alpha activated at all intensities | AMA [E], Bobo (TP) [R] |
| "Short intervals are better than long" | Both have distinct advantages; long maximize cardiac strain, short maximize time at VO2max | Fitzgerald (TP) [R] |
| "VO2max work is only for young/elite athletes" | Masters athletes benefit most from maintaining VO2max; it's the metric that declines fastest with age | Friel (TP) [R] |
| "Study X shows 15% VO2max gains" | Cannot stack gains from multiple studies; early growth curve results may not transfer to trained athletes | WD-61 [E] |
