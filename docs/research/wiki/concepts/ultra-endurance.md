# Ultra-Endurance Cycling

Training, racing, and analysis for cycling events lasting 5-24+ hours and covering 200km+. This is a **high-priority** topic -- the platform user is an ultra-endurance cyclist.

Evidence levels: **[R]** = Research-backed, **[E]** = Experience-based, **[O]** = Opinion.

---

## 1. Defining Ultra-Endurance Cycling

### Event Categories

| Category | Duration | Distance | Examples |
|----------|----------|----------|----------|
| Century | 5-8 hours | 160 km / 100 mi | Gran Fondos, charity centuries |
| Ultra-Century | 8-12 hours | 200-300 km | Double centuries, Everesting attempts |
| Ultra-Distance | 12-24 hours | 300-500 km | Leadville 100 MTB, single-day brevets |
| Multi-Day | 2-21+ days | 500-4,500+ km | Tour Divide, Race Across America, Paris-Brest-Paris |
| Everesting | 15-20+ hours | ~270 km avg | 8,848m climbing on a single hill |

### Key Characteristics vs Road Racing

| Parameter | Road Racing | Ultra-Endurance |
|-----------|------------|-----------------|
| Intensity (IF) | 0.65-0.87 | 0.40-0.60 |
| Primary energy system | Aerobic + anaerobic bursts | Almost entirely aerobic |
| Pacing strategy | Tactical (save energy, burst) | Steady-state (conserve, execute plan) |
| Nutrition criticality | Important | Existential -- failure = DNF |
| Sleep management | N/A | Critical for multi-day |
| Equipment resilience | Race-optimized | Durability-optimized |
| Mental game | Competitive tactics | Sustained motivation, suffering management |

## 2. Physiological Demands

### What the PD Curve Tells Us

- **Ultra events are raced almost entirely in Zones 1-2** -- IF typically 0.40-0.55 for multi-day, 0.50-0.65 for single-day ultras [E]
- **FTP matters less than TTE and durability** -- the ability to sustain a given percentage of FTP for extended hours is the key differentiator [E]
- **TTer phenotype is ideal for ultra-distance** -- slow-twitch dominant, high TTE; the Everesting case study showed 82% slow-twitch fiber as ideal (WKO4 Everest Challenge) [E]
- **Total kJ expenditure is enormous** -- Everesting: ~9,300 kcal (39,300 kJ); Tour Divide: ~5,100 kJ/day for 19.5 days (Alex Howes) [E]

### Tour de France vs Tour Divide (Alex Howes -- First Person to Complete Both)

| Metric | Tour de France | Tour Divide |
|--------|---------------|-------------|
| Days | 23 (2 rest) | 19.5 (1 rest) |
| Total TSS | 5,474 | 5,202 |
| TSS/day (racing days) | 266 | 266 |
| Total ride time | 104 hours | 244 hours |
| Daily ride time | 4.5 hours | 12.5 hours |
| Total kJ | 75,069 | 99,452 |
| Daily kJ | 3,263 | 5,100 |
| Average IF | 0.70-0.87 | 0.40-0.54 |
| Average speed | 36.35 kph | 18.1 kph |

> **Key insight:** TSS/day converged to the exact same number (266) despite radically different intensity profiles. This suggests a human daily stress ceiling -- "after a certain amount of load, whether intensity or duration, the body says 'we're done'" (Howes) [O]

### Durability as the Ultra-Endurance Differentiator

- **kJ/kg is the standard metric for durability** -- use bins of 10 kJ/kg (WD-60) [R]
- **Total time riding is the best predictor of durability** -- not specific "durability workouts" (WD-60) [E]
- **Fueling is a dangling confounder** -- cannot separate nutrition effects from true durability in field data (WD-60) [E]
- **Elite pro power drop at 50 kJ/kg: <2%**; average amateur: 20-40% (WD-60) [E]

### WKO5 Fatigue Resistance Visualization

- WKO5 plots PD curve after a specific kJ expenditure (typically 1,500 kJ benchmark)
- **Poor fatigue resistance**: 35% loss of 1-min power after 1,500 kJ
- **Strong fatigue resistance**: 9% loss of 1-min power after 1,500 kJ
- Without WKO5: perform a 1-min all-out effort after 2.5 hours (~1,500 kJ at 170W avg) and compare to all-time best
  - <5% drop = very good; 5-10% = good; 10-15% = average; <1% = elite/pro

## 3. Pacing for Ultra Events

### Fundamental Principle

- **Setting off too hard will be paid for dearly hours later** -- the Everesting case study showed only a 4W power trend drop over 5+ hours when pacing was correct [E]
- **Pace for the second half, not the first** -- the ride should feel easy for the first quarter [E]
- **Use IF targets rather than absolute watts** -- 0.50-0.65 for single-day, 0.40-0.55 for multi-day [E]

### Pacing Strategy by Event Type

| Event Duration | Target IF | Pacing Notes |
|---------------|-----------|--------------|
| 5-8 hours (Century) | 0.55-0.65 | Steady effort; eat early and often |
| 8-12 hours (Double Century) | 0.50-0.60 | Conservative start; negative split if possible |
| 12-20 hours (Everesting/ultra) | 0.45-0.55 | Must feel easy for first 3-4 hours |
| Multi-day | 0.40-0.55 max | Daily TSS ceiling ~266; recovery between days is paramount |

### Progressive Build Strategy (from Everesting Case Study)

1. **Quarter-distance attempt** (~2,500m climbing) -- learn pacing, test nutrition, measure fatigue
2. **Half-distance attempt** (~4,500m climbing) -- refine pacing, test equipment, validate recovery
3. **Full attempt** -- execute with known pacing parameters

Key learning: Go into ultra-events with **high TSB (form) and slightly lower CTL (fitness)**, rather than the other way around. The quarter-distance attempt at ~300 TSS showed that preparation itself generates heavy fatigue.

### BestBikeSplit for Ultra Pacing

- Model predicts optimal power targets segment-by-segment based on elevation, conditions, equipment
- **Tour de France TT predictions within 3-11 seconds** of actual times [E]
- Particularly valuable for ultra events where terrain varies significantly over many hours

## 4. Nutrition for Ultra-Distance

### The Existential Challenge

- **Fueling failure is the #1 cause of DNF in ultra-distance cycling** [E]
- **Energy demand during Everesting: over 3x normal daily intake** -- body more interested in propelling than digesting (Everest Challenge case study) [E]
- **60-90g/hr carbs is the modern standard** -- up to 120g/hr for elite with trained guts (Persp-41, TMT-73) [R]
- **Start eating at km 0** -- not when hungry; by then it is too late (TMT-73) [E]
- **On-bike fueling does NOT spare muscle glycogen, only liver glycogen** (Persp-41) [R]

### Ultra-Specific Nutrition Strategy

| Parameter | Guideline | Source |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| Carbs/hour (trained gut) | 60-90 g/hr standard; 90-120 g/hr elite | Persp-41 [R] |
| Start eating | Immediately (km 0) | TMT-73 [E] |
| Glucose:fructose ratio | 2:1 for >60g/hr absorption | [R] |
| Protein during multi-day | Small amounts every few hours to slow muscle catabolism | [E] |
| Solid vs liquid food | Shift from solid to liquid as intensity rises or gut distresses | [E] |
| Post-stage recovery | 1.2 g/kg/hr carbs for 4-5 hours | Persp-41 [R] |
| Fat adaptation as strategy | NOT recommended for performance by EC; molecular dead end (WD-54) | [R] |

### Gut Training

- **Absorb capacity is trainable** -- range is 50-150 g/hr across individuals (Persp-41) [R]
- **Practice race-day nutrition in training** -- start at 40-50 g/hr and progressively increase over weeks
- **GI distress is the main limiter** -- not absorption capacity; heat and dehydration compound GI issues

> **Conflict:** The Uphill Athlete / Ballinger Everest story advocates fat adaptation with ketogenic approach. EC explicitly dismisses train-low for performance: "I checked my list... there is nothing here" (WD-60). Fat adaptation may have niche value for multi-day mountain events where consistent high-carb intake is logistically impossible, but performance evidence for cycling remains negative [R].

## 5. Sleep Management (Multi-Day Events)

### Sleep Deprivation Effects

- **Cognitive function degrades before physical function** -- decision-making and navigation suffer first [R]
- **Hallucinations common in ultra-racing** -- reported frequently in 24+ hour events [E]
- **Sleep banking (pre-loading) has limited but measurable benefit** -- extra sleep in the week before an event may provide a small buffer (TrainingPeaks: Should You Bank Sleep for Ultramarathon) [R]
- **Creatine may have minor cognitive benefit when sleep-deprived** -- 1-2 sec/question improvement at 4 AM; but zero aerobic performance effect (WD-58) [R]

### Practical Sleep Strategy

| Event Type | Sleep Approach |
|------------|---------------|
| Single-day (12-20 hours) | No sleep; manage fatigue with caffeine and planned rest stops |
| 24-hour events | 0-1 short naps (20 min max); caffeine timing critical |
| Multi-day (Tour Divide style) | 3-5 hours sleep per night minimum; declining below this accelerates cognitive collapse |
| Supported multi-day | Aim for 5-7 hours sleep; recovery meals timed to maximize sleep quality |

### Caffeine Protocol for Ultra

| Timing | Dose | Purpose |
|--------|------|---------|
| Pre-event | 3-6 mg/kg body weight, 60 min before | Performance enhancement |
| During event | 50-100 mg every 2-3 hours after halfway | Maintain alertness |
| Night hours | 100-200 mg strategically | Combat drowsiness during most dangerous periods |
| Withdrawal awareness | Reduce intake in week before if habitually high | Greater effect when less tolerant |

## 6. Equipment for Ultra-Distance

### Key Differences from Standard Road Setup

| Component | Standard Road | Ultra-Distance |
|-----------|--------------|----------------|
| Tire width | 25-28mm | 28-35mm (comfort over speed) |
| Tire pressure | Higher | Lower (compliance, reduced fatigue) |
| Saddle | Race-fit | Comfort-optimized; test over 4+ hour rides |
| Handlebar position | Aggressive | More upright; multiple hand positions; aero bars for time efficiency |
| Lighting | None | Essential for night riding; front + rear + backup |
| Storage | Minimal | Frame bags, saddle bag, handlebar roll; self-supported nutrition/tools |
| Power meter | Nice to have | Critical for pacing over 8+ hours |
| Bike fit | Performance | Endurance-optimized; slightly more upright, wider bars |

### Equipment Testing Protocol

- All equipment must be tested in training at race-duration intensity
- **Saddle sores and hand numbness** are primary equipment-related DNF causes in ultra events
- **Tune-up race 4 weeks out** -- test nutrition, equipment, pacing on a shorter off-road endurance race [E]

## 7. Training Volume & Structure for Ultra

### Volume Requirements

| Athlete Level | Weekly Volume (hours) | Long Ride (hours) | Source |
|--------------|----------------------|-------------------|--------|
| Minimum for century | 8-10 | 4-5 | [E] |
| Competitive ultra | 12-18 | 5-7 | [E] |
| Elite ultra/multi-day | 15-25+ | 6-10 | [E] |

### 28-Week Preparation Framework (100-Mile MTB Model)

| Phase | Duration | Focus | Key Workouts |
|-------|----------|-------|-------------|
| Base | 12 weeks | Aerobic foundation, strength integration | Sweet spot intervals (30-45 min at 90-95% FTP); aerobic threshold rides |
| Build | 8-10 weeks | Threshold, anaerobic capacity | Threshold ladders; over-under surges; 1 strength day/week |
| Specialty | 8-10 weeks | Race specifics, course simulation | FTP intervals; long MTB rides with tempo finish; equipment testing |
| Taper | 1-2 weeks | CTL drop <10%; TSB target +15 to +25 | Reduced volume, maintained intensity |

### The Simplest Effective Plan (EC Framework Applied to Ultra)

- **2 hard days + fill remaining time with endurance** (TMT-45) [E]
- For ultra focus: 1 threshold/sweet spot day + 1 long endurance day + endurance fill
- **Progressive overload before protocol change** -- exhaust duration progression before switching protocols (TMT-44, 45) [E]
- **Long rides are the irreplaceable session** -- there is no substitute for time in the saddle for ultra preparation [E]
- **Long ride frequency (TMT-75):** At least one 6+ hour ride per month is "almost non-negotiable" for athletes targeting 15+ hour events; shorter structured work helps but cannot replace this specificity [E]
- **Diminishing returns on extreme duration (TMT-75):** A 6-7 hour ride captures most of the adaptation; 10+ hours adds disproportionate recovery cost relative to new adaptation. Exception: occasional extreme-duration rides reveal edge-case weak points (e.g., knee pain at hour 25, hand numbness at hour 8) that shorter rides cannot surface [E]
- **Training camp autoregulation (TMT-75):** For training camps (e.g., a week in Mallorca), rigid plans are counterproductive; provide guardrails (intensity cap, duration guidance, altitude HR ceiling) but let the athlete ride on vibes; treat recovery planning like post-cyclocross season [E]

## 8. Mental Strategies for Ultra-Distance

### Psychology of Ultra-Endurance Athletes

From research by Tatjana Bill (Ph.D. dissertation, published PLOS ONE 2023):

- **Five pivotal themes**: Making of the UE athlete, finding a tribe, peak experiences, UE lifestyle, passion and outcomes [R]
- **Harmonious vs obsessive passion** -- harmonious passion brings adaptive outcomes (healthy lifestyle, balanced training); obsessive passion leads to potential injuries, burnout, lower happiness [R]
- **20% of ultramarathoners screen positive for exercise addiction** vs 0.5% general population (Buck et al., 2018) [R]
- **Self-determination needs**: competence, autonomy, relatedness -- when all three are fulfilled by a single activity, it becomes a high-value need [R]

### Practical Mental Frameworks

| Framework | Application to Ultra | Source |
|-----------|---------------------|--------|
| ACT (Acceptance & Commitment) | "Is this thought workable right now?" -- pain at hour 15 is expected, not actionable | Persp-37 [R] |
| Process goals | Focus on executing segments, not final outcome | TMT-73 [E] |
| Segmentation | Break the ride into manageable chunks (e.g., lap-by-lap for Everesting) | [E] |
| Visualization | Practice in 1st person for competition period; visualize specific challenging moments | [E] |
| Tribe support | Community and crew are essential for psychological sustainability | Bill 2023 [R] |
| Identity check | If sport is the ONLY source of identity, risk of obsessive passion increases | Bill 2023 [R] |

### Dealing with Low Points

- **Low points are universal and temporary** -- they pass if you keep moving [E]
- **Motivation is a physiological signal, not a character flaw** (TMT-48) [E]
- **"Drop the rope"** -- stop fighting unhelpful thoughts; let them pass (Persp-37) [R]
- **Pivot goals** -- if behind schedule, shift from "race" to "finish/experience" (TMT-56) [E]
- **"Periodize fun"** -- mental health rides have tangible training value (TMT-54) [E]

## 9. Common Mistakes in Ultra-Endurance

1. **Starting too fast** -- the #1 ultra error; feels easy in first hour, catastrophic by hour 6 [E]
2. **Insufficient nutrition** -- old 25g/hr paradigm was destructive; modern standard is 60-90g/hr (TMT-73) [E]
3. **No progressive distance buildup** -- jumping from 4-hour rides to 15-hour events; use quarter/half distance progressions [E]
4. **Ignoring sleep debt pre-event** -- arrive rested; the event will create enough fatigue [E]
5. **Weight loss during build phase** -- creates energy deficit that undermines adaptation (TMT-48) [E]
6. **Mono-focusing on FTP** -- ultra performance depends on durability, TTE, nutrition execution, and pacing more than raw FTP (TMT-70) [E]
7. **Treating CTL as fitness** -- specifically objected to by EC; high CTL can mask fatigue that destroys ultra performance (TMT-68) [E]
8. **Obsessive passion unchecked** -- recognize when training crosses from harmonious to compulsive; ask "is my life in harmony?" (Bill 2023) [R]
9. **Not testing equipment at race duration** -- saddle, nutrition system, lighting must be validated over full-duration simulations [E]
