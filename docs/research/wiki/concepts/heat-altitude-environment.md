# Heat, Altitude & Environmental Training

Protocols and strategies for training and racing in extreme heat, altitude, cold, and humidity.

Evidence levels: **[R]** = Research-backed, **[E]** = Experience-based, **[O]** = Opinion.

---

## 1. Environmental Effects on Performance

### Overview

- **Environment is an explicit confounder in all field performance data** -- heat, cold, altitude, wind all affect measurements (WD-60) [E]
- **Heat compounds cramping risk independently of hydration** (TMT-59) [R]
- **Altitude causes ~1% power reduction per 1,000 ft of elevation** -- a rider at 10,000 ft operates at approximately 90% of sea-level capacity (Sauser Leadville analysis) [R]
- **Core temperature is the master limiter in hot conditions** -- once elevated, it cannot be quickly reversed regardless of hydration (Hal Higdon) [E]
- **Acclimatization is the single most effective countermeasure** for all environmental stressors [R]

### Performance Impact Summary

| Environmental Factor | Power Impact | Duration Impact | Primary Mechanism |
|---------------------|-------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Heat (>30C / 86F) | -5% to -15% FTP | Increased RPE, earlier fatigue | Core temp rise, cardiac drift, impaired heat dissipation |
| Altitude (>1,500m) | -1% per 300m above 1,500m | Minimal at easy pace, severe at threshold+ | Reduced O2 saturation, lower VO2max |
| Cold (<5C / 41F) | Minimal if dressed properly | Extended warmup needed | Vasoconstriction, reduced muscle compliance |
| Humidity (>70%) | Compounds heat effect ~2x | Sweat evaporation impaired | Heat dissipation failure |
| Wind (headwind) | Significant at >15mph | CdA becomes dominant factor | Aerodynamic drag increase |

## 2. Heat Acclimatization

### Physiology of Heat Adaptation

- **Plasma volume expansion** -- primary early adaptation (3-5 days); blood volume increases, lowering HR at same power [R]
- **Sweat onset earlier and rate increases** -- skin cooling becomes more efficient [R]
- **Core temperature regulation improves** -- lower resting and exercising core temps [R]
- **Perceived exertion decreases** at same absolute intensity [R]
- **Full heat acclimatization requires 10-14 days** of repeated heat exposure [R]
- **Partial acclimatization (meaningful performance benefit) begins at 5-7 days** [R]

### Heat Acclimatization Protocols

| Protocol | Duration | Method | Evidence |
|----------|----------|--------|----------|
| Natural outdoor training | 10-14 days | Train in target heat conditions, 60-90 min/day | Gold standard [R] |
| Sauna post-exercise | 10-14 sessions | 20-30 min sauna immediately after training | Effective plasma volume expansion [R] |
| Hot bath immersion | 6-10 sessions | 30-40 min in 40C water post-exercise | Similar to sauna [R] |
| Overdressing (layers) | Variable | Extra clothing during training to elevate core temp | Anecdotal; used by Olympians (Higdon) [E] |
| Travel to similar climate | 3+ weeks pre-race | Kansas for Kona (similar heat/humidity/wind) -- used by Matt Hanson | Practical approach [E] |
| Indoor trainer heat | As needed | Train indoors without fan, add layers | Accessible but harder to control dose [E] |

### Race-Day Heat Strategy

1. **Recognize temperature spikes early** -- a too-fast start in heat will elevate core temp irreversibly during the race (Higdon) [E]
2. **Slow down proactively** -- planned pace based on prior race times will not work in heat [E]
3. **Adjust drink ratios** -- shift to higher water vs sports drink percentage in heat (e.g., 75% water / 25% sports drink above 70F) to avoid GI distress [E]
4. **Pre-cooling** -- ice vests, cold towels, slushie drinks before start can buy 10-15 min of lower core temp [R]
5. **Continuous cooling** -- ice in jersey, water over head at aid stations [E]
6. **Adjust power targets downward** -- 5-10% FTP reduction is typical in hot conditions [E]

### Heat-Related Risks

| Condition | Warning Signs | Action |
|-----------|--------------|--------|
| Heat exhaustion | Heavy sweating, weakness, cold/pale/clammy skin, nausea | Stop, cool, hydrate, seek shade |
| Heat stroke | High body temp (>104F), hot/red/dry skin, confusion | MEDICAL EMERGENCY -- cool immediately, call emergency services |
| Hyponatremia | Nausea, headache, confusion, seizures | Stop drinking plain water; seek medical care |
| Exercise-associated cramping | Muscle spasms, typically late in event | Neural fatigue theory > electrolyte theory (TMT-59) [R] |

## 3. Altitude Training

### Oxygen Saturation Response

- **Oxyhemoglobin saturation begins dropping immediately upon arrival at altitude** [R]
- **Saturation bottoms out at approximately 72 hours** post-arrival [R]
- **Full acclimatization (red blood cell adaptation) takes 2-3 weeks** [R]
- **Body builds more oxygen-carrying red corpuscles** as adaptation, but this is gradual [R]

### Live High / Train Low (LHTL) Protocol

| Parameter | Value | Evidence |
|-----------|-------|----------|
| Sleeping altitude | 2,000-2,500m (6,500-8,200 ft) | Optimal EPO stimulation range [R] |
| Training altitude | Below 1,500m (5,000 ft) | Allows full training intensity [R] |
| Minimum duration | 3-4 weeks | Less than 3 weeks shows minimal benefit [R] |
| Altitude tents | Simulated altitude during sleep | Convenient but compliance issues [E] |
| Return-to-sea-level window | Race within 1-3 days or after 2-3 weeks | Avoid 3-10 day "valley" of deacclimatization [R] |

### Practical Altitude Strategy (from Higdon)

Three options for competing at altitude:
1. **Arrive 3+ weeks early** -- allows full acclimatization
2. **Arrive at the last possible minute** -- before blood O2 saturation crashes (race within 24 hours of arrival)
3. **Slow down significantly** -- accept reduced performance; the slower you go, the less altitude affects you

### Altitude Impact on Cycling

- **Christoph Sauser at Leadville 100**: average elevation 10,169 ft, highest recorded 12,360 ft; racing at approximately 90% of full capacity [E]
- **WKO elevation-corrected power** available to adjust PD curves for altitude effects [E]
- **VO2max decreases approximately 6-7% per 1,000m above 1,500m** in unacclimatized athletes [R]

## 4. Cold Weather Training

### Physiological Effects

- **Vasoconstriction reduces blood flow to extremities** -- hands and feet go cold first [R]
- **Muscle compliance decreases** -- higher injury risk without adequate warmup [R]
- **Respiratory heat loss increases** -- cold air can trigger bronchospasm in susceptible athletes [R]
- **Appetite increases in cold** -- higher caloric expenditure from thermogenesis (TrainingPeaks: Understanding Your Appetite During Cold Weather Training) [R]
- **Reynaud's-like symptoms in winter athletes** -- blood shunting to core, especially in glycolytically-stressed athletes (Uphill Athlete / Scott Johnston observation) [E]

### Cold Training Strategies

| Strategy | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| Layering system | Base layer (wicking) + mid layer (insulating) + outer layer (wind/waterproof) |
| Extremity protection | Toe warmers, lobster gloves, neoprene shoe covers; cut toe warmers to access shoe straps |
| Extended warmup | 15-20 min progressive warmup before any intensity |
| Indoor backup | Trainer sessions for extreme cold days; maintain key interval quality indoors |
| Equipment testing | Test cold-weather gear in training, not on race day; check grip on wet handlebars |
| Hydration awareness | Thirst perception decreases in cold; maintain hydration schedule regardless |

### Cold-Weather Racing Preparation

- **Pack for worst case** -- toe warmers, gloves, winter hat, arm warmers, rain jacket for every travel race pack [E]
- **Test all equipment in training** -- especially modified items (cut toe warmers, alternate handlebar tape) [E]
- **Mental advantage** -- athletes who train in and prepare for bad conditions gain a significant psychological edge (Nick Radkewich) [E]

## 5. Humidity Effects

### Impact Mechanism

- **High humidity impairs sweat evaporation** -- the primary cooling mechanism [R]
- **Wet Bulb Globe Temperature (WBGT) is the best composite metric** combining temperature, humidity, wind, and radiation [R]
- **>70% relative humidity at >25C creates dangerous conditions** even at moderate intensities [R]

### WBGT Risk Categories

| WBGT (C) | Risk Level | Recommendation |
|----------|------------|----------------|
| <18 | Low | Normal training |
| 18-23 | Moderate | Increase hydration, monitor RPE |
| 23-28 | High | Reduce intensity 5-10%, shorten duration |
| 28-30 | Very High | Reduce intensity significantly, consider indoor alternatives |
| >30 | Extreme | Cancel or move indoors |

### Practical Adjustments

- **Heart rate decoupling increases faster in humid conditions** -- monitor HR:power ratio as early warning [E]
- **GI distress more common** -- higher fluid needs but gut absorption is challenged [E]
- **Indoor trainer without fan simulates humid conditions** -- useful for acclimatization but uncomfortable [E]

## 6. Integrated Race-Day Environmental Strategy

### Pre-Race Checklist

1. **Check weather forecast 7 days out and 24 hours out** -- adjust pacing plan for temperature, wind, humidity
2. **Arrive early enough for partial acclimatization** if environmental conditions differ significantly from training environment
3. **Pack equipment for all conditions** -- the "Nick Radkewich rule": toe warmers, gloves, hat, arm warmers, rain jacket, wetsuit (if applicable) always in race bag
4. **Test nutrition plan in target conditions** -- heat changes GI tolerance, cold reduces appetite
5. **Adjust power targets** -- use WKO5 / BestBikeSplit modeling with environmental corrections

### Power Adjustment Guidelines

| Condition | FTP Adjustment | Source |
|-----------|---------------|--------|
| Hot (>30C) | -5% to -10% | [E] |
| Very hot (>35C) + humid | -10% to -15% | [E] |
| Altitude 1,500-2,500m | -3% to -7% | [R] |
| Altitude >2,500m | -7% to -15% | [R] |
| Cold (<5C) | 0% to -2% (after warmup) | [E] |
| Headwind >25 kph | Adjust target speed, not watts | [E] |

## 7. Common Mistakes

1. **No warm-weather training before a hot race** -- spring races are harder than fall races because there was no summer acclimatization (Higdon) [E]
2. **Arriving at altitude 3-5 days early** -- the worst timing; O2 saturation has crashed but adaptation has not begun [R]
3. **Trusting sweat tests for electrolyte prescription** -- nearly useless; composition changes daily (Persp-41) [R]
4. **Drinking only water in heat** -- risks hyponatremia; need electrolytes for events >4 hours [R]
5. **Not packing cold-weather gear** -- race conditions can change overnight (lake turnover example) [E]
6. **Ignoring WBGT in favor of temperature alone** -- humidity is often the more dangerous factor [R]
7. **Maintaining sea-level power targets at altitude** -- guaranteed early blowup [E]
