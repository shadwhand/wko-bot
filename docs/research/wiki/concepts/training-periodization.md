# Training Periodization

Periodization is the systematic structuring of training into cycles of progressive overload and recovery, designed to maximize adaptation while minimizing injury, illness, and burnout. This page covers traditional, block, polarized, and pyramidal models, phase design, seasonal planning, and how these concepts map to the platform's analytics modules.

Evidence levels: **[R]** = Research-backed, **[E]** = Experience-based (practitioner consensus), **[O]** = Opinion/emerging.

---

## 1. Core Principle: Overload + Recovery = Adaptation

Training itself does not produce adaptation. The recovery that follows training allows the body to rebuild at a higher functional level (supercompensation). Periodization structures this cycle so that overload periods are intense enough to stimulate growth, and recovery periods are long enough to allow the body to absorb the stimulus. [R]

- **Performance plateaus are a dynamic equilibrium** between stimulus and recovery -- an inverted U: beyond recovery capacity, performance regresses (EC TMT-72) [E]
- **The body has ONE pool for ALL stress** -- training + work + family + sleep debt are not separate accounts; they draw from a shared allostatic load budget (McEwen; EC TMT-48) [R]
- **Consistency beats optimization** -- a boom-bust pattern (rapid CTL ramp + crash, repeated annually) produces the same annual training as a moderate approach, with more misery and higher injury risk (EC TMT-48) [E]

## 2. The Three Cycles

### 2.1 Macrocycle (Season)

The macrocycle spans an entire year or competitive season. It provides the bird's-eye view of training, incorporating all phases from off-season through competition. A macrocycle typically contains 8-12 mesocycles and is organized around 1-3 "A" priority events.

| Component | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Duration | 1 full season (typically 40-52 weeks) |
| Purpose | Long-range planning, periodization of all phases |
| Key tool | Annual Training Plan (ATP) in TrainingPeaks |
| Planning direction | Work backward from A-priority events |

**Seasonal structure (traditional road cycling):**

```
Off-Season --> Base --> Build --> Peak --> Race --> Transition --> Off-Season
  (2-4 wk)   (8-12)   (4-8)   (1-3)  (varies)   (1-2 wk)     (repeat)
```

[TrainingPeaks: Macrocycles, Mesocycles, Microcycles; How to Create Your Annual Training Plan]

### 2.2 Mesocycle (Training Block)

A mesocycle is a focused block of training designed to develop a specific fitness quality. Typical duration is 3-6 weeks, consisting of 2-5 weeks of progressive loading followed by a recovery period.

| Pattern | Structure | Best For |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| 21-day (3:1) | 16 hard days + 5 recovery days | Masters, newer athletes, high life stress |
| 28-day (3:1) | 23 hard days + 5 recovery days | Experienced, younger athletes |
| 2:1 | 2 weeks load + 1 week rest | VO2max/threshold blocks (EC TMT-55) |
| 4:1 | 4 weeks load + 1 week rest | Base/endurance blocks (EC TMT-55) |

**EC guidelines for rest-week frequency by block type:**

| Block Intensity | Rest Every | Source |
|----------------|------------|--------|
| VO2max / threshold+ | 2-3 weeks | EC TMT-55 [E] |
| Threshold / sweet spot | 3 weeks | EC TMT-55 [E] |
| Base / endurance | 3-5 weeks (6 for experienced) | EC TMT-55 [E] |

[TrainingPeaks: Macrocycles, Mesocycles, Microcycles; EC TMT-55, 58]

### 2.3 Microcycle (Week)

The microcycle is the smallest training unit, typically one week, containing 3-5 key workouts, lighter recovery sessions, and rest days.

**EC minimum viable weekly plan:**

> 2 hard days + fill remaining time with endurance: 1 threshold day + 1 VO2max day + endurance fill (EC TMT-45) [E]

Key microcycle principles:
- Progressive loading within the mesocycle -- each microcycle builds on the last in duration and/or intensity
- Hard/easy day alternation to allow partial recovery between key sessions
- Endurance rides fill remaining time at genuinely easy pace (IF 0.50-0.65, RPE 1-3/10) (EC TMT-69) [E]
- Sprint add-ons (3-5 x 10-15 sec) at the start of endurance rides have low opportunity cost and can be included year-round (EC TMT-45, 64) [E]

## 3. Periodization Models

### 3.1 Traditional (Linear) Periodization

The classic model originating from 1960s Soviet Olympic training. A large General Preparation phase (base) is followed by increasingly specific and intense training toward a single competitive peak.

**Sequence:** Prep --> Base (high volume, low intensity) --> Build (moderate volume, increasing intensity) --> Peak (reduced volume, high intensity, race-specific) --> Race --> Transition

**Strengths:**
- Gradual, safe progression -- minimal risk of injury [R]
- Deep aerobic conditioning from high-volume base phase [E]
- Well suited for athletes with 1-2 major events per year [E]

**Limitations:**
- One main peak per year -- poor fit for athletes with multiple target events [E]
- Long base phase causes detraining of high-intensity abilities (VO2max, sprint) [R]
- Requires high volume during base phase -- unrealistic for time-crunched athletes [E]
- Maintenance of non-focus abilities declines during single-focus phases [E]

**EC position on traditional base training:** Two- to three-week endurance blocks can be incorporated throughout the year. A single 2-3 month block of low-intensity riding will see power at lactate threshold and VO2max decline significantly. For amateur athletes, time-crunched programs with intensity throughout the year are more effective. [E]

[TrainingPeaks: Exploring Periodization Methods; The Myth of Winter Base Training for Cyclists]

### 3.2 Block Periodization

Developed in the 1980s-90s to address the limitations of the traditional model. Features shorter, highly focused training cycles that allow multiple peaks per season.

**Three phases per block:**

| Phase | Duration | Focus | Volume | Intensity |
|-------|----------|-------|--------|-----------|
| Accumulation | 2-6 weeks | Aerobic base, endurance, muscular endurance | High | Low-moderate |
| Transmutation | 2-4 weeks | Specific high-intensity (one energy system) | Moderate | High |
| Realization | 8-14 days | Taper / rest / race preparation | Low | Race-specific |

**Core concepts:**
- **Training residuals**: After an ability is trained, there is a set window (25-35 days for aerobic, shorter for anaerobic) before it begins to deteriorate when no longer trained [R] (Issurin 2007, 2010)
- **Variation**: Rotating stimuli prevents plateau and keeps the body adapting [R]
- **System overload**: Focusing one phase on one primary ability allows deeper overload than spreading effort across all abilities simultaneously [R]
- **Restitution**: While one system is being trained, previously trained systems rest and consolidate [R]

**Limitations:**
- Complex to program -- requires understanding of residuals and timing [E]
- Not for beginners -- consistency matters more than periodization sophistication at low training ages [E]
- Blocks changed too often can prevent sufficient adaptation in any single area [E]

**EC approach (simplified block model):** The simplest effective plan uses 2 hard days per week (1 threshold + 1 VO2max) with endurance fill. Block transitions follow natural progression: exhaust duration progression at current intensity before switching protocols. Progressive overload before protocol change. (EC TMT-44, 45, 52, 60) [E]

[TrainingPeaks: Exploring Periodization Methods; Implementing Block Periodization; Block Periodization in Action]

### 3.3 Polarized (80/20) Distribution

Not a periodization model per se, but an intensity distribution framework that can be applied within any periodization structure.

**Definition:** Approximately 80% of training time at low intensity (below ventilatory/lactate threshold 1), 20% at moderate-to-high intensity. Very little time in the "moderate" zone. [R]

**Evidence:**
- Stephen Seiler's research found this pattern across world-class cyclists, Nordic skiers, rowers, runners, swimmers, and triathletes globally [R]
- A 2014 study of Salzburg endurance athletes found the group closest to 80/20 achieved the greatest improvements in VO2max, time to exhaustion, peak power, and peak running velocity [R] (Sperlich & Stoggl 2014)
- Club runners doing 80/20 improved 10K times by twice as much as runners doing 50% moderate intensity [R] (Seiler 2013)

**EC nuance:** "No single training intensity distribution is statistically superior" -- the IPD meta-analysis (~350 athletes, 13 studies) found no meaningful overall difference between distributions. Individual variation (-20% to +30%) dwarfs the distribution effect (~1-2%). (EC Persp-38) [R]

**Practical implication:** The biggest amateur error is riding endurance too hard (IF 0.70-0.75). The 80/20 framework's main value is forcing athletes to make their easy days truly easy, which preserves quality for hard days. (EC TMT-69) [E]

[TrainingPeaks: Using the 80/20 Rule; EC Persp-38, TMT-69]

### 3.4 Pyramidal Distribution

A variation where moderate-intensity training occupies a larger share than in polarized models. The distribution looks like a pyramid: most time at low intensity, moderate time at threshold, least time at high intensity.

**EC position:** "All roads lead to PGC-1alpha" -- tempo, sweet spot, and threshold all stimulate mitochondrial biogenesis similarly (EC TMT-54). The practical differences between polarized and pyramidal approaches are small relative to individual variation. What matters most is total volume, sufficient intensity stimulus, and genuinely easy recovery days. [R+E]

### 3.5 Reverse Periodization

Flips the traditional model: high-intensity, low-volume work in winter (when daylight and weather are limited), layering longer aerobic training as the goal race approaches in summer.

**Best for:** Northern hemisphere athletes racing Ironman/70.3 in summer, athletes with established aerobic bases, time-crunched athletes who cannot do high winter volume. [E]

**Caution:** Risk of mental burnout from focused high-intensity training far from goal race. Athletes may feel "fit" too early and peak prematurely. [E]

[TrainingPeaks: Reverse vs Traditional Periodization for Triathlon Training]

## 4. Training Phases in Detail

### 4.1 Off-Season / Transition (1-4 weeks)

**Purpose:** Physical and mental recovery from the competitive season.

- **1-2 weeks minimal/no riding**, then slow re-entry (EC TMT-43) [E]
- Cross-training encouraged: strength (posterior chain, core), running, swimming for bone density and injury prevention (EC TMT-48) [E]
- Do NOT crack yourself mentally in off-season -- burnout before season starts is real (EC TMT-64) [E]
- Weight loss timing: off-season or low-intensity phases ONLY (EC TMT-48, 52) [E]
- Gym: off-season build at 2-3x/week heavy, low reps (EC WD-56, TMT-42) [R]
- Season review: analyze what worked, identify strengths/limiters, set goals for next season

**Detraining timeline:**
| Days Off | Effect |
|----------|--------|
| 0-10 days | Minimal measurable detraining (EC TMT-55) [E] |
| 10-14 days | VO2max begins to decline; aerobic markers start dropping [R] |
| 2-4 weeks | 6% VO2max decline; cardiac output, blood volume, stroke volume decrease [R] |
| 4-8 weeks | Significant cardiovascular deconditioning; lactate threshold declines [R] |
| 8+ weeks | Near-complete reversal of recently acquired adaptations [R] |

**Key finding:** Strength gains and neural drive are maintained much longer than aerobic adaptations. Sprint power and maximal output show minimal decline in the first 2-4 weeks. (TrainingPeaks: All About Detraining) [R]

**Residual fitness:** Athletes with many consecutive years of training hold onto fitness longer than those who recently acquired it. (TrainingPeaks: All About Detraining) [R]

### 4.2 Base Phase (6-12 weeks)

**Purpose:** Develop overall training capacity ("training to train"). Prepare the body for more intensive training in build and peak phases.

**Focus areas:** Aerobic endurance, muscular endurance, neuromuscular efficiency, movement patterns, gym strength.

**EC guidance:**
- Do NOT spend 2-3 months doing exclusively low-intensity riding [E]
- Include some intensity year-round -- "going slow makes you slow" applies to time-crunched athletes [E]
- For athletes with limited time, higher-intensity programs (8-10 hrs/week with LT and VO2max intervals) are more effective than pure base miles [E]
- Endurance IF target: 0.50-0.65 (EC TMT-69) [E]
- LT1 rises best from lots of hours WELL UNDER it, not training at LT1 specifically (EC TMT-73) [E]

**TrainingPeaks base-phase metrics to track:** cardiac drift (endurance indicator), lactate threshold pace/power (increasing), HRV baseline establishment. [E]

### 4.3 Build Phase (4-8 weeks)

**Purpose:** Maximize all-around endurance fitness and begin honing race-specific fitness.

**Structure:**
- Early build: varied workouts challenging all fitness components (anaerobic power, VO2max, threshold, endurance)
- Late build: increasingly specific to demands of upcoming A-race
- Highest or near-highest training load of the year [E]

**EC progression framework:**
- Threshold: 4x10 -> 3x15 -> 2x20 -> 2x25 -> 1x40+ min at FTP (EC TMT-45, 60) [E]
- VO2max: ~20 min total at target; formats 8x3, 5x5, 3x8 all viable (EC WD-55) [E]
- Sweet spot: ~88-93% FTP; becomes time-inefficient as fitness improves (EC TMT-44) [E]
- Progressive overload before protocol change -- exhaust simple duration/volume progression before switching protocols (EC TMT-44, 45, 52, 60) [E]

**CTL ramp rate guidelines:**

| Rate | Risk | Source |
|------|------|--------|
| < 3 TSS/day per week | May lack sufficient stimulus | Couzens [E] |
| 3-5 TSS/day per week | Sustainable long-term ramp | PMC consensus [E] |
| 5-8 TSS/day per week | Upper sustainable range | Friel, Nicoli [E] |
| > 8 TSS/day per week | High risk of overreaching | Friel, Allison [E] |
| 5-15 CTL per month | Good range (fragile to hardy) | Couzens [E] |

### 4.4 Peak Phase (1-3 weeks)

**Purpose:** Final race-specific preparation and taper.

**Structure:**
- Most challenging workouts are highly race-specific
- Reduced volume, maintained intensity
- Deemphasize non-race-specific fitness components
- CTL should reach its highest point 2-3 weeks before race day, then begin controlled decline during taper (TrainingPeaks: Managing Your Training Stress Balance) [E]

**Taper guidelines:**
- Target race-day TSB of slightly positive (+5 to +20) [E]
- Pro cycling example (Tinkoff-Saxo/Boaro): optimal TSB around +20 with highest possible CTL [E]
- Shorter events require longer tapers (more freshness needed); longer events require shorter tapers [E]
- End-of-rest test: sprint power + 5 min at FTP; if both feel good, resume (EC TMT-55) [E]

### 4.5 Race Phase (varies)

**Purpose:** Competition execution and in-season maintenance.

- **In-season FTP maintenance:** 1 threshold/sweet spot session every 1-2 weeks at RPE 6-7/10 (EC TMT-60) [E]
- **Gym: in-season maintain** at 1x/week heavy singles RPE 7/10 (EC TMT-42, WD-56) [R]
- Multiple peaks possible with block periodization (3-season example: Tinkoff-Saxo Boaro peaked 3 times across Feb-Oct) [E]
- Mid-season breaks (full week off May/June) are invaluable for long seasons (EC TMT-48) [E]

## 5. Phase Transitions

### 5.1 Base to Build

- Increase intensity gradually while maintaining volume; do not spike intensity suddenly
- FTP test or rolling PD curve check to establish current zones before increasing intensity
- Begin including 1-2 moderate-intensity sessions per week alongside endurance base

### 5.2 Build to Peak

- Shift from developing all fitness components to prioritizing race-specific abilities
- Begin reducing volume while maintaining or slightly increasing intensity
- Deemphasize fitness components less relevant to target event

### 5.3 Race to Transition

- Allow full recovery: both physical and psychological
- Season review while memories are fresh
- Identify what worked and what did not before committing to next year's plan

### 5.4 Anti-Panic Protocol (EC TMT-71) [E]

When training falls behind schedule:
- **8+ weeks to event:** Add volume first for 3-4 weeks, THEN start intensity progression
- **<4 weeks to event:** Pivot goals (finish, enjoy, treat as training race)
- **NEVER:** Double intensity immediately to "catch up" -- panic training almost always backfires

## 6. Seasonal Planning Considerations

### 6.1 Multiple A-Events

Athletes with multiple target events can use block periodization to create 2-3 peaks per season. Each peak requires its own accumulation-transmutation-realization cycle.

**Practical rule:** The more important the event, the longer the preceding block should be. Small blocks as short as 4-5 weeks can provide adequate preparation for mid-season goals. (TrainingPeaks: Implementing Block Periodization) [E]

### 6.2 The Double Peak

Peaking twice in a season requires careful management of the "valley" between peaks. CTL should not decline for more than 2 consecutive weeks after the first peak, and the second build phase begins from a higher fitness baseline. (TrainingPeaks: How to Peak Twice in One Racing Season) [E]

### 6.3 Winter/Indoor Training

- Indoor training generates 1.1-1.2x the recovery cost of equivalent outdoor TSS (thermoregulation strain) (EC TMT-51) [E]
- Block periodization with shorter, focused intervals suits trainer-based training well [E]
- Include variety: tempo, sweet spot, strength intervals, sprints to prevent boredom and maintain broad fitness [E]

### 6.4 Time-Crunched Athletes

- Traditional base training is counterproductive when volume cannot increase (TrainingPeaks: Myth of Winter Base Training) [E]
- Higher-intensity programs at 8-10 hrs/week can produce competitive fitness for events up to 3 hours [E]
- Minimum effective dose: 4-5x/week for any duration; habit > plan perfection (EC TMT-48) [E]
- Training camps can provide CTL boost of 8-10 TSS/day for desk-jockey athletes (TrainingPeaks: PMC for Spring Training Camp) [E]

## 7. Conflicts and Debates

| Topic | Traditional View | EC/Modern Position | Evidence |
|-------|-----------------|-------------------|----------|
| Winter base miles required | Yes, 2-3 months LSD | No -- 2-3 week blocks sufficient; include intensity year-round | TMT-69, TMT-73 [E] |
| Polarized is best | 80/20 is optimal for all | No single distribution is superior; individual variation dominates | Persp-38 [R] |
| Zone 2 is special | Zone 2 has unique adaptations | Total volume matters more than sitting in specific zone | TMT-69 [E] |
| CTL = fitness | Higher CTL = fitter | CTL is a proxy, not fitness itself; peak performances often occur after CTL dips | TMT-68 [E] |
| Rigid plan adherence | Follow the plan exactly | Plans are "written in sand"; 100% compliance is a warning sign | TMT-52, 73 [E] |
| More training = more fitness | Always add load | Low-volume athletes can be recovery-limited, not stimulus-limited | TMT-72 [E] |
| Strength hurts endurance | Avoid gym for cyclists | Aerobic and anaerobic are NOT zero-sum; heavy lifting does not hurt durability | WD-60 [E] |
