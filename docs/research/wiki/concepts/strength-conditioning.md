# Strength & Conditioning for Cyclists

Off-bike training to improve power output, injury resilience, bone health, and longevity.

Evidence levels: **[R]** = Research-backed, **[E]** = Experience-based, **[O]** = Opinion.

---

## 1. Why Cyclists Need Strength Training

### Core Rationale

- **Strength is a neural skill** -- neural drive adaptations can be maintained with very low volume once established (TMT-42) [R]
- **Cyclists over 35-40: strength training is essential for health** even if bike performance is not the primary goal (TMT-73) [E]
- **Cycling is non-weight-bearing** -- road cyclists who train regularly have significantly low bone mineral density in the lumbar spine, pelvis, hip, and femoral neck compared to both athletes in other sports and non-athletes (Olmedillas 2012 systematic review of 31 studies) [R]
- **Aerobic and anaerobic are NOT zero-sum** -- heavy lifting does NOT hurt durability long-term (WD-60) [E]
- **Cyclists are somewhat protected from unwanted hypertrophy** due to AMPK pathway interference with mTOR from high aerobic volume (WD-56) [R]
- **Caloric surplus is required for significant hypertrophy** -- cyclists in energy balance are unlikely to gain meaningful mass (WD-56) [R]
- **Strength training can unlock 1-min power PRs** especially for riders who rarely train above 120% FTP (TMT-70) [E]

### What the Research Shows About Bone Density

| Finding | Detail | Source |
|---------|--------|--------|
| Masters cyclists vs age-matched | More masters classified as osteoporotic; risk increased over 7 years | Nichols 2011 [R] |
| Elite/experienced cyclists | Consistently low BMD in lumbar spine, pelvis, femoral neck | Olmedillas 2012 [R] |
| Low-level cyclists | No significant difference from controls | Olmedillas 2012 [R] |
| Bone stimulus threshold | A bone needs 1/10th of its breaking force to stimulate growth | NSCA 2015 [R] |
| Site-specificity | Bone density improvements are local to loaded areas | NSCA 2015 [R] |

> **Platform relevance:** The platform user is an ultra-endurance cyclist logging high volume. Bone health monitoring and strength prompts are particularly relevant.

## 2. Periodized Strength Protocols

### EC-Derived Protocols (Kolie Moore)

| Phase | Protocol | Source |
|-------|----------|--------|
| Off-season build | 2-3x/week, 1-3 heavy singles at RPE 9-9.5 | WD-56 [R] |
| In-season maintain | 1x/week, 3 heavy singles at RPE 7/10 | TMT-42 [R] |
| Strength without mass | Avoid back-off sets, AMRAPs; keep reps 1-3 | WD-56 [R] |
| Limited equipment | Single-leg elevated heel squats + slow eccentrics | TMT-47 [E] |
| If weight increases | Reduce volume (sets) before reducing intensity (load) | WD-56 [R] |

### General Preparation Framework (TrainingPeaks)

| Period | Focus | Frequency | Notes |
|--------|-------|-----------|-------|
| General Prep (off-season) | Regenerative protocols, movement competence | 3-4x/week, 30-45 min | FMS assessment recommended; build movement literacy first |
| Specific Prep (pre-season) | Power format (e.g., back squats 4x8 at 50% max) | 2-3x/week | Mix regenerative + power protocols |
| Pre-Competition | Regenerative only or minimal maintenance | 1x/week | Reduce to avoid fatigue accumulation |
| Competition (in-season) | Heavy singles, RPE 7/10 | 1x/week | Neural maintenance, minimal hypertrophy |

### Minimum Effective Dose

- **3-4 sessions of 30-45 minutes per week** during off-season is typically sufficient (TrainingPeaks) [E]
- **1 session per week** during racing season for maintenance (TMT-42) [R]
- Compound lifts (squats, deadlifts, lunges) + isolation (calf raises, hamstring curls) = foundation

## 3. Key Exercises for Cyclists

### Primary Movements

| Exercise | Target | Why It Matters |
|----------|--------|----------------|
| Back Squat / Front Squat | Quads, glutes, core | Primary force production pattern; load spine for bone density |
| Deadlift (trap bar or KB) | Posterior chain, hip hinge | Reinforces hip hinge pattern needed on bike; addresses quad dominance |
| Single-Leg Split Squat (RFESS) | Unilateral strength | Mimics pedal stroke asymmetry; addresses imbalances |
| Nordic Hamstring Curl | Hamstrings, eccentric | Injury prevention; corrects anterior pelvic tilt from cycling |
| Farmer's Carry | Grip, core, whole-body | Spine loading for bone density; postural endurance |

### Ancillary Movements

| Exercise | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| Pallof Press | Anti-rotation core stability |
| Lateral Lunges / Side Step-Ups | Frontal plane work (cycling is sagittal-dominant) |
| Calf Raises | Connective tissue resilience |
| Plyometrics (box jumps, jump squats) | Bone stimulus via impact; neuromuscular power |
| Pull-up Holds / Hanging | Grip endurance (especially relevant for MTB) |

### Movement Quality Before Load

1. **Functional Movement Screen (FMS)** as starting point -- identifies mobility, stability, and strength limitations [E]
2. Cyclists are commonly quad-dominant with overstretched hamstrings from anterior pelvic tilt [E]
3. Many cyclists are "knee squatters" -- must learn hip hinge pattern before loading [E]
4. Core strength and endurance are key for control, especially in ultra-distance where fatigue degrades position [E]

## 4. Power-to-Weight Optimization

### Three Paths to Improve W/kg

1. **Increase power output, keep weight constant** -- safest approach
2. **Keep power, decrease weight** -- requires careful management
3. **Increase power while decreasing weight** -- most effective but highest risk

### Key Principles

- **W/kg is one of the single best predictors of cycling performance** -- especially for climbing [R]
- **One pound of excess weight costs ~2 watts on a climb** [E]
- **5% body mass reduction = ~15 minutes faster on Leadville 100** (BestBikeSplit modeling) [E]
- **Weight loss should NEVER coincide with high-intensity blocks** (TMT-48, 52) [E]
- **Minimum healthy body fat: 6% men, 14% women** -- below these ranges negatively affects health and performance [R]
- **Energy Availability below 30 kcal/kg FFM/day causes significant health consequences** -- RED-S risk (Persp-36) [R]

### Integration with Platform

Use Power Duration Curve profiling at 5s, 1min, 5min, and FTP durations to identify which system to target for W/kg improvement. Calculate W/kg at each duration and compare against phenotype profile.

> **Conflict:** EC explicitly warns against Goodhart's Law around durability -- over-indexing on weight can lead to bad race strategies and RED-S (WD-60) [E]

## 5. Injury Prevention

### Cyclist-Specific Risks

| Risk | Cause | Prevention |
|------|-------|------------|
| Low bone density / osteoporosis | Non-weight-bearing + low body mass | Heavy lifting + plyometrics + impact exercise |
| Anterior pelvic tilt | Prolonged seated position | Posterior chain work (deadlifts, Nordic curls, hip flexor stretching) |
| IT band syndrome | Sagittal-plane dominance | Lateral movements, foam rolling, hip abductor strengthening |
| Lower back pain | Core fatigue during long rides | Anti-rotation exercises, plank variations, core endurance |
| Knee pain | Quad dominance, poor hinge pattern | Movement screening, single-leg work, hamstring strengthening |
| Grip fatigue (MTB/gravel) | Prolonged vibration + braking | Grip training, farmer's carries |

### Connective Tissue Adaptation

- Tissues adapt more slowly than muscles -- progressive loading over months is critical [R]
- Higher total mileage and training years = more essential load tolerance becomes [E]
- Plyometrics and impact training should be phased in gradually after building a foundation [E]

## 6. Common Mistakes

1. **Confusing strength maintenance with hypertrophy** -- low reps/heavy weight maintains strength; moderate reps/moderate weight builds mass (TMT-42) [R]
2. **Exclusively unilateral work** -- mix in bilateral when possible (TMT-47) [E]
3. **High-rep AMRAPs for cyclists** -- increasingly aerobic at higher reps, taxing recovery differently (TMT-47) [R]
4. **Skipping gym work after 35** -- health benefits are independent of bike performance (TMT-73) [E]
5. **Neglecting bone density** -- cyclists avoid weight-bearing even during recovery periods, compounding risk [R]
6. **Circuit training for bone health** -- lower resistance means insufficient force production for bone stimulus; heavy loads with longer rest intervals are more effective [R]
7. **Loading spine too late** -- lumbar spine is the most critical area for cyclists and requires deliberate spinal loading (squats, deadlifts) not just leg press [R]
