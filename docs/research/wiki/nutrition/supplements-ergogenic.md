# Supplements & Ergogenic Aids

Caffeine, creatine, beetroot/nitrates, beta-alanine, and other supplements with evidence levels for cycling performance.

Evidence levels: **[R]** = Research-backed, **[E]** = Experience-based, **[O]** = Opinion.

---

## 1. Caffeine

### Performance Effect

Meta-analyses consistently show **2-4% improvement** in endurance time-trial performance. [R] (Southward et al. 2018; Guest et al. 2021)

This is the single most reliable legal ergogenic aid available to cyclists.

### Dosing

| Parameter | Value | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Optimal dose | 3-6 mg/kg body mass | 210-420 mg for 70 kg rider |
| Minimum effective | ~2 mg/kg | ~140 mg for 70 kg rider |
| Diminishing returns | >6 mg/kg | More GI distress, no additional benefit |
| Toxic threshold | >9 mg/kg | Tremor, tachycardia, GI issues |

Source: nutrition-racing.md, Guest et al. 2021 (ISSN Position Stand) [R]

### Timing

| Strategy | Protocol | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-race | 3-6 mg/kg, 40-60 min before start | Peak plasma at ~30-60 min post-ingestion |
| Mid-race boost | 1-2 mg/kg at fatigue onset | Effective for long events |
| Late-race rescue | ~200 mg in final 30-60 min | As effective as larger pre-race dose for final effort (Spriet 2014) |
| Half-life | 3-5 hr (3-7 hr range) | CYP1A2 polymorphism dependent |

Source: nutrition-racing.md, Spriet 2014 [R]

### Habituation

- Regular consumers: blunted but still meaningful benefit (~1.5-2% vs ~3-4% for naive users) [R]
- **Withdrawal protocol**: 2-4 days abstinence before race restores full sensitivity [R]
- Evidence is mixed on whether this is worth the withdrawal symptoms [R]
- **Current practical advice**: Do NOT withdraw before important races if habituated. Withdrawal symptoms impair training quality leading into the event. Benefit still exists for habitual users. [R]

**Conflict with conventional wisdom**: Some TP articles and coaches recommend 10-day pre-race caffeine withdrawal. Current evidence says this is counterproductive for habituated athletes. [R] vs [E]

### Genetic Variation (CYP1A2)

- AA genotype (~50% of population) = "fast metabolizer": full benefit [R]
- AC/CC genotype = "slow metabolizer": may get less benefit or even impairment at high doses [R] (Guest et al. 2018)
- For modeling without genetic data: assume moderate response (2-3% TT improvement) [R]

### Caffeine and GI Interaction

- Stimulates gastric acid secretion and colonic motility [R]
- In sleep-deprived riders with compromised GI function, high doses can worsen nausea and cause urgent diarrhea [E]
- Coffee is more GI-stimulating than caffeine pills (chlorogenic acids) [R]
- For late-event caffeine: pills or caffeinated gels may be better tolerated than coffee [E]

### Multi-Day Caffeine Strategy

See [Ultra Nutrition](ultra-nutrition.md) Section 7 for detailed multi-day protocol.

Key points:
- 24-hr budget: stay under 800-1,000 mg total to avoid significant side effects [E]
- 48-hr event budget: ~1,200-1,600 mg total, weighted toward nighttime hours [E]
- "Coffee nap": consume caffeine, nap 20 min, wake as caffeine peaks [E]
- Caffeine "stops working" by second night of sustained wakefulness for most riders [E]

## 2. Creatine

### Headline: ZERO Effect on Endurance Performance [R]

2023 meta-analysis: 277 total participants, well-trained. Effect size: **-0.07** (95% CI: -0.27 to +0.13). Brackets zero. No effect. [R] (WD-58)

### Detailed Effects

| Effect | Evidence | Magnitude | Practical Relevance |
|---|---|---|---|
| Aerobic/endurance performance | Zero | ES -0.07, N=277 | Irrelevant to road/endurance cycling |
| Repeated sprint mean power | Small positive | ES 0.61, ~27W on 1000W sprint | Within power meter error (~2-3%) |
| Peak sprint power | No effect | -- | -- |
| Fatigue rate | No effect | -- | -- |
| Body mass gain (water) | Largest reliable effect | ES 0.79, 0.8-1.0 kg avg, some 2+ kg | Net negative for climbing |
| Cognitive (sleep-deprived) | Preliminary | 1-2 sec/question at 4am, n=15 | Interesting for ultras, needs replication |

Source: WD-58, ec-master-reference.md [R]

### Decision Matrix by Athlete Type

| Athlete Type | Recommendation | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Road racer (climbing) | **No** | Weight gain negates any sprint benefit |
| Road racer (flat/sprinter) | **Maybe** -- try it, assess weight gain | Small sprint gain; if weight minimal, net positive possible |
| Endurance track sprinter (gym-heavy) | **Yes** | Supports gym work for sprint power |
| Track sprinter (no gym) | **No** | Without gym stimulus, only water weight |
| Mountain biker XC / CX | **No** | W/kg matters; no aerobic benefit |
| Downhill / Enduro | **Yes** | Mass helps; gravity assists; starts matter |
| Ultra-endurance | **Consider for cognition** | Sleep deprivation decision-making may improve |

Source: WD-58 [R][E]

### Practical Dosing

| Detail | Guideline |
|---|---|
| Dosing | 3-5 g/day, no loading needed unless time-constrained |
| Loading (if desired) | 4x5g/day for 5-7 days (NEVER 20g at once -- GI distress) |
| Timing | Mix in yogurt, shake, hot drink -- doesn't matter |
| Duration | Continuous; stop 1-2 weeks pre-body-comp scan if desired |
| Stopping | Strength/muscle gains persist; only water weight is lost |
| Contamination | Use third-party tested product; keep lot numbers |

Source: WD-58 [R]

### Myths Debunked (WD-58)

1. **"Creatine is a game-changer for all athletes"** -- No. Zero endurance effect. Marginal for cycling sprints. [R]
2. **"Women need more creatine"** -- Based on misreferenced MDPI paper. No evidence of differential effectiveness. [R]
3. **"Creatine helps recovery"** -- No evidence found in the literature. [R]
4. **"You must load creatine"** -- Not necessary; 3-5 g/day reaches saturation in ~3-4 weeks. [R]

**Platform stance**: Creatine is irrelevant to endurance performance modeling. Do not factor into power predictions. If tracking supplements, annotate body mass jumps as water weight, not fat. (WD-58, ec-master-reference.md) [R]

## 3. Beetroot Juice / Nitrates

### Mechanism

Dietary nitrate (NO3-) is converted to nitrite (NO2-) by oral bacteria, then to nitric oxide (NO) in tissues. NO improves:
- Mitochondrial efficiency (reduced O2 cost for same power) [R]
- Muscle contractile efficiency [R]
- Blood flow distribution [R]

### Performance Effect

| Finding | Details | Evidence Level |
|---|---|---|
| O2 cost reduction | 3-5% reduction at submaximal intensities | [R] (Lansley et al. 2011; Jones 2014) |
| Time trial improvement | 1-3% in moderately trained athletes | [R] (meta-analyses) |
| Effect in elite athletes | Attenuated or absent | [R] (Porcelli et al. 2015) |
| Effect on sprint/high-intensity | Moderate positive (~3-5%) for Type II fiber actions | [R] |
| Effect on endurance TT | Small but real (~1-2%) in sub-elite | [R] |

### Who Benefits Most

| Group | Benefit | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Recreational / moderately trained | Meaningful (2-3%) | Largest effect sizes |
| Well-trained (4+ W/kg) | Small (0.5-1.5%) | Diminishing returns |
| Elite (5+ W/kg) | Negligible to zero | Already optimized NO pathways |
| Altitude / hypoxic conditions | Enhanced | Nitrate more effective when O2 is limited |

### Dosing Protocol

| Parameter | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Acute dose | 6-8 mmol nitrate (~400-500 mg), 2-3 hr before event |
| Chronic loading | 6-8 mmol/day for 3-6 days before event (additive benefit) |
| Form | Concentrated beetroot juice (e.g., Beet It shots) preferred over whole beets for dose control |
| Timing | 2-3 hr before exercise (peak plasma nitrite at ~2-3 hr) |

### Caveats

- Antibacterial mouthwash blocks nitrate-to-nitrite conversion (kills oral bacteria) -- avoid for 4+ hr pre-event [R]
- High-dose vitamin C may interfere [R]
- Gastrointestinal distress at high doses (beeturia is harmless but alarming) [E]
- Effect declines with training status; elite athletes unlikely to benefit meaningfully [R]

## 4. Beta-Alanine

### Mechanism

Beta-alanine is the rate-limiting precursor to carnosine, an intramuscular buffer. Increased carnosine = better buffering of H+ ions during high-intensity exercise. [R]

### Performance Effect

| Finding | Details | Evidence Level |
|---|---|---|
| Buffering capacity | Increases muscle carnosine by 40-80% over 4-10 weeks | [R] |
| Performance improvement | 0.5-3% in efforts lasting 1-10 min | [R] (Hobson et al. 2012 meta-analysis) |
| Optimal duration benefit | Peaks at 1-4 min all-out efforts | [R] |
| Effect on longer endurance | Minimal to zero | [R] |
| Sprint repeatability | Small positive effect | [R] |

### Relevance to Cycling

- Most relevant for: track pursuit, short time trials, finishing surges, hill climbs 2-8 min [E]
- Less relevant for: road races >30 min, ultra-endurance, flat time trials [E]
- FTP-duration efforts: marginal at best [R]

### Dosing

| Parameter | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Daily dose | 3.2-6.4 g/day (split into 4x 0.8-1.6 g doses) |
| Loading period | 4-10 weeks for carnosine saturation |
| Side effects | Paraesthesia (skin tingling) -- harmless but unpleasant at high single doses |
| Mitigation | Use sustained-release formulations or split doses |

### Platform Relevance

Marginal. Only worth tracking for athletes focused on short, high-intensity efforts (1-4 min). Not a priority for the endurance-focused platform.

## 5. Other Supplements

### Evidence Summary Table

| Supplement | Claimed Benefit | Actual Evidence | Dose | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Sodium bicarbonate** | Buffer H+ ions | 1-2% improvement in 1-7 min efforts [R] | 0.2-0.3 g/kg, 60-90 min pre | GI distress is major limiter; useful for specific events |
| **Tart cherry juice** | Reduce inflammation, speed recovery | Weak; must start 1 week before event [R] (Persp-41) | 30 mL concentrate 2x/day for 7 days | Low priority; endurance athletes already have robust antioxidant systems |
| **Omega-3 fatty acids** | Anti-inflammatory, cardiovascular | Beneficial for general health; performance benefit unclear [R] | 2-4 g EPA+DHA/day | Health supplement, not ergogenic; Omega Index of 8-12% optimal |
| **Vitamin D** | Bone health, immune, muscle function | Important to correct deficiency; no benefit if replete [R] | 2,000-10,000 IU/day based on blood levels | Test serum 25(OH)D; target >40 ng/mL |
| **Iron** | Oxygen transport | Critical to correct deficiency; dangerous if over-supplemented [R] | Full iron panel required before supplementing | 11% of males and up to 35% of females in athletic populations are deficient; 3-4+ month correction time (TMT-64) |
| **Probiotics** | GI health, reduced infection | Some evidence for reduced GI symptoms pre-competition [R] (Jager et al. 2019 ISSN) | Multi-strain, 4+ weeks pre-competition | Modest effect sizes; useful for GI-sensitive athletes |
| **BCAAs** | Muscle recovery | Unnecessary if protein intake is adequate (2+ g/kg/day) [R] | -- | Save your money if eating sufficient protein |
| **Collagen/gelatin** | Tendon/ligament repair | Emerging evidence for connective tissue [R] | 15 g + vitamin C, 30-60 min pre-exercise | Promising for injury prevention; not for performance |

## 6. Supplement Priority Ranking for Cyclists

### Tier 1: Strong Evidence, Large Effect

1. **Caffeine** -- 2-4% TT improvement; most reliable ergogenic aid [R]
2. **Carbohydrate** (on-bike fueling) -- Not a "supplement" but the #1 performance lever [R]
3. **Sodium/electrolytes** -- For events >4 hr in heat [R]

### Tier 2: Moderate Evidence, Conditional Benefit

4. **Beetroot juice/nitrates** -- 1-3% for sub-elite; negligible for elite [R]
5. **Beta-alanine** -- 0.5-3% for 1-10 min efforts only [R]
6. **Sodium bicarbonate** -- 1-2% for 1-7 min efforts; GI risk [R]

### Tier 3: Health/Recovery, Not Directly Ergogenic

7. **Vitamin D** -- Correct deficiency; no benefit if replete [R]
8. **Iron** -- Correct deficiency; dangerous to over-supplement [R]
9. **Omega-3** -- Health benefit; performance unclear [R]
10. **Probiotics** -- Modest GI benefit pre-competition [R]

### Tier 4: Minimal or No Evidence for Cycling

11. **Creatine** -- Zero endurance effect; weight gain is net negative for most cyclists [R]
12. **Tart cherry** -- Weak; timing is impractical [R]
13. **BCAAs** -- Redundant if protein intake is adequate [R]
14. **Collagen** -- For connective tissue, not performance [R]

## 7. Common Mistakes

1. **Spending on supplements before fixing fueling basics** -- Carbs on the bike and adequate daily protein are 10x more impactful than any supplement [E]
2. **Using creatine for road cycling** -- Net negative due to weight gain [R]
3. **High-dose caffeine (>6 mg/kg)** -- GI distress and anxiety with no additional benefit [R]
4. **Caffeine withdrawal before races** -- Counterproductive for habituated athletes [R]
5. **Beetroot juice for elite athletes** -- Effect disappears with training status [R]
6. **Iron supplementation without blood work** -- Dangerous; can cause hemochromatosis [R]
7. **Relying on antioxidant supplements** -- May actually blunt training adaptation [R]
8. **Not accounting for caffeine in GI strategy** -- High caffeine + depleted gut = disaster [E]
