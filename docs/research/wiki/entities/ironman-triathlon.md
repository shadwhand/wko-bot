# Ironman and 70.3 Triathlon Power Analysis

Reference compilation of bike power data, pacing strategies, and race execution from professional and age-group Ironman/70.3 events, sourced from TrainingPeaks articles and Best Bike Split analyses (2012-2017).

## Kona IRONMAN World Championships

### Course Characteristics

The Kona bike course (112 mi / 180 km) is uniquely challenging due to three compounding factors:

1. **Heat and humidity**: Typically ~80F / 80% humidity. Athletes see 5-10% power reduction vs temperate races. [Source: Kona Course Preview 2017, Ryan Cooper]

2. **Persistent adverse winds**: The prevailing wind pattern means athletes face a head or crosswind for the vast majority of the course:
   - Out of town toward Hawi: sheltered low headwind transitioning to stronger exposed headwind
   - After turnaround: crosswind off the coast (the famous Kona crosswinds)
   - Final section: headwind strengthens from the south back to transition
   - Conditions intensify throughout the day, so age groupers face worse wind than pros

3. **Climbing profile**: ~5000 ft / 1524 m total. The climb to Hawi (~500 ft) gets attention, but rolling terrain and constant wind add disproportionate difficulty.

**Rule of thumb**: In a windy year, add ~5 minutes to your bike split; in a good year, subtract ~5 minutes from typical Kona time. [Source: 2017 Qollector Analysis, Ryan Cooper]

[Source: "2017 Ironman World Championship Course Preview and Pro Predictions" -- Ryan Cooper, TrainingPeaks]

#### 2017 Kona -- Race Dynamics and Pacing (Quarq Qollector Analysis)

**Men's race tactical evolution**:

- Large swim pack (Lange, McNamee, Hoffman, O'Donnell, etc.) rode together conservatively until Wurf/Sanders/Kienle bridged before Hawi
- Pivotal moment at Hawi climb: Sanders lowered avg power to 315 W but this was <80% FTP for him vs ~90% FTP for chasers who followed
- After turnaround, Sanders' group put in a 365 W effort on 1.7-mile Kawaihae climb, breaking the chase group
- Top-3 men's bike times all broke the previous course record by 3+ minutes (mild wind year)
- Lange rode consistent 4:28:53 (vs 4:37:49 in 2016) without surging, saving energy for his record-breaking run
- Sanders rode ~9 min ahead of Lange into T2 but was caught at mile 23 of the run

**Strategic lesson**: "To bridge a 4-minute gap by the turnaround, Kienle or Sanders would need to push ~30 more watts than the lead group." This illustrates the asymmetric energy cost of chasing at Kona.

**Women's race**: Ryf rode consistent but not dominant on the bike, only overtaking Charles/Brandon in the final 25 miles (mile 87+). Ryf gained 5+ minutes in 25 miles when she finally pushed.

[Source: "2017 IRONMAN World Championship Interactive Quarq Qollector Pacing Analysis" -- Ryan Cooper, TrainingPeaks]

## 70.3 World Championship Data

### 2017 Chattanooga -- Ben Kanute (2nd Place)

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Finish time | 3:51:06 |
| Bike split | 2:08:10 |
| Average power | 325 W |
| Normalized power | 334 W |
| W/kg | 4.78 |
| TSS | 174 |
| IF | 0.90 |
| VI | 1.03 |
| Avg speed | 26.1 mph |
| Avg cadence | 99 rpm |

**Course**: 56 mi with 3000 ft climbing, including 8.5 mi / 8% grade climb to Lookout Mountain.

**Pacing strategy**: Kanute capped himself at 400 W on the Lookout Mountain climb, averaged 385 W for the 8.5-mile ascent. Led out of the swim and pushed the bike hard to establish a 4-minute lead over Kienle/Gomez. His coach Jim Vance said: "If he ran 1:14-1:16, not many could beat him." He ran 1:16:23 (3rd-fastest half-marathon ever for him).

**Key insight from coach Vance**: "We wanted to put everyone else on their heels from the beginning and make them question a lot of things." An IF of 0.90 is at the very aggressive end of 70.3 pacing -- but it worked because Kanute's swim lead reduced the need to conserve for a chase.

[Source: "Ben Kanute's 70.3 World Championship Power File" -- Susan Legacki, TrainingPeaks]

### 2017 Chattanooga -- Leon Griffin (3rd, non-championship race)

- First hour: 303 W (at FTP, effectively redlining)
- 30-mile mark climb (6%): NP 329 W for 4:30 (well above FTP) -- "this effort finally cracked him"
- Final hour: 264 W (significant fade)
- VI: 1.03 (still smooth despite the power fade)

**Age group advice from Griffin**:
- "If anyone underestimates the course, that's your biggest mistake"
- Miles 40-50 are the hardest if you haven't paced well: 329 W for 1 min at only 10 mph on short steep climbs
- Keep to the tri bike -- flat highway sections are too valuable to sacrifice aero position
- Sub-10 mph segments: use 27 or 28 tooth cassette if not a strong climber

[Source: "Pro Tips: 70.3 World Championship Bike Course" -- AJ Johnson, TrainingPeaks]

## Pacing Framework

### Optimal IF Targets by Distance

| Distance | Optimal IF Range | Source |
|----------|-----------------|--------|
| Ironman (full) | 0.68-0.78 | Allen/Coggan, adjusted for Kona heat |
| Ironman (typical, non-Kona) | 0.72-0.82 | Allen/Coggan |
| 70.3 (half) | 0.83-0.87 | Allen/Coggan, Training and Racing with a Power Meter |
| 70.3 (aggressive/pro) | 0.87-0.92 | Kanute example: 0.90 IF |

**Kona-specific adjustment**: Top male podium athletes typically race at 260-270 W in Kona vs 295-300+ W at other Ironman events -- a deliberate ~10% power reduction for heat/wind. [Source: 2017 Kona Preview, Ryan Cooper]

### Variability Index Targets

| Race Distance | Optimal VI | Notes |
|---------------|------------|-------|
| Ironman | < 1.05 | Joe Friel recommendation for good run off bike |
| 70.3 | < 1.05 | Same target; easier to achieve on flatter courses |
| Kona (pro) | 1.02-1.05 | Strategic surges (Hawi, Kawaihae) may raise VI |

**Evidence**: Chris Thomas (elite AG) ranged 1.00-1.08 across 40 half-Ironman races. His 1.02 VI at 2014 70.3 Worlds was good, but pushing extra bullets in final 7.5 km still affected his run. [Source: "Taking Risks: IRONMAN 70.3 World Championships Bike Pacing" -- Chris Thomas, TrainingPeaks]

### Pacing Decision Framework

From Thomas: "My effort on race day is dictated by triangulating my perceived effort, heart rate, and power in that order."

Priority order:
1. **Perceived effort** -- primary guide, especially early when legs are fresh
2. **Heart rate** -- secondary check for overextension
3. **Power** -- tertiary reference to stay within known zones

"I need to ride to my own feel. If I get too hung up on a certain power number or heart rate, I do not race as well."

[Source: "Taking Risks: IRONMAN 70.3 World Championships Bike Pacing" -- Chris Thomas, TrainingPeaks]

## Power-to-Run Correlation

### The Run Penalty for Overbiking

**Evidence from Kona 2012 (McKenzie)**:
- Bike: 290 W / 4.0 W/kg (80% FTP) for first half -- aggressive
- Dropped to 238 W second half -- clear fade
- Result: 24th overall despite being 8th off the bike. Run implosion.

**Evidence from Kona 2014 (Buckingham)**:
- First half: 284 W / 4.0 W/kg
- Second half: 245 W / 3.45 W/kg
- Result: 25th. Classic negative bike split = bad run.

**Counter-example (Jacobs 2012 winner)**:
- First half: 298 W / 4.3 W/kg (83% FTP)
- Second half: 265 W / 3.8 W/kg
- Result: 1st. Managed fade to only ~33 W; had fastest marathon splits in prior 2 years.

**The pattern**: A first-to-second-half power fade >15% correlates strongly with run implosion. Jacobs' ~11% fade was manageable; McKenzie's ~18% and Buckingham's ~14% were not.

### Run Power Analysis (Stryd Data from Kona)

| Athlete | Place | Marathon Time | Avg Run Power | 1st Half | 2nd Half | Drop-off | VI |
|---------|-------|---------------|---------------|----------|----------|----------|-----|
| Matt Russell | 12th | 2:54 | ~310 W | 321 W | 300 W | 6.5% / 21 W | 1.01 |
| Lionel Sanders | 29th | ~3:15 est. | 270 W | 319 W | ~240 est. | ~25% / 49 W | 1.04 |

**Russell's pacing hallmarks**: Highest 1 hr power didn't start until mile 2 (patient). Power drops on downhills (using gravity). No major uphill surges.

**Sanders' issues**: First hour was highest power output. Struggled from the Palani climb onward. 15% power drop-off vs Russell's 4%.

[Source: "Using Run Power to Perfect Your Ironman Run" -- Jim Vance, TrainingPeaks]

## Pro Training Benchmarks

### Craig Alexander's Kona Build Sessions

Three key sessions from 3x Kona champion Craig Alexander's 2014 build:

#### Session 1: IRONMAN Pace Intervals
- 8 x 10 min with 2 min recovery
- NP per interval: 275-290 W (slightly above 4 W/kg)
- IF per interval: 0.84-0.86 (slightly higher than race day)
- Cadence: high 80s to low 90s
- Followed by 1 hr motorpacing at NP 260 W / 96 rpm

**Purpose**: Replicate "all day long pace" -- IRONMAN effort or slightly above. Motorpacing afterward builds fast cadence under fatigue.

#### Session 2: Long Ride with Climbing + IM Pace TT
- 2 hrs flat/rolling, then Big Thompson climb (~30 min, NP 226 W, 75 rpm low cad strength work), Estes climb (40 min, NP 238 W, 72 rpm), 1 hr descent
- Finished with 32 min / 11 mi TT at 258 W / 3.79 W/kg -- *race day W/kg* after 6 hrs and 5700 ft climbing
- Total: 115 mi, NP 208 W, 3905 kcal

**Purpose**: Replicate end-of-race bike execution. The final TT confirms ability to hit race power under deep fatigue.

#### Session 3: Hard Intervals at Altitude
- Long warm-up with 2 x (30 sec on/30 sec off) sets: first at 300-330 W, second at 360-370 W
- Main set: 3-4-5-5-4-3 min pyramid with equal recovery, all at 340 W (slightly above threshold)
- Cadence: low 90s

[Source: "3 Key Sessions of Craig Alexander's Build to Kona" -- AJ Johnson, TrainingPeaks]

## Kona-Specific Strategy Checklist

Based on compiled pro and AG analysis:

### Pre-Race
- [ ] Acclimate to heat: 10-14 days in similar conditions, or heat training (sauna, indoor sessions in warm rooms)
- [ ] Model your bike plan with weather: Best Bike Split or similar; adjust power targets for forecast wind
- [ ] Determine race-day power: Start with non-Kona FTP, apply 5-10% reduction for heat
- [ ] Gear selection: 54 or 55 tooth chainring for Hawi descent (Jacobs spun out at 120 rpm with 54t)

### Bike Execution
- [ ] Swim-to-bike transition: Don't chase. "If you miss the first packs and are stuck solo, the engine size won't matter" (Ryan Cooper)
- [ ] First 30 miles (out to coast): Conservative. Sheltered, slight headwind. Keep IF below race target.
- [ ] Miles 30-55 (Hawi climb): Headwind increases. Power stays at or slightly below target. Do NOT bridge to groups above your power level.
- [ ] Hawi descent: Use gravity. Gear for the descent (55t helps avoid spinning out). Brief recovery zone.
- [ ] Miles 55-80 (crosswinds): Most dangerous section. Crosswinds shift from coast. Aero position critical. Power may need to increase to maintain speed.
- [ ] Miles 80-112 (return to town): Headwind often strengthens. Do NOT surge to maintain a speed target. Ride to power.
- [ ] **Nutrition**: Target 60-90 g/hr carbs. Prepare backup plan if special needs bag is missed (Potts 2016 experience).

### Run Execution
- [ ] Patient start up Palani Road. Do NOT push hard on the initial climb.
- [ ] Even pacing through Queen K and Energy Lab. VI target: 1.01-1.03.
- [ ] Manage power fade: Aim for <10% drop first-to-second-half. >15% = implosion risk.

## Key Strategic Insights

1. **Kona is won on the run, but lost on the bike.** Every overbiking case study (McKenzie 2012, Buckingham 2014, Beranek 2016, Kienle 2017) shows the same pattern: aggressive early bike power leads to run implosion. [Multiple sources]

2. **The ~80% FTP ceiling**: Pro men winning at Kona consistently ride 75-83% of FTP. Even a few percentage points above this threshold compounds over 4.5 hours into devastating run fatigue. [Jacobs 2012: 83% FTP; McKenzie 2012: 80% FTP and still faded]

3. **Aerodynamics matter more than raw watts at Kona.** Recent years have seen athletes focused on aero efficiency outperform those with higher raw power: "With the increased emphasis on aerodynamics efficiency across the sport it will be hard for Kienle to repeat his dominating bike performance from 2014, where he gained 10+ minutes on many of the favorites." [Source: 2017 Kona Preview]

4. **70.3 tolerates higher relative intensity.** IF of 0.83-0.87 is standard; aggressive riders like Kanute (0.90 IF) can succeed if the swim gives them a cushion. The key is VI < 1.05. [Kanute 2017, Griffin 2014, Thomas 2014]

5. **Negative splits are a warning sign in Ironman, not a strategy.** Unlike running, a significant negative bike split (high first half, low second half) at Ironman distance almost always indicates overbiking, not closing speed.

6. **The wind is never your friend at Kona.** Athletes who expect a tailwind on the return and don't get one suffer mental fatigue on top of the physical cost. Plan for head/crosswind the entire day. [Source: 2016/2017 Kona analysis, Ryan Cooper]

7. **Power-to-run watts**: Stryd data shows well-paced Ironman runs maintain <10% power drop-off (Russell: 4%). Poorly paced runs drop 15%+ (Sanders: ~25%). Run training at zone 2 power builds specific fitness for "Efficiency Index" (speed per watt). [Source: Jim Vance, Run With Power]
