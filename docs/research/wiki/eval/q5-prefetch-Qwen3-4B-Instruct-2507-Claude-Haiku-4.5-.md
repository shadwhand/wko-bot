# Durability in Cycling: Distinction from FTP

## Core Definitions

### Durability
**Definition:** The ability to maintain power output after accumulated work. It is the decline of physiological characteristics (CP, VO2max, etc.) during prolonged exercise. [R] Maunder et al. (2021)

**Key distinction from FTP:** Durability measures *post-work retention*, while FTP measures *initial sustainable power*. An athlete can have high FTP but poor durability, or vice versa.

**Practical formulation:** Durability = percentage power retained after accumulating kJ of work. [E] WD-60

### FTP (Functional Threshold Power)
**Definition:** The highest sustainable power output for approximately 30-70 minutes (TTE). Represented by the inflection point in the Power-Duration Curve (PD curve). [R] ec-master-reference

**Key metric:** FTP is the baseline against which durability degradation is measured. If your fresh FTP is 300W, your durability tells you what percentage of 300W you can maintain after 50 kJ of work.

## The Fundamental Relationship

### Why Durability Matters for Performance
- **Race context:** In long events, the cumulative cost of early power expenditures becomes the dominant limiting factor. An athlete with better durability can sustain higher average power over the course of a race.
- **The "missing piece" narrative:** Coaches often claim durability is the missing factor for performance, but this requires sufficient absolute power first. [E] WD-60
- **Fatigue resistance:** The ability to resist fatigue and maintain performance; includes mental aspects. [R] Definitions paper

### The Van Erp (2021) Comparative Analysis
This cross-sectional study of World Tour cyclists reveals the correlation between power quality and fatigue resistance:

| Category | Starting Power | Drop at 50 kJ/kg | Source |
|----------|----------------|------------------|--------|
| **Successful sprinters** | 18.25 W/kg (10s) | ~8% |
| **Less successful sprinters** | 17.7 W/kg (10s) | ~18% |
| **Successful climbers** | 6.28 W/kg (20min) | ~4% |
| **Less successful climbers** | 5.99 W/kg (20min) | ~9% |

**Critical interpretation:** The better group starts with more power AND loses less. This is correlation, not causality. [R] WD-60

## Measurement: The kJ/kg Standard

### Why kJ/kg (not raw kJ)?
- **Normalization:** Raw kJ is misleading across body weights. A 2000 kJ workload is massive for a 55kg woman but "just getting warmed up" for a 75kg professional male. [E] WD-60
- **Standardization:** kJ/kg allows cross-athlete comparisons and is the unit used in all durability analysis. [R] van Erp et al. (2021)
