## VO2max, FTP, and Whether You Can Raise One Without the Other

**Short answer: Yes, you can raise FTP without raising VO2max — and it happens routinely.**

### The Relationship

FTP is not a fixed fraction of VO2max. It represents the highest metabolic rate you can sustain while maintaining lactate steady state, typically expressed as a percentage of VO2max. In trained cyclists this fraction ranges widely:

- **Untrained individuals:** FTP sits around 50-60% of VO2max.
- **Well-trained club riders:** 70-80% of VO2max.
- **Elite/professional road cyclists:** 80-90% of VO2max, with some grand tour climbers reportedly exceeding 90% (Lucia et al., 2000, *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise*).

Coyle et al. (1991, *Journal of Applied Physiology*) followed competitive cyclists over several years and showed that while VO2max remained stable or even declined slightly, power at lactate threshold continued to improve. The mechanism was a rightward shift of the lactate curve — more work done before lactate accumulation overwhelmed clearance.

### How FTP Rises Without VO2max Changing

Several physiological adaptations drive FTP upward independently of VO2max:

1. **Increased mitochondrial density and oxidative enzyme activity** — more efficient substrate use at a given oxygen delivery (Holloszy & Coyle, 1984).
2. **Improved lactate clearance** — enhanced monocarboxylate transporter (MCT1/MCT4) expression and greater capacity to oxidize lactate in adjacent muscle fibers and organs.
3. **Fiber-type shifts and capillarization** — greater capillary-to-fiber ratio improves O2 extraction without increasing cardiac output or VO2max.
4. **Improved gross efficiency** — watts per liter of O2 consumed increases with prolonged endurance training. Coyle (2005) documented efficiency differences of 3-5% between riders of similar VO2max, translating to 15-25 W at threshold.

### Practical Evidence

Joyner and Coyle (2008, *Journal of Physiology*) argued that endurance performance is best explained by the interaction of VO2max, fractional utilization (% of VO2max at threshold), and economy/efficiency. A cyclist with a VO2max of 65 ml/kg/min working at 90% utilization outperforms one at 75 ml/kg/min working at 72%.

WKO5 modeling supports this: mFTP can climb steadily across a season while modeled VO2max (from the power-duration curve) plateaus within weeks of returning to structured training.

### The Ceiling

VO2max does impose an absolute ceiling. You cannot push fractional utilization to 100%. Once a rider is already at 85-90% utilization, further FTP gains almost certainly require raising VO2max — which is why elite athletes periodize high-intensity blocks specifically targeting VO2max even when their primary goal is time-trial power.

### Bottom Line

For most trained cyclists, the fastest route to higher FTP is not more VO2max work — it is raising fractional utilization through sustained threshold and sweet-spot training. VO2max sets the roof; FTP is how much of that roof you actually use.
