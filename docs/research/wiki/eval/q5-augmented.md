# Q5 (Augmented): What is durability in cycling, and how is it different from just having a high FTP?

## Answer

Durability is the ability to maintain power output after accumulated work. The formal definition from Maunder et al. (2021) -- the first paper to use the term -- describes it as "the time of onset and magnitude of deterioration in physiological characteristics over time during prolonged exercise" [R] (durability-fatigue wiki, Section 1). Coaches have measured this since power meters existed in the late 1990s, calling it "fatigue resistance" or "stamina" long before the academic literature formalized it [E] (WD-60).

FTP, by contrast, is your maximal lactate steady state -- the highest power you can sustain for roughly 30-70 minutes in a fresh state [R] (ftp-threshold-testing wiki). It is a snapshot of your engine at full rest. Durability answers a different question: how much of that engine remains after hours of riding?

The distinction is concrete. Tim Cusick's WKO5 case study compares Amber Neben (world TT champion, 50 kg) with a Category 3 male rider (65 kg). Fresh 20-minute power is nearly identical -- 272W vs 274W. After 2000 kJ of work, Neben drops only 4W (1.5%), while the Cat 3 rider drops 32W (11.7%) (durability-fatigue wiki, Section 3). Same FTP, vastly different durability. That gap decides race outcomes in events lasting more than two hours.

Van Erp et al. (2021) quantified this across World Tour riders. Successful climbers started at 6.28 W/kg for 20 minutes and lost approximately 4% at 50 kJ/kg. Less successful climbers started at 5.99 W/kg and lost approximately 9% [R] (ep-durability-phenotype-insights, WD-60). The better group had both higher starting power and lower degradation -- the two are correlated, not independent.

This correlation leads to an important caveat: you cannot "durability" your way to a result you lack the engine for [E] (WD-60). An athlete who drops only 2% but starts at 4.5 W/kg still loses to one who drops 10% from 6.0 W/kg. The FTP threshold-testing wiki reinforces this: "FTP stagnation does NOT mean fitness stagnation" -- race results can improve through durability, repeatability, and race craft even with flat FTP [E] (TMT-70). But the reverse is also true: durability improvements without sufficient absolute power yield diminishing competitive returns.

The platform models durability as a dual exponential decay combining kJ-based fatigue (glycogen depletion, metabolic byproducts) and time-based fatigue (central fatigue, thermoregulation, postural load) (durability-fatigue wiki, Section 5). FTP anchors the fresh baseline; durability determines how far below that baseline you fall as the race progresses. Both metrics must be evaluated together -- the platform explicitly warns against showing durability in isolation (durability-fatigue wiki, Section 16).

**Bottom line:** FTP is your ceiling when fresh. Durability is how much of that ceiling you keep as the hours accumulate. Elite performance requires both.
