# What is durability in cycling, and how is it different from just having a high FTP?

Durability refers to a rider's ability to maintain their physiological capacities -- particularly threshold power and sub-threshold efficiency -- as exercise duration increases and fatigue accumulates. A rider with high durability experiences less performance degradation over time compared to a rider who may test well fresh but fades significantly in longer efforts.

## Why FTP alone is insufficient

FTP (Functional Threshold Power) is measured in a rested state, typically via a 20-minute or ramp test. It captures a single snapshot of sustainable power. But cycling events rarely unfold on fresh legs. A rider with a 300W FTP who can still hold 280W at threshold after four hours of riding is far more durable than a rider with 310W FTP who drops to 250W under the same fatigue.

## How durability is quantified

Researchers like Maarten Munten and those at the Cycling Science podcast have operationalized durability as the decline in power output at specific physiological markers (VT1, VT2, or critical power) relative to accumulated work or time. Common approaches include:

- **Power-duration curve shift**: Comparing your power-duration curve from the first hour of a ride to the last hour. A durable rider's curve shifts down less.
- **Decoupling (Pw:Hr)**: The ratio of power to heart rate over time. If heart rate rises while power drops, aerobic decoupling is high, indicating poor durability.
- **Lap-over-lap threshold testing**: Performing identical structured intervals at the start and end of a long ride and comparing power at the same RPE or heart rate.

## Physiological underpinnings

Durability is driven by factors distinct from raw threshold power: glycogen conservation and fat oxidation efficiency, mitochondrial density and resistance to metabolic byproduct accumulation, muscle fiber recruitment patterns under fatigue, and heat dissipation capacity. Years of high-volume endurance training preferentially develop these qualities, which is why grand tour riders often exhibit extraordinary durability even when their fresh FTP values are not the highest in the peloton.

## Practical example

Consider two riders preparing for a 200 km gran fondo with 3,000 m of climbing. Rider A has an FTP of 4.5 W/kg and Rider B has 4.2 W/kg. If the decisive climb arrives at km 170, Rider B -- if more durable -- may still operate at 95% of their fresh threshold (4.0 W/kg effective), while Rider A has decayed to 85% (3.8 W/kg effective). Rider B wins the race despite the lower lab number.

## Training implications

Building durability requires sustained aerobic volume, long rides with intensity in the final hours (progression runs), heat acclimation, and dialed nutrition strategy. It is not built by short, high-intensity blocks alone -- which is precisely why FTP-centric training can leave athletes underprepared for real-world racing demands.
