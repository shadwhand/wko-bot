Hydration & Electrolytes\n\n### Sweat Rate Estimation\n\n**Typical range for cycling:** 0.5–2.5 L/hr depending on intensity, temperature, humidity, body size, acclimatization.\n\n**Estimation formula (rough):**\n- `sweat_rate_L_hr = 0.5 + 0.013 * (temp_C - 15) + 0.004 * relative_humidity_pct + 0.3 * (intensity_fraction - 0.5)`\n- This is a simplified heuristic. Trying to match 100% is neither necessary nor practical.\n- **Acceptable deficit:** Up to 2–3% body mass loss does not impair cycling performance in most conditions (Goulet, 2011; Wall et al., 2015). The older \"2% threshold\" has been challenged.\n- **Hyponatremia risk:** Primarily from overdrinking (>sweat rate), not underdrinking. Slower riders in longer events are most at risk.\n- **Practical intake:** 400–800 mL/hr for most conditions.\n\n### Sodium\n\n- Sweat sodium concentration: **20–80 mmol/L** (highly individual, genetically determined, not meaningfully trainable). Average ~40–50 mmol/L.\n- Sodium loss: `sodium_mg_hr = sweat_rate_L_hr * sweat_Na_mmol_L * 23` (molar mass of Na)\n- At 1.0 L/hr sweat and 50 mmol/L: **1,150 mg Na/hr lost**.\n- Replacement: 300–1000 mg sodium/hr in race fluid, depending on sweat rate and concentration.\n- Most commercial drink mixes: 300–500 mg Na/L. For heavy/salty sweaters: 800–1500 mg Na/L or supplemental salt capsules.\n\n**Key references:**\n- Sawka MN et al.
