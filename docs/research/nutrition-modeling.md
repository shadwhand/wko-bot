prediction:\n\n```\nT_bonk = (G_muscle_0 − G_critical + Exo_CHO_rate × T_bonk × Sparing_factor) / Glycogen_use_rate(intensity)\n```\n\nSolving for T_bonk:\n\n```\nT_bonk = (G_muscle_0 − G_critical) / (Glycogen_use_rate − Exo_CHO_rate × Sparing_factor)\n```\n\nFor the reference cyclist at sweet spot (265 W), no feeding:\n- G_muscle_0 = 500 g, G_critical = 75 g, Glycogen_use_rate = 105 g/hr\n- T_bonk = (500 − 75) / 105 = 4.0 hours\n\nWith 80 g/hr glucose+fructose intake, sparing factor = 0.35:\n- T_bonk = (500 − 75) / (105 − 80 × 0.35) = 425 / 77 = 5.5 hours\n\nWith 120 g/hr intake:\n- T_bonk = 425 / (105 − 120 × 0.35) = 425 / 63 = 6.7 hours\n\n### Inputs needed\n\n- Current glycogen estimate (from depletion model)\n- Current and planned intensity\n- CHO ingestion rate and type\n- Blood glucose estimate (from liver model + absorption model)\n\n### Limitations\n\n- \"Bonking\" is a spectrum, not a binary event — some athletes experience gradual fadeout, others hit a wall\n- The glycogen depletion threshold varies by individual and by which fiber types are depleted\n- Psychological/motivational factors strongly modulate the performance response to low glycogen\n- Ketone/IMTG availability can buffer performance loss somewhat in fat-adapted athletes (but this is controversial and limited at high intensities)\n- The model cannot capture the heterogeneity of glycogen depletion across muscle groups (quads vs glutes vs calves)\n\n---\n\n## 6. Hydration Model\n\n### Sweat rate model\n\nSweat rate (L/hr) is a function of metabolic heat production, environmental conditions, and individual factors:\n\n```\nSR (L/hr) = (H_metabolic − H_convective − H_radiative − H_evap_resp) / H_vap\n```\n\nwhere H_vap = latent heat of vaporization of sweat ≈ 2426 kJ/L at skin temperature.\n\nA more practical empirical model:\n\n```\nSR = SR_base × f(power) × f(temperature) × f(humidity) × f(body_mass) × f(acclimation)\n```\n\n**Power factor:**\n```\nf(power) = 0.3 + 0.7 × (P / P_max)^0.8\n```\nMore power → more heat → more sweat. At rest, f ≈ 0.3; at FTP, f ≈ 0.85.\n\n**Temperature factor:**\n```\nf(temperature) = 0.5 + 0.5 × (T_ambient − 10) / 25    [clamp 0.5 to 1.5]\n```\nAt 10°C: f = 0.5; at 22°C: f = 0.74; at 35°C: f = 1.0; hot conditions can exceed 1.5.\n\n**Humidity factor:**\n```\nf(humidity) = 1.0 + 0.3 × (RH − 0.50) / 0.50          [clamp 0.85 to 1.30]\n```\nIn humid conditions, evaporative cooling is impaired, so the body sweats more (even though less evaporates). Note: this factor is somewhat counterintuitive — sweating rate may increase or stay high but *effective* cooling drops.\n\n**Body mass factor:**\n```\nf(body_mass) = BM / 75                                   [linear scaling]\n```\n\n**Acclimation factor:**\nHeat-acclimated athletes sweat more (and earlier), with more dilute sweat:\n```\nf(acclimation) = 1.0 (unacclimated) to 1.3 (fully heat acclimated, ~10-14 days)\n```\n\n**Reference values for the 78 kg cyclist at various conditions:**\n\n| Condition | Power | Temperature | Humidity | SR (L/hr) |\n|---|---|---|---|---|\n| Indoor trainer | 200 W | 22°C | 60% | 1.2-1.5 |\n| Temperate ride | 200 W | 20°C | 50% | 0.7-0.9 |\n| Hot outdoor | 200 W | 35°C | 40% | 1.0-1.5 |\n| Hot humid | 200 W | 33°C | 80% | 1.3-1.8 |\n| Race effort, hot | 280 W | 30°C | 50% | 1.5-2.0 |\n\n### Sodium loss model\n\n```\nNa_loss (mg/hr) = SR (L/hr) × Sweat_Na_concentration (mg/L)\n```\n\nSweat Na concentration ranges widely: 200-1500 mg/L (mean ~800-1000 mg/L). Heat acclimation reduces concentration by ~20-40%.\n\nFor the reference cyclist: ~800 mg/L × 1.0 L/hr = ~800 mg/hr Na loss at moderate effort in temperate conditions.\n\n### Performance degradation from dehydration\n\nThe relationship between body mass loss (from dehydration) and performance:\n\n```\nPF_hydration = 1.0                                        when BM_loss < 2%\nPF_hydration = 1.0 − k_dehy × (BM_loss% − 2%)           when 2% ≤ BM_loss < 5%\nPF_hydration = 1.0 − k_dehy × 3 − k_severe × (BM_loss% − 5%)   when BM_loss ≥ 5%\n```\n\nwhere k_dehy ≈ 0.03-0.05 per 1% BM loss (3-5% performance drop per 1% BM lost beyond 2%), k_severe ≈ 0.08-0.10 per 1% above 5%.\n\nHowever, this \"2% threshold\" model is increasingly contested. Recent work (Goulet, 2011; Wall et al., 2015) suggests:\n- In laboratory time-trials with ad libitum drinking, the relationship is more gradual\n- In real-world conditions (outdoor, airflow, self-paced), up to 3-4% loss may be tolerable with modest performance impact\n- The performance hit is strongly mediated by thermal strain — dehydration at 35°C is far worse than at 15°C\n\n**Temperature-modified dehydration model:**\n```\nPF_hydration = 1.0 − k_base × max(0, BM_loss% − threshold%) × T_modifier\nT_modifier = 0.5 + 0.5 × (T_ambient − 15) / 20         [clamp 0.5 to 1.5]\nthreshold% = 3.0 − T_modifier × 1.0                      [clamp 1.5 to 3.0]\n```\n\nThis captures that dehydration is much more harmful in hot conditions.\n\n### Fluid balance model\n\n```\nBM_loss(t) = ∫SR(t)dt − ∫Fluid_intake(t)dt − Metabolic_water_production(t) + Respiratory_water_loss(t)\n```\n\n- Metabolic water: ~0.13 L per hour of moderate exercise (byproduct of substrate oxidation; CHO oxidation produces ~0.6 g H2O per g CHO; fat produces ~1.07 g per g fat)\n- Respiratory water loss: ~0.1-0.3 L/hr depending on ventilation rate and ambient humidity\n\n### Inputs needed\n\n- Power (continuous)\n- Ambient temperature and humidity\n- Body mass\n- Fluid intake log (timing, volume)\n- Acclimation status\n- Optional: individual sweat rate from prior testing\n\n### Limitations\n\n- Individual sweat rates vary by 2-3x even at same conditions\n- Sweat sodium concentration is highly individual and requires a sweat test\n- The \"dehydration threshold\" for performance degradation is debated and context-dependent\n- Plasma volume shifts (from posture, exercise onset, carb intake) confound simple body mass tracking\n- Overhydration (hyponatremia) is a real risk with aggressive fluid intake and must be modeled as a floor on sodium concentration\n\n---\n\n## 7. Thermal Model\n\n### Heat balance equation\n\nThe fundamental thermal balance during exercise:\n\n```\ndT_core/dt = (H_metabolic − H_evap − H_convective − H_radiative − H_respiratory) / (BM × c_body)\n```\n\nwhere c_body ≈ 3.49 kJ/(kg·°C) (specific heat of human body).\n\n### Heat production\n\nFrom section 1:\n```\nH_metabolic (W) = P / GE × (1 − GE) = P × (1/GE − 1)\n```\n\nAt 290 W, GE = 0.225: H_metabolic ≈ 999 W\nAt 190 W, GE = 0.23: H_metabolic ≈ 636 W\n\n### Cooling mechanisms\n\n**Evaporative cooling (dominant mechanism):**\n```\nH_evap = SR_effective × H_vap / 3600\n       = SR_effective (L/hr) × 2426 (kJ/L) / 3.6 → in watts\n       = SR_effective × 674 W per L/hr\n```\n\nBut effective evaporation depends on humidity:\n```\nSR_effective = SR × (1 − RH × 0.8)     [simplified; full model uses wet-bulb temperature]\n```\n\nAt SR = 1.0 L/hr, 50% humidity: H_evap ≈ 674 × 1.0 × 0.60 = 404 W. This is often insufficient to balance H_metabolic at race intensities.\n\n**Convective cooling:**\n```\nH_convective = h_c × A_skin × (T_skin − T_ambient)\n```\nwhere h_c depends on air velocity:\n```\nh_c ≈ 8.3 × v_air^0.6    (W/m²/°C)\n```\nFor cycling at 35 km/h (9.7 m/s): h_c ≈ 8.3 × 9.7^0.6 ≈ 36 W/m²/°C\nA_skin ≈ 1.9 m² for 78 kg athlete (DuBois formula)\nT_skin ≈ 33-35°C\n\nAt 20°C ambient: H_convective ≈ 36 × 1.9 × (34 − 20) = 958 W → very significant. This is why outdoor cycling is thermally much easier than indoor training.\n\nAt 35°C ambient: H_convective ≈ 36 × 1.9 × (34 − 35) = −68 W → net heat GAIN (convection works against you in hot weather).\n\n**Radiative cooling:**\n```\nH_radiative = ε × σ × A_eff × (T_skin⁴ − T_environment⁴)\n```\nThis is usually small (50-100 W) and partially offset by solar radiation gain (~100-300 W in direct sun). For modeling, a net radiative term of −50 to +200 W covers most scenarios.\n\n**Respiratory cooling:**\n```\nH_respiratory ≈ VE × (c_air × (T_exhaled − T_inspired) + H_vap_resp × (W_exhaled − W_inspired))\n```\nTypically 30-80 W at exercise ventilation rates. Relatively minor.\n\n### Core temperature dynamics\n\nSolving the heat balance with typical values for the reference cyclist at 265 W (sweet spot) outdoors at 30°C, 50% humidity, 30 km/h:\n\n- H_metabolic ≈ 880 W\n- H_evap ≈ 400-500 W (depending on SR and humidity)\n- H_convective ≈ 200-300 W (depends on speed and T_ambient)\n- H_radiative ≈ −50 to +100 W\n- H_respiratory ≈ 40 W\n- Net heat storage ≈ 880 − 500 − 250 − 40 = ~90 W\n\n```\ndT_core/dt = 90 / (78 × 3490) = 0.00033 °C/s = 0.020 °C/min = 1.2 °C/hr\n```\n\nStarting from 37.0°C, reaching 39.0°C in ~100 min. At 39.5-40.0°C, central governor mechanisms begin limiting output.\n\n### Performance degradation from hyperthermia\n\n```\nPF_thermal = 1.0                                      when T_core < 38.5°C\nPF_thermal = 1.0 − k_therm × (T_core − 38.5)        when 38.5 ≤ T_core < 40.0\nPF_thermal = 0.3                                       when T_core ≥ 40.0\n```\n\nwhere k_therm ≈ 0.25-0.35 (25-35% performance drop per °C above 38.5°C).\n\n### Interaction with nutrition\n\nHeat stress increases CHO utilization (higher catecholamines → more glycogenolysis):\n```\nCHO_ox_heat = CHO_ox_thermoneutral × (1 + 0.15 × max(0, T_core − 38.0))\n```\n\nApproximately 15% increase in CHO oxidation rate per °C of core temperature above 38.0°C. This means hot conditions accelerate glycogen depletion and bonking.\n\nHeat stress also impairs gastric emptying and intestinal absorption (splanchnic blood flow reduced by up to 60-80% during hard exercise in the heat):\n```\nAbsorption_rate_heat = Absorption_rate_thermoneutral × (1 − 0.3 × max(0, T_core − 38.0))\n```\n\nThis creates a vicious circle: hot conditions increase CHO demand while reducing the ability to absorb exogenous CHO.\n\n### Inputs needed\n\n- Power\n- Ambient temperature, humidity, wind speed / cycling speed\n- Solar radiation (time of day, cloud cover, or direct measurement)\n- Body mass, body surface area\n- Clothing (affects evaporation and convection)\n- Acclimation status\n\n### Limitations\n\n- Core temperature is not directly measurable in the field (though ingestible pills exist)\n- The thermal model is highly sensitive to wind speed and evaporative conditions, which are hard to predict\n- Individual variation in heat tolerance is substantial\n- Indoor vs outdoor cycling has dramatically different thermal profiles (lack of convective cooling indoors makes it ~2x harder to cool)\n\n---\n\n## 8. Fatigue Interaction — Glycogen-Durability Coupling\n\n### The durability framework\n\n\"Durability\" in recent exercise science (Maunder et al., 2021; Van Erp et al., 2021) is defined as the resistance to deterioration of physiological parameters (critical power, FTP, VO2max, efficiency) over prolonged exercise. The durability decay curve for power at a given physiological threshold can be modeled as:\n\n```\nCP(t) = CP_0 × (1 − d × (t / τ_dur)^n)\n```\n\nwhere CP_0 = initial critical power, d = maximum fractional decline (0.05-0.15), τ_dur = time constant (3-6 hours), n = shape parameter (1-2).\n\nFor the reference cyclist:\n```\nCP(t) = 290 × (1 − 0.10 × (t / 4.5)^1.5)\n```\nPredicting: CP at 2 hr = 282 W, at 4 hr = 264 W, at 6 hr = 248 W.\n\n### Glycogen as a durability modulator\n\nThe central hypothesis: glycogen depletion is a primary *mechanism* of durability loss, not just a parallel process. Evidence:\n\n1. **Low glycogen impairs calcium release** from the sarcoplasmic reticulum (Ørtenblad et al., 2011, 2013). Glycogen is physically associated with the SR Ca²+ release channels, and depletion of this specific pool impairs excitation-contraction coupling even when other glycogen pools are not fully depleted. This is a peripheral fatigue mechanism.\n\n2. **Low glycogen increases central fatigue.** Brain serotonin synthesis increases (via free tryptophan competing with BCAAs for transport across the blood-brain barrier — the \"central fatigue hypothesis\" of Newsholme, 1987). Also, hypoglycemia directly impairs CNS motor drive.\n\n3. **Low glycogen reduces efficiency.** GE drops 2-4% when glycogen is depleted, because fat oxidation is less efficient per ATP and motor unit recruitment shifts to less efficient patterns.\n\n### Mathematical coupling\n\nThe durability decay should be modified by glycogen status:\n\n```\nCP(t) = CP_0 × PF_durability(t) × PF_glycogen(t) ×
