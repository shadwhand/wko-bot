## TMT #69: The Case for Riding Easier

### Key Claims

- **Endurance ride intensity floor of 50-65% FTP is ideal for most athletes** (experience-based, large coaching N)
  - Coaches check IF distribution as first diagnostic; floor at 0.70-0.75 is a red flag
  - Elite example: Cory's rides span IF 0.15-0.20 up to 0.90+; amateurs cluster too high
- **Hard workout success rate should be ~90%+ for cycling** (experience-based)
  - "1/3 good, 1/3 okay, 1/3 bad" model (from running) is far too high a failure rate for cycling
  - Frequent interval failures signal endurance rides are too hard
- **"Fatigue security blanket"** — athletes mistake chronic fatigue for productive training (experience-based)
  - Symptom: workout comments shift from "could do more" to "glad I survived"
  - Athletes resist riding easier due to sunk-cost fallacy around accumulated TSS
- **Endurance pace going up while FTP stagnates is NOT a positive sign** (experience-based)
  - It usually means the athlete is just pushing endurance rides harder, not that LT1 improved
  - True LT1 improvement requires ungodly volume at 40-50% FTP
- **Substrate choice (fat vs carb) is irrelevant to adaptation signal** (research-backed, ref: Watts Doc #40)
  - PGC-1alpha / mitochondrial biogenesis signals are energy-stress dependent, not substrate-dependent
  - "Fat max training" does not provide a better stimulus
- **No physiological switch at zone boundaries** — adaptations are a continuum (research-backed)
  - Excursions out of zone do NOT "ruin" the ride; metabolic/hormonal markers settle in minutes
- **Deep acute energy deficit from hard long rides worsens diet outcomes** (experience-based)
  - Sets up binge patterns; body is an "excellent energy accountant"
- **30-40 watt FTP gain observed in client just from riding endurance easier** (anecdotal, single case)

### Actionable Guidelines

| Guideline | Numbers | Module |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| Endurance ride IF target | 0.50-0.65 | zones, training_load |
| Recovery ride IF target | < 0.30 (heart rate double digits) | zones |
| Hard workout success rate threshold | >= 90% | training_load, blocks |
| RPE for endurance rides | 1-3 out of 10 | zones |
| Return-from-ride test | Feel same or better than when you left | training_load |
| Hill excursion cap during endurance | < 75% FTP (below tempo) | zones, pacing |
| Fueling: first hour | 50-60g carbs | clinical |
| Fueling: after first hour | 80-90g/hr depending on output | clinical |
| Volume matters more than intensity for endurance adaptations | Total hours > specific zone | durability |

### Conflicts with Conventional Wisdom

- **Zone 2 has nothing special about it** — total volume matters more than sitting in a specific zone
- **Higher endurance power != better stimulus** — a 6hr ride at 150W beats a failed 5hr at 200W
- **Endurance training does NOT kill sprint power** — riders with 1800W sprints train 20-25hr weeks; speed loss comes from riding endurance too hard, not from volume
- **LT1 varies from 45-85% FTP** — far wider than most zone models assume; affected by genetics, volume history, even whether riding indoors/outdoors

### Platform Relevance

- **zones**: Need flexible LT1 estimation, not fixed % of FTP; flag IF floors
- **training_load**: Track IF distribution over time; flag compressed spread
- **durability**: Volume accumulation tracking regardless of intensity zone
- **clinical**: Energy deficit estimation from ride calorie burn vs intake capacity
- **gap_analysis**: Hard workout success rate as diagnostic metric

## TMT #70: Fitness Beyond FTP

### Key Claims

- **FTP stagnation does not mean fitness stagnation** (experience-based)
  - Race results can improve substantially even with flat/declining FTP
  - Durability, repeatability, race craft, and specificity all matter independently
- **Specificity of efforts over duration is the most common workout prescription** (experience-based)
  - E.g., threshold intervals spread over 3-4 hours; goal is to nail the last intervals at same power as first
  - Failure in late intervals usually = fueling issue or over-ambitious wattage
- **Race-specific workout design is critical** — replicate course demands (experience-based)
  - Grade, cadence, position, and duration all matter beyond just power
  - Practice races/group rides within longer endurance rides are highly valued
- **Sprinting applies to all disciplines** and has low opportunity cost to practice (experience-based)
- **Strength training can unlock 1-min power PRs** especially for riders who rarely train >120% FTP (experience-based)
- **Gap analysis is the core coaching framework** — gap between current physiology/skills vs race demands (experience-based)
  - Athletes often misdiagnose their own weaknesses; data + race footage correct this
- **Opportunity cost must be weighed for every training decision** (experience-based)
  - 20 more watts of 1-min power vs 10 more watts of FTP — which matters more for YOUR racing?

### Actionable Guidelines

| Guideline | Detail | Module |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| Specific race simulation workout | Hard efforts + endurance base; nail last intervals at same watts as first | durability, blocks |
| Grade/cadence specificity | Practice on actual race climbs or equivalent grade | pacing |
| Sprint maintenance | Short sprints year-round, low fatigue cost | blocks |
| Race skill assessment | GoPro footage review; 5 things visible in 30 seconds | gap_analysis |
| Bike-specific training | Time on TT/MTB/CX bike; don't just train on road bike | gap_analysis |
| Warmup experimentation | Test hard vs easy warmups in off-season, not on race day | blocks |

### Conflicts with Conventional Wisdom

- **Race results, not FTP, are the ultimate metric** — being mono-metrically focused on FTP is an easy mistake
- **Switching power meter (single to dual sided) can show zero FTP gain even with massive race improvement**
- **Nutrition may matter more than interval design** for race performance in most amateurs

### Platform Relevance

- **durability**: Measure late-ride power retention vs early-ride; flag fueling as likely cause of decline
- **gap_analysis**: Compare power curve profile to race-specific demands; identify mismatches
- **pacing**: Course-specific power demand modeling
- **blocks**: Race-specific workout templates with mixed effort types

## TMT #72: Stimulus vs Recovery in Plateaus

### Key Claims

- **Performance plateaus are a dynamic equilibrium between stimulus and recovery** (experience-based + research parallel)
  - Inverted U: stimulus up to recovery capacity = gains; beyond = regression
  - "Stimulus + Rest = Performance" — rearrange for any unknown
- **Low-volume athletes CAN be recovery-limited, not just stimulus-limited** (experience-based, surprising finding)
  - Case: low-volume client took holiday, gained watts immediately on return
  - Life stress (job, kids, sleep loss) limits recovery more than training volume does
  - Getting up earlier to train literally cuts into recovery
- **Holiday recovery >> normal-life recovery** for most people (experience-based)
  - Reduced work stress, better sleep, better eating drive the difference
- **Quality training = progressive overload + successful completion of hard sessions** (experience-based)
  - Easy days must SET UP hard days, not be training stimuli themselves
  - Rest days are not training days
- **CTL/TSS chasing is counterproductive** (experience-based)
  - Athletes who doubled down on TSS when performance declined; resting fixed everything
  - "CTL is bullshit" is the wrong conclusion — it's that stimulus + rest = performance
- **Under-eating + weight loss can create short-term watts/kg spike followed by cliff** (experience-based)
  - Turning a log growth curve into an inverted U via chronic energy deficit
- **Compacting stimulus into shorter blocks + longer recovery works for experienced athletes** (experience-based)
  - Alternative to steady year-round loading

### Actionable Guidelines

| Guideline | Detail | Module |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| Diagnose plateau: stimulus or recovery? | Check life stress, sleep, not just training metrics | training_load, clinical |
| For low-volume riders: holiday test | If watts jump after time off, you were recovery-limited | training_load |
| High-intensity blocks | 2-3 weeks before rest week | blocks |
| Low-intensity/base blocks | 4-5 weeks before rest week | blocks |
| Sprint PRs after time off | Common; don't chase more sprint training if sprint is down late-season | blocks |
| Top-end power limiters | Muscle mass, strength, AND rest are all factors | gap_analysis |
| Diagnosing over-training | Performance declining despite high CTL = rest needed, not more stimulus | training_load |

### Conflicts with Conventional Wisdom

- **More training is NOT always better for low-volume riders** — the time-crunched athlete may need more rest, not more intervals
- **Top-end power can decline from accumulated fatigue, not from lack of sprint training**
- **CTL going up while performance goes down is a classic over-reaching signal, not a sign to train harder**

### Platform Relevance

- **training_load**: Need recovery capacity estimation; flag when CTL rises but performance declines
- **blocks**: Periodization rules for rest week timing based on block intensity
- **clinical**: Life stress / sleep quality as recovery modifiers
- **gap_analysis**: Distinguish "need more stimulus" vs "need more rest" via performance trend analysis

## TMT #64: Diagnosing and Training Weaknesses in the Off-Season

### Key Claims

- **"Train your weaknesses, race your strengths" is incomplete advice** (experience-based)
  - Must consider: trainability of weakness, opportunity cost, time available
  - Some weaknesses are genetic (sprint); diminishing returns on over-training them
- **Fatigue masks true abilities** — athletes misdiagnose weaknesses when chronically tired (experience-based)
  - "I can't sprint" often = "I'm always tired when I try to sprint"
  - Rider doing all-sprint training had terrible sprint because of chronic fatigue
  - Endurance training does NOT kill speed; riding endurance too hard does
- **Anaerobic capacity diagnosis via 1-min power** (experience-based)
  - Look at consistency across year, not just peak
  - Two peak performances at 600W but normally 450W = consistency problem, not capacity problem
  - Surge struggles in races may be skill/positioning, not anaerobic capacity
- **WKO5 power profile chart is useful** for benchmarking relative strengths across durations (tool recommendation)
- **Off-season is for low-opportunity-cost improvements** (experience-based)
  - Sleep, nutrition, blood panels (iron takes 3-4+ months to correct), warmup protocols, bike fit
  - Strength training: excellent off-season investment
  - Full build-and-taper to test entirely new training approach (high opportunity cost, reserve for when current approach isn't working)
- **Don't crack yourself mentally in off-season prep** (experience-based)
  - Burnout before season starts is real; preserve motivation

### Actionable Guidelines

| Guideline | Detail | Module |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| Sprint assessment | Must be done fresh; chronic fatigue invalidates results | gap_analysis |
| 1-min power consistency check | Compare peak vs typical across year | gap_analysis |
| Race footage review | Cheap GoPro; 5 actionable items in 30 seconds | gap_analysis |
| Iron panel timing | Check off-season; correction takes 3-4+ months | clinical |
| Strength training periodization | Off-season start; can unlock 1-min PRs | blocks |
| New training experiment | 2-month proof of concept with full build + taper | blocks |
| What you enjoy = probably your strength | Simple diagnostic heuristic | gap_analysis |

### Conflicts with Conventional Wisdom

- **Overtraining a weakness can make it worse** — dose matters; like medication
- **Repeatability problems may be race craft, not fitness** — cornering, positioning, pack skills
- **Off-season: don't diagnose weaknesses while fatigued from long season**

### Platform Relevance

- **gap_analysis**: Compare power curve shape to race demands; flag consistency issues in short-duration power; integrate race footage analysis concept
- **blocks**: Off-season block templates with strength training integration
- **clinical**: Blood panel reminders; iron timeline modeling

## TMT #68: Using Data in Coaching

### Key Claims

- **Coaching is a decision science / logical framework, not biochemistry** (experience-based)
  - Athletes want to know WHY, but the answer is a logical framework, not enzyme kinetics
  - The fundamental training programming framework has existed since the 1970s
- **You can get remarkably far with basics: FTP training block -> VO2max block -> threshold** (experience-based)
  - "People would be surprised how far you can get by being a good listener and sticking with basics"
- **Data doesn't inform you — you must have a question first** (experience-based)
  - WKO5 is the best power model available, but data in a vacuum tells nothing
  - Metrics are shortcuts to answer questions you already had
  - "We are not driven by metrics... the number is above threshold therefore we must do this" is wrong
- **IF over time is the first diagnostic metric** coaches check for new clients (experience-based)
  - Floor at 0.70-0.75 = "easy gains available" from riding easier
  - Wide IF spread = healthy training distribution
- **TSB/Form is widely misinterpreted** (experience-based)
  - Positive TSB does not mean peaked; negative TSB does not mean fatigued
  - "Best way to make TSB positive is to get sick"
  - Calling CTL "fitness" is specifically objected to
- **Mix-science: big flavor, low nutritional value** (opinion)
  - Training media temptation to make things look fancier/more scientific than needed
  - Coaches feel pressure to be "sciencey" — busy work in training plans
  - Arrow-gains fallacy: stacking 10% from one study + 15% from another does not compound
- **Compound score not particularly useful** — just as easy to build logical framework of strengths/weaknesses (opinion)
- **Scientific literature elucidates relationships between variables; it does not prescribe workouts** (experience-based)
  - Small N, methodology not standardized, results from early growth curve may not apply to trained athletes
  - Studies cannot capture long-term athlete management

### Actionable Guidelines

| Guideline | Detail | Module |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| First client diagnostic | Check IF distribution over time; look for compressed floor | training_load |
| Workout comment sentiment | Track shift from positive to survival-focused | training_load |
| Pattern detection in missed workouts | Clusters of skipped sessions = illness/overtraining signal | training_load |
| Season-over-season comparison | Compare training load and intensity at same calendar point | training_load |
| Planned workout success rate | Key quality metric | blocks |
| Subjective metrics (RPE, motivation, mood) | Critical data; not replaceable by power/HR | clinical |

### Conflicts with Conventional Wisdom

- **You don't need deep exercise physiology knowledge to coach well** — logical framework + good listening >> biochemistry
- **Training Stress Balance (TSB) is not "form"** — the label is misleading and leads to bad decisions
- **Compound score has not proved useful enough to persist** in the coaching community
- **Most training media over-sells the importance of specific interval protocols**

### Platform Relevance

- **training_load**: IF distribution analysis is the #1 diagnostic; build as first-class feature
- **training_load**: Missed workout pattern detection as overtraining signal
- **zones**: Do not over-prescribe zone precision; RPE is the gold standard for internal load
- **clinical**: Subjective metric tracking (motivation, sleep, mood) alongside power data

## Watts Doc #61: Quantifying Diminishing Returns

### Key Claims

- **Diminishing returns follow a logarithmic growth curve** (research-backed, N=14,690)
  - Steel et al. study: minimum dose resistance training, 1 session/week, 1 set to failure, 4-6 reps
  - Log-transformed time yields linear fit; untransformed = classic log/natural-log curve
  - Change point (knee) mode: 26-31 weeks; some individuals: 250+ weeks
- **Cycling data shows the same diminishing returns pattern** (experience-based, case series)
  - Client: 280 -> 325 -> 350 -> 375 -> 390 -> 400 FTP over 5 years (45W yr1, 10W yr5)
  - Within-season: same log growth curve pattern after off-season break
  - Linear R=0.93, growth model R=0.98
  - Multiple clients: ~25% improvement over baseline is common long-term ceiling
- **Early growth curve stimuli are non-specific; multiple adaptations overlap** (research-backed)
  - Sprint intervals and threshold training both raise VO2max early on
  - This cross-stimulation disappears as you move right on the growth curve
  - Therefore: study results from early growth may not transfer to well-trained athletes
- **Studies on trained athletes face severe detection problems** (research-backed)
  - Far right of growth curve: ~0.1%/week improvement; over 12 weeks = 1.2% change
  - Noise makes this nearly impossible to detect without massive sample sizes
  - R-squared values for physiology outcomes typically 0.15-0.40
- **Changing intervention rate of early growth != changing the asymptote** (speculative, strongly held)
  - A faster protocol may get you to the plateau sooner, not to a higher plateau
  - "No free lunch" — no evidence that short-term superior interventions raise long-term ceiling
- **Baseline fitness and rate of improvement are NOT necessarily correlated** (experience-based)
  - Some start high + improve slowly; some start low + improve fast then stop; some start low + improve steadily
- **After a layoff, athletes rejoin their long-term growth curve within weeks** (experience-based + research-backed)
  - Strength training literature: muscle/strength returns to pre-break levels quickly
  - Cycling: 3 months from couch to previous year's best FTP in mid-career athlete
- **Genetic plateau is a diagnosis of exclusion** (experience-based)
  - Must first exclude: insufficient stimulus, insufficient recovery, life stress, nutrition, sleep
  - Most amateurs are limited by stimulus AND recovery, not genetics

### Actionable Guidelines

| Guideline | Detail | Module |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| Plot your own growth curve | FTP per year, log-transform time, fit linear model | training_load |
| Reasonable improvement expectations | 5-10W/year for well-trained; 25-50W/year for newer riders | training_load |
| Post-layoff: expect quick return | 2-4 weeks back to recent levels; don't panic | training_load |
| When FTP gains arrive, shift to specificity | Don't keep hammering aerobic block; move to race prep | blocks |
| Set realistic season goals | Based on personal growth curve, not others' results | training_load |
| Diagnose plateau: exclusion method | Rule out recovery, nutrition, sleep, life stress before concluding genetic limit | clinical, gap_analysis |

### Conflicts with Conventional Wisdom

- **Short-term study results may only reflect rate of early adaptation, not long-term superiority**
- **You cannot stack gains from multiple studies** — arrow-gains fallacy
- **2 weeks off the bike is NOT the end of the world** — you rejoin your growth curve quickly
- **Sprint intervals and threshold training converge long-term** despite short-term protocol differences
- **FTP training alone hits a ceiling; VO2max work needed to raise it further** (well-known but precisely framed here with growth curve model)

### Platform Relevance

- **training_load**: Year-over-year FTP growth curve modeling; log-transform visualization
- **training_load**: Expected improvement rate calculator based on training age
- **blocks**: Trigger for switching from aerobic building to race specificity when growth flattens
- **gap_analysis**: Multi-variable performance attribution (don't just track FTP)

## Community Notes: TMT #54 (Junk Miles)

### Key Claims

- **"Junk miles" = any miles where fatigue cost exceeds benefit** (experience-based)
  - NOT defined by intensity zone; defined by outcome in context
- **Total training volume is a primary driver of endurance adaptation** (experience-based)
  - Commuter effect: athletes lost fitness when commuting stopped (even low intensity)
  - 25hr week at very low intensity > 20hr week at moderate intensity for aerobic adaptation
- **"Fatigue security blanket"** — riders at 70-75% FTP on easy days mistake chronic tiredness for progress (experience-based)
- **All roads lead to PGC-1alpha** — tempo, sweet spot, and FTP intervals all stimulate mitochondrial biogenesis similarly (research-backed)
- **"Periodize fun"** — mental health rides have tangible training value (experience-based)
- **True junk miles are rare**: unsustainable commute stress, unplanned hard efforts on rest days, blind compliance leading to burnout (experience-based)

### Platform Relevance

- **training_load**: Flag rides where fatigue cost (duration x intensity) is disproportionate to training goal
- **durability**: Volume accumulation tracking with credit for all intensities
- **zones**: Don't penalize low-intensity rides; they have real value

## Community Notes: TMT #55 (Rest Weeks / Subjective Metrics)

### Key Claims

- **Planned rest weeks: 3-on-1-off for high intensity; 4-5 weeks for base** (experience-based)
- **Reactive rest weeks triggered by: performance regression, loss of motivation, persistent fatigue, illness** (experience-based)
- **Subjective metrics are critical but require honesty** (experience-based)
  - Type-A athletes self-deceive; external accountability needed
  - Key indicators: motivation, mood/irritability (ask partner), RPE creep, sleep quality
  - Dissociate RPE: legs (peripheral) vs lungs (central)
- **"Train WITH data, not BY data"** — use power/HR to calibrate perception (experience-based)
- **When subjective feel conflicts with performance data, trust performance** (experience-based)
  - Feel great but power down = need rest (motivation overriding physiology)
- **Look for trends, not single data points** (experience-based)

### Actionable Guidelines

| Guideline | Detail | Module |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| Rest week frequency (high intensity) | Every 2-3 weeks | blocks |
| Rest week frequency (base/endurance) | Every 4-5 weeks | blocks |
| Reactive rest triggers | Performance decline, motivation drop, persistent fatigue, illness | training_load |
| RPE dissociation | Track legs vs lungs separately | clinical |
| Conflict resolution | Performance data > subjective feel when they diverge | training_load |

### Platform Relevance

- **blocks**: Automated rest week scheduling based on block intensity type
- **training_load**: Performance trend detection to trigger reactive rest recommendations
- **clinical**: Subjective wellness tracking with trend analysis (motivation, sleep, RPE creep)

## Cross-Cutting Themes

### 1. Intensity Distribution is the #1 Diagnostic
Every coaching episode returns to the same first check: **is the athlete's endurance riding too hard?** IF distribution over time with a floor analysis should be a first-class dashboard element.

### 2. Recovery is Undervalued, Stimulus is Overvalued
Across all episodes, the most common mistake is insufficient recovery — not insufficient training. The platform should model recovery capacity (incorporating life stress, sleep, nutrition) not just training load.

### 3. FTP is Necessary but Insufficient
FTP is one metric among many. The platform needs multi-dimensional fitness tracking: durability (late-ride power retention), repeatability (effort consistency within rides), top-end power, and race-specific demands.

### 4. Growth Curves and Realistic Expectations
Diminishing returns are real and quantifiable. The platform should model individual growth curves and set expectations accordingly — preventing both under-ambition and over-reaching.

### 5. Subjective Data Matters
RPE, motivation, mood, and sleep quality are not soft metrics — they are leading indicators of overtraining and under-recovery. The platform should integrate subjective tracking alongside power data.

### 6. Gap Analysis > Generic Prescription
The most valuable coaching framework is comparing current abilities to race demands. The platform should facilitate gap analysis across the power curve, not just FTP benchmarking.
