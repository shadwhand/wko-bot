## Episode 1: Perspectives #41 — Macros, Energy Needs, Nutrient Timing (w/ Tim Podlogar)

**Guest**: Tim Podlogar — Exercise physiologist, former performance nutritionist at Bora-Hansgrohe (Giro win), current nutrition consultant for Tudor Pro Cycling, lecturer at University of Exeter.

### Key Claims

- **Energy estimation is guesswork** [experience-based, research-backed]
  - Efficiency ranges from 20-25%; a 5% change yields ~900 kcal difference on a 4000 kJ day
  - Efficiency varies with intensity, can't be measured above 2nd threshold with lab equipment
  - Nutrition labels have 20% error allowed; absorption is 85-95%, not 100%
  - Doubly labeled water (DLW) studies in Grand Tours show 10-15% overestimation vs actual intake at energy balance
  - DLW results arrive weeks after the event — limited practical utility

- **Body is a good energy accountant, not a substrate accountant** [research-backed]
  - Total energy balance matters more than whether calories came from fat or carbs
  - Burning more carbs on the bike vs off the bike self-balances over 24h

- **Calibration takes 1-2 months** [experience-based]
  - Adjusting estimations until weight is stable and recovery is good
  - Young riders can be "black holes" — weight keeps dropping despite increasing intake
  - Experienced riders can be in 800-1000 kcal deficit with stable weight for weeks, then sudden drop

- **On-bike carb intake: 90g vs 120g/hr tradeoff** [research-backed, experience-based]
  - >67g/hr spares liver glycogen; no strong evidence on-bike carbs spare muscle glycogen
  - 120g/hr vs 90g/hr: you burn more carbs (less fat), end with same glycogen, but use 120g more of your daily carb budget
  - This can leave insufficient carbs for recovery, impairing next-day glycogen stores
  - Dose-response relationship: more post-exercise carbs = more glycogen next day

- **Carb absorption is individualizable** [research-backed]
  - Lab test with 13C tracer can personalize max absorption rate
  - Range: some absorb only 50g/hr, others up to 150g/hr
  - Gut training evidence for increasing absorption is currently weak

- **Protein needs for endurance athletes: ~2g/kg/day total body mass** [research-backed]
  - From Daniel Moore's lab tracer studies in runners
  - Scale up to 2.5-3g/kg if high-energy intake means lots of low-quality protein (pasta)
  - General recommendation of 1.4g/kg is likely too low for high-volume athletes

- **Fat minimum: ~0.8g/kg/day** [experience-based]
  - Can go lower on weight-loss days, prioritizing carbs
  - Grand Tour days: up to 1.5-2g/kg when energy expenditure is massive
  - No strong evidence low fat causes hormonal issues if glycogen/energy stores are maintained
  - Signs of insufficient fat: lethargy, low libido (similar to LEA)

- **Glycogen loading: 10-12g carbs/kg** [research-backed]
  - Rice/potato preferred over pasta (gluten causes bloating even in non-intolerant)
  - High-carb loading inadvertently increases sodium intake, causing water retention
  - Haribo/sweets preferred by pros: no gluten, no fiber, no extra sodium, no fat — just carbs

- **Electrolyte replacement during exercise is overemphasized** [research-backed]
  - No evidence you need to replace electrolytes during exercise <4 hours
  - Daily balance matters more than acute replacement
  - Sweat composition tests are nearly useless — composition changes with training state, intensity, diet, and day-to-day

- **Tart cherry juice: weak evidence, timing matters** [research-backed]
  - Must start 1 week before event, not just post-race
  - Mechanism unclear (polyphenols vs antioxidants)
  - Endurance athletes already have robust endogenous antioxidant systems

### Actionable Guidelines

| Parameter | Value | Context |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| On-bike carbs | 60-120+ g/hr | Scale to event importance; budget rest-of-day carbs |
| Liver glycogen sparing | >67 g/hr on bike | Threshold for meaningful sparing |
| Post-exercise carb repletion | 1.2 g/kg/hr for 4-5 hrs | Optimal glycogen resynthesis rate |
| Glycogen supercompensation | 10-12 g/kg carbs | Day before key event |
| Protein | 2-2.5 g/kg/day (total BW) | Endurance athletes; higher if low-quality sources dominate |
| Fat minimum | 0.8 g/kg/day | Can go lower on acute weight-loss days |
| Calibration period | 4-8 weeks | Before trusting energy balance estimates |
| Electrolytes during exercise | Not required <4 hrs | Daily sodium balance is what matters |

### Relevance to Platform

- **Nutrition module**: Energy budget calculator should model on-bike vs off-bike carb distribution, not just total intake. Must account for next-day glycogen implications of high on-bike intake.
- **Durability module**: Carb absorption rate is individual — platform could flag when prescribed intake exceeds likely absorption ceiling.
- **Clinical / LEA detection**: Weight trends alongside CTL/ATL and menstrual cycle (already in WKO5 dashboards). Signs of LEA are hard to detect acutely.

### Conflicts with Conventional Wisdom

- **"Replace the carbs you burned"** is wrong — you must replace total energy, not just carb substrate
- **Higher on-bike carbs is not always better** — 120g/hr can impair next-day readiness if recovery carb budget is squeezed
- **Sweat tests are nearly useless** for guiding electrolyte intake — too variable day-to-day
- **Doubly labeled water is not a practical gold standard** for individual athletes — 10-15% error, weeks for results

## Episode 2: Watts Doc #59 — A Muscle Glycogen Paradox

**Hosts**: Kolie Moore, Kyle Helsen

### Key Claims

- **Delayed post-exercise carbs impair next-day performance by ~30% despite identical glycogen levels** [research-backed]
  - Double-blind crossover, n=9 (8 complete), recreationally active men
  - Protocol: Day 1 = 10x2min @ ~94% ramp power (1min rest). Day 2 = max reps same protocol
  - Immediate carb group: avg 18 reps (range 16-22). Delayed carb group: avg 12-13 reps (range 10-15)
  - RPE ~2 units higher in delayed group
  - Diets fully controlled: 7g/kg carbs, 2g/kg protein, 1g/kg fat
  - Only difference: 2.4g/kg carbs immediately post-exercise vs zero-cal placebo (reversed 8hrs later)
  - Total daily calories identical between groups

- **Muscle glycogen returned to near-baseline in BOTH groups** [research-backed]
  - ~10% lower in delayed group = normal day-to-day variation
  - Not nearly enough to explain 30% performance decrease
  - Effect size for performance: 2.03 — massive

- **Recovery involves more than glycogen replenishment** [research-backed + speculation]
  - Possible mechanisms: liver glycogen depletion, gluconeogenesis/protein catabolism, acute energy deficit signaling
  - Tim Podlogar (cross-referenced in episode): agrees recovery processes were delayed, body was in catabolic state
  - Liver glycogen was not measured — likely a factor

- **The "glycogen window" is real but misunderstood** [research-backed]
  - For total muscle glycogen: the window is wide (24hrs is fine if total intake is sufficient)
  - For performance: immediate post-exercise fueling matters via non-glycogen pathways
  - Critical when next hard effort is <24 hours away (doubles, stage races, track meets)

- **Within-day energy deficits matter even at equal 24hr totals** [research-backed]
  - Literature shows: groups with same daily calories but larger hourly deficits have worse outcomes
  - Suppressed RMR group: largest hourly deficit ~3000 kcal vs ~1300 kcal in normal group
  - Associated with elevated cortisol, suppressed RMR, menstrual disruption

- **On-bike fueling does not spare muscle glycogen, only liver glycogen** [research-backed]
  - Confirmed by Podlogar in Perspectives 41 cross-reference

### Actionable Guidelines

| Scenario | Recommendation |
|----------|---------------|
| Back-to-back hard days | Eat immediately post-ride — do not delay >1 hour |
| Doubles (two-a-days) | Post-session fueling is critical; even gels immediately are better than waiting |
| Single hard day, next hard day 3-5 days away | Less critical; total daily intake matters more |
| Dieting / intentional deficit | Do NOT stack hard days back-to-back; fuel on-bike, create deficit elsewhere |
| Sleep disruption from hunger | Sign of inadequate fueling — eat (even crackers at 2am) |

### Relevance to Platform

- **Durability module**: This is direct evidence that recovery quality (not just glycogen quantity) determines next-day capacity. A "recovery quality" metric could flag days where post-ride fueling was likely delayed or insufficient based on ride end time vs next meal.
- **Pacing / race planning**: For multi-day events, model the carb distribution across days — not just total intake.
- **Nutrition module**: Implement within-day energy deficit tracking — flag prolonged fasted periods after hard sessions.

### Conflicts with Conventional Wisdom

- **"Just hit your daily macros"** is insufficient — timing distribution within the day matters for performance
- **"Glycogen window is a myth"** is an overcorrection — the window IS real for performance, just not for glycogen quantity
- **Muscle glycogen level alone is a poor predictor of next-day performance** — something else (likely systemic recovery state) matters more

## Episode 3: Watts Doc #58 — Creatine's Actual Effects on Cycling Performance

**Hosts**: Kolie Moore, Kyle Helsen

### Key Claims

- **Creatine has ZERO effect on aerobic/endurance performance in trained athletes** [research-backed, meta-analysis]
  - 2023 meta-analysis: 277 total participants, well-trained
  - Effect size: -0.07 (95% CI: -0.27 to +0.13) — brackets zero, no effect
  - Applies to VO2max efforts, time trials, threshold-and-above sustained work
  - Two studies in elite rowers: no body mass change from creatine

- **Creatine shows a small positive effect on repeated sprint mean power** [research-backed, meta-analysis, uncertain]
  - 2022 meta: 14 studies, sprints <10sec with >90sec rest
  - Effect size for mean power: 0.61 (real units: ~27 watts on ~1000W sprint)
  - BUT: confidence intervals are wide, data is scattered, only 3 cycling studies
  - No effect on peak power or fatigue rate

- **Body mass gain is the largest, most reliable effect of creatine** [research-backed]
  - Effect size: 0.79 (huge by exercise phys standards)
  - Average: ~0.8-1.0 kg; some individuals gain 2+ kg
  - Individual variation: some gain 1 lb, others 5 lbs regardless of body size
  - This is water, not contractile tissue (short-term)

- **Weight gain may negate sprint power gains on a W/kg basis** [research-backed + analysis]
  - 1000W sprint at 70kg = 14.3 W/kg
  - +30W at 71kg = 14.5 W/kg (marginal gain)
  - +30W at 72kg = 14.3 W/kg (no net gain)
  - For road cycling with climbing: net negative

- **Simulated bike race study (2010): no performance benefit, +2kg** [research-backed]
  - 2hr @ 60% VO2peak with sprints every 15min, final sprint to exhaustion
  - Both creatine and placebo groups improved equally on final sprint time (60s -> 85s)
  - Creatine group gained 2kg; no change in absolute VO2max

- **Cognitive benefits: preliminary, small, needs replication** [research-backed, limited]
  - One well-designed study (n=15, double-blind crossover): sleep-deprived subjects
  - Creatine group completed cognitive tasks ~1-2 sec faster per question at 4am
  - NOT the "creatine makes you not tired" claim circulating on social media
  - Older adults (>60): larger effect sizes (~0.88) in meta of 2 studies
  - Confounders not controlled: water intake, snacking habits

- **Women do NOT have 70-80% less endogenous creatine** [research-backed, myth-busting]
  - Viral claim from MDPI paper that misreferenced its source
  - Actual finding: women's daily total body creatine synthesis rates are 70-80% of men's
  - Likely explained by muscle mass differences, activity levels
  - No differences in creatine supplementation effectiveness between sexes in subgroup analyses

- **Loading protocol is optional** [research-backed]
  - Standard: 4x5g/day for 5-7 days (DO NOT take 20g at once — GI distress)
  - Alternative: 3-5g/day indefinitely; reaches saturation in ~3-4 weeks
  - No meaningful difference in long-term outcomes

### Actionable Guidelines

| Athlete Type | Creatine Recommendation | Rationale |
|-------------|------------------------|-----------|
| Road racer (climbing) | **No** | Weight gain negates any sprint benefit; no aerobic benefit |
| Road racer (flat/sprinter) | **Maybe** — try it, assess weight gain | Small sprint power gain; if weight gain is minimal, net positive possible |
| Endurance track sprinter (gym-heavy) | **Yes** | Supports gym work, which feeds sprint power long-term |
| Track sprinter (no gym) | **No** | Without gym stimulus, creatine adds only water weight |
| Mountain biker XC / CX | **No** | W/kg matters; no aerobic benefit |
| Downhill / Enduro | **Yes** | Mass helps; gravity is your friend; good starts matter |
| Ultra-endurance | **Consider for cognition** | Sleep deprivation decision-making may improve |

| Practical Detail | Guideline |
|-----------------|-----------|
| Dosing | 3-5g/day, no loading needed unless time-constrained |
| Timing | Mix in yogurt, shake, hot drink — doesn't matter |
| Duration | Continuous; stop 1-2 weeks pre-body-comp scan if desired |
| Side effects | GI distress if dose too high at once; weight gain is reversible |
| Contamination risk | Use third-party tested product; keep lot numbers |
| Stopping | Strength/muscle gains persist; only water weight is lost |

### Relevance to Platform

- **Nutrition module**: Creatine is irrelevant to endurance performance modeling — do not factor it into power predictions
- **Sprint module**: If modeling repeated sprint capacity, creatine's effect is within power meter measurement error (~2-3%). Not worth modeling.
- **Body composition tracking**: Flag unexplained 1-2kg jumps when athlete starts creatine — annotate as water weight, not fat gain
- **Supplement tracker** (if built): Creatine = low priority for cyclists; caffeine and carbs remain the big movers

### Conflicts with Conventional Wisdom

- **"Creatine is a game-changer for all athletes"** — No. Zero effect on aerobic performance. Marginal at best for cycling sprints.
- **"Women need more creatine"** — Based on a misreferenced MDPI paper. No evidence of differential effectiveness.
- **"Creatine helps recovery"** — No evidence found by Kolie Moore in the literature.
- **"You must load creatine"** — Not necessary; 3-5g/day reaches saturation in a few weeks.

## Cross-Reference: Community Notes WD-54 — Glycogen's Effects on AMPK

### Key Claims

- **AMPK is both an energy sensor (AMP) AND a glycogen sensor** [research-backed, in-vitro]
  - 2009 paper: AMPK beta subunit has a glycogen-binding domain (GBD)
  - Glycogen physically binds AMPK, sequestering and inhibiting it
  - It's not that low glycogen activates AMPK — it's that high glycogen suppresses it
  - This inhibition is independent of AMP/ATP status

- **"Train low" strategies do NOT improve performance despite higher AMPK activation** [research-backed]
  - In-vitro mechanism does not translate to in-vivo performance gains
  - Low glycogen training increases fat oxidation but impairs high-intensity training capacity
  - Delayed feeding impairs recovery, disrupts metabolic health, decreases next-day performance

- **Mechanism is not outcome** [epistemological]
  - Discovering a molecular pathway != validating a training intervention
  - Correlation (ice cream sales and shark attacks analogy)

### Relevance to Platform

- **Training prescription**: Do NOT build "train low" recommendations into the platform. The molecular evidence is real but the performance evidence goes against it.
- **Durability module**: Glycogen depletion during rides should be modeled as a cost to recovery, not a training stimulus.

## Cross-Reference: Community Notes TMT-50 — Fat and Carb Burning Myths

### Key Claims (3 myths debunked)

- **Myth 1: "Only replace carbs burned on ride"** [research-backed]
  - A 5hr/3500 kcal ride creates a massive deficit even if all carb calories are replaced
  - 3500 kcal deficit = ~1 lb fat equivalent, 30-60x the recommended hourly fat loss rate
  - This acute deficit triggers: metabolic disturbance, impaired recovery, hormonal suppression (thyroid, testosterone, IGF-1)
  - Delaying post-ride meal by even a few hours measurably impairs next-day performance

- **Myth 2: "Burn more fat = better weight loss"** [research-backed]
  - Body fat is an active endocrine organ (leptin, ghrelin signaling)
  - Acute deficit → leptin crash → ravenous hunger for days, metabolic slowdown
  - Minnesota Starvation Experiment as extreme example
  - Successful fat loss = small, consistent, sustainable deficit

- **Myth 3: "TDEE = BMR + bike kJ"** [research-backed]
  - Ignores TEF (~10% of intake) and NEAT (substantial for active people)
  - Tour de France DLW studies: off-bike expenditure = 1.6-2.3x predicted BMR
  - Athletes chronically underestimate energy needs using this formula

### Actionable Guidelines

- Prioritize total energy balance over substrate tracking
- Fuel rides to minimize acute deficit, not to "burn fat"
- TDEE for athletes is significantly higher than BMR + bike kJ

### Relevance to Platform

- **Energy balance dashboard**: Must model off-bike expenditure, not just ride kJ. The 1.6-2.3x BMR multiplier for off-bike metabolism is a key finding.
- **LEA risk scoring**: Track within-day energy deficit patterns, not just daily totals.

## Synthesis: Top Platform-Relevant Takeaways

1. **Carb distribution > carb total**: The platform should model how on-bike carb intake affects the recovery carb budget. Higher on-bike intake is not always better if it squeezes post-ride replenishment below ~1.2g/kg/hr.

2. **Recovery is not just glycogen**: Next-day performance depends on immediate post-exercise fueling even when 24hr glycogen levels equalize. A "recovery quality" flag based on post-ride fueling timing would be valuable.

3. **Within-day energy deficit is a distinct risk**: Even at adequate daily totals, prolonged intra-day deficits impair performance and health markers. Platform should track hourly energy balance where data is available.

4. **Creatine is noise for endurance cycling**: Zero aerobic effect, marginal sprint effect within power meter error, reliable weight gain. Not worth modeling in power prediction. Worth noting in supplement tracking as "body composition annotation."

5. **Absorption ceiling is individual**: Carb absorption ranges from 50-150g/hr. Platform fueling recommendations should cap at ~90g/hr unless athlete has lab-tested higher ceiling.

6. **"Train low" is a molecular dead end for performance**: Despite compelling AMPK mechanism, performance evidence consistently negative. Platform should never recommend glycogen depletion as a training stimulus.

7. **Energy estimation error is massive**: ~900 kcal swing from efficiency assumptions alone; nutrition labels 20% error; absorption losses. Platform calorie estimates should display confidence intervals, not point estimates.
