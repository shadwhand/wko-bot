# TMT #75: The Art of Autoregulation (Training To Vibes)

**Date:** March 26, 2026
**Duration:** 1:20
**Hosts:** Kolie Moore, James, Gita Minas
**Topic:** Coaching athletes to autoregulate training -- making choices between structured and unstructured training, hard work and fun, intensity and rest.

Evidence levels: **[R]** = Research-backed, **[E]** = Experience-based, **[O]** = Opinion.

---

## Key Claims

| # | Claim | Evidence | Source |
|---|-------|----------|--------|
| 1 | Autoregulation is a spectrum: most athletes should operate somewhere between 25% and 75% vibes-based training, not 0% or 100% | [E] | Moore, Minas |
| 2 | Prefab/canned plans are like long-range weather forecasts -- accuracy degrades the further out you plan; coaches working 1-2 weeks ahead have tighter "forecast windows" than 12-16 week plans | [E] | Moore |
| 3 | To break rules effectively, you must first understand them -- autoregulation works best when you know the trade-offs of deviating from structure | [E] | Minas |
| 4 | Short FTP efforts (3-5 min at threshold) are an excellent low-fatigue readiness gauge -- if FTP feels unusually hard for those few minutes, you need more rest | [E] | Moore, James |
| 5 | Short sprints serve as a neural drive barometer -- if the motor pattern feels foreign or numbers are well below baseline (e.g., seated peak <1600W when normal is 1700-1800W), skip the hard workout | [E] | Moore |
| 6 | Training priorities should be ranked (primary, secondary, tertiary) so that when autoregulating, the most important stimulus is never skipped in favor of fun | [E] | Moore, Minas |
| 7 | For ultra-endurance athletes, at least one 6+ hour ride per month is "almost non-negotiable" specificity -- FTP work and sweet spot help but cannot replace long ride stimulus | [E] | Minas |
| 8 | Diminishing returns on ultra-long training rides: a 6-7 hour ride captures most of the adaptation; 10+ hours adds disproportionate fatigue relative to new learning, except for discovering edge-case weak points (e.g., knee pain at hour 25) | [E] | Moore, Minas |
| 9 | Suggestion bias is real: athletes conflate what they expect to feel with what they actually feel -- objective short tests (FTP check, sprint) ground self-perception in reality | [E] | Minas |
| 10 | "Rest inertia" -- by end of a rest week, motivation vanishes and athletes feel they could quit cycling forever; this resolves within 1-2 rides and is not a signal to extend rest | [E] | Moore |
| 11 | Anticipated recovery environment determines whether to use "good legs" -- if stress is high and recovery will be poor in the next few days, you may only get one day of good legs; deploy them on the most important session | [E] | Moore |
| 12 | Undercooking training by 5-10% or even 20% is far better than overcooking by 10% -- the recovery hole from overcooking is disproportionately deep | [E] | Moore |
| 13 | "When in doubt, sit it out" -- skipping a hard workout has near-zero downside; the net gain is always in the recovery | [E] | James |
| 14 | 100% structured plan compliance is a warning sign, not a badge of honor -- less compliance (with good autoregulation) is explicitly preferred by EC coaches | [E] | Moore |
| 15 | Athletes under high life stress can still see fitness gains months later from training banked during low-stress periods -- fitness is not lost as quickly as people fear | [E] | Moore |
| 16 | People genuinely shocked at how little structured training is needed for meaningful long-term progress | [E] | Moore |
| 17 | Vibes-based training can still have a clear session purpose: "give me 60 minutes at FTP using any combination of intervals" is valid structure -- 3x20 in erg mode is not meaningfully different for practical purposes | [E] | Minas |
| 18 | Danger of vibes training without purpose: doing a bit of everything all the time (group ride / race simulation style) provides variety but limited long-term progression | [E] | James |
| 19 | Highly motivated athletes are at risk with vibes training (will overdo it); low-motivation athletes also at risk (will underdo it) -- extremes of motivation disposition require more structure | [E] | James |
| 20 | For threshold workouts, RPE will creep up and that is normal -- hold power steady; do not drop power to chase a heart rate zone or constant RPE | [E] | Moore |
| 21 | For VO2max efforts, breathing and effort are the primary indicators, not power -- power will drop slightly across reps and that is acceptable as long as the athlete is pushing hard | [E] | Moore |
| 22 | For anaerobic/sprint efforts, quality matters most -- if RPE drops (cannot push hard anymore), that is the signal to stop the session | [E] | Moore |
| 23 | RPE ceiling per workout type: threshold rarely needs to exceed 8/10; going to 10/10 at threshold is usually unnecessary stimulus for well-trained athletes | [E] | Moore |
| 24 | Outdoor workout precision: within 10W of target at threshold is "definitely close enough"; do not expect indoor erg-mode precision outdoors | [E] | Minas |
| 25 | Performance trending down and attributed to "vibes training" is not acceptable -- objective metrics must show maintenance or improvement; regression is a red flag regardless of training philosophy | [E] | Minas |
| 26 | Substituting sweet spot or tempo for threshold when tired is acceptable in-season maintenance or early return from illness, but during build phase most workouts should be high quality and progressive | [E] | James, Moore |
| 27 | Tim Cusick's suggestion (cited by Moore): getting a coach for 1-3 years to learn the training rhythm before self-coaching is excellent preparation for successful autoregulation | [E] | Moore, Cusick |
| 28 | Eating more can directly fix low motivation and slow-to-start problems -- time to get ready for a ride correlates with recent nutrition quality | [E] | Minas |
| 29 | Tracking mood and motivation metrics in TrainingPeaks is one of the most valuable data points for coach and athlete -- tied to overreaching and underfueling | [E] | James |
| 30 | The "fatigue security blanket" -- athletes maintain fatigue because it feels like they are doing everything they could; accepting that skipping a hard workout is correct is a sign of training maturity | [E] | Moore, Minas |

## Actionable Guidelines

### Autoregulation Decision Framework

| Situation | Action | Evidence |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| Feeling uncertain before a workout | Warm up, do 3-5 min at FTP + a few sprints; use numbers and feel to decide | [E] TMT-75 |
| FTP check feels unusually hard | Skip the hard workout; ride easy or take the day off | [E] TMT-75 |
| Sprint numbers well below personal baseline | Neural fatigue -- rest day or easy spin only | [E] TMT-75 |
| Good legs but high life stress + poor upcoming recovery | Deploy good legs on the most important session only; protect the key workout later in the week | [E] TMT-75 |
| Tired off the bike but legs OK (acute, 1-day) | Fine to use them; monitor next day | [E] TMT-75 |
| Tired off the bike chronically (1+ week) | Address root cause: sleep, nutrition, life stress; do not push through | [E] TMT-75 |
| Rest inertia at end of rest week | Normal; get on the bike and see what happens -- usually resolves in 1-2 rides | [E] TMT-75 |
| Missing sessions mid-week, Saturday has good legs | Autoregulate which of 2-3 planned sessions to do based on what was missed | [E] TMT-75 |
| Training camp (e.g., Mallorca trip) | Provide guardrails (intensity cap, duration guidance, altitude HR ceiling) but no rigid plan; treat like cyclocross season for recovery planning | [E] TMT-75 |
| Coming back from illness | Day-by-day planning only; low bar for success; ramp slowly; do not set 6-week plan | [E] TMT-75 |

### Vibes-Based Workout Prescriptions

| Workout Type | Vibes Prescription | Guardrails |
|-------------|-------------------|------------|
| Threshold | "Give me ~60 min at FTP, any combination, 10 min minimum intervals" | Power within 10W of target |
| VO2max | "Hard hill repeats, 2-8 min, track time up the hill for progress" | Minimum 3 reps, push hard, track numbers |
| Group ride (specificity) | "Sit in and contest the town line sprints" or "attack early and hold it" | Clear single goal per ride |
| Group ride (endurance) | "Sit in, do not work on the front, recover" | Stay social, resist urge to attack |
| Endurance long ride | "Get in 6+ hours; eat well; feel the same at end as start" | Fuel 60-90 g/hr; IF < 0.65 |
| Recovery / unsure day | "Swing a leg over and see what you have" | No obligation to continue if bad |

### Progress Tracking for Less-Structured Training

| Method | What It Measures | Minimum Frequency |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Time up a benchmark hill | Power/fitness trend | Weekly |
| Time in zone per ride | Stimulus accumulation | Every hard ride |
| Short FTP check (3-5 min) | Recovery / readiness | 2-3x per week during hard blocks |
| Sprint peak power trend | Neural drive / freshness | Weekly |
| RPE at constant power | Fatigue accumulation | Every workout |
| Mood + motivation tracking (TP metrics) | Overreaching / underfueling | Daily |

## Who Benefits Most from Vibes Training

| Profile | Vibes % | Rationale |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| Unpredictable schedules (medical, shift work, oil rigs) | 75%+ | Cannot adhere to fixed plan; autoregulation is the only viable approach |
| Parents with newborns | 75%+ | Sleep-deprived; day-by-day is the only realistic window |
| Multi-sport athletes (cycling + skiing, climbing, gym) | 50-75% | Cross-training demands prevent rigid cycling structure |
| Athletes who feel constrained by plans | 50-75% | Psychological cost of rigid structure undermines consistency |
| High life stress periods | 50-75% | Recovery environment is poor; rigid plans lead to failure and guilt |
| Experienced athletes with deep training history | 50-75% | In touch with legs; can read signals well enough to self-regulate |
| New to structured training | 25% | Need to learn the rules before breaking them; objective metrics essential |
| Highly motivated / Type A | 25% | Will overdo fun days; need structure to hold them back |
| Low motivation | 25% | Will underdo it; need structure to ensure minimum dose |
| Close to A-race / peaking | 25% | Precision matters; cannot afford to waste good legs on group rides |

### New Detection Rules from TMT-75

| Signal | Detection | Action |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| Performance regression attributed to vibes training | Power trends declining over 3+ weeks | AMBER FLAG -- vibes is not an excuse for declining fitness |
| Mood/motivation declining for 1+ week | Self-report metrics trending down | AMBER FLAG -- check nutrition, sleep, life stress before adjusting training |
| No 6+ hour ride in 8+ weeks (ultra-endurance athlete) | Ride duration audit | Specificity gap alert for ultra events |
| Sprint peak power trending down for 2+ weeks | 5-15 sec peak power trend | Neural fatigue flag -- likely needs rest, not more sprint training |
| Rest inertia > 3 days without improvement | Post-rest-week readiness check | Normal if 1-2 days; flag if extending beyond 3 days as potential deeper issue |

## Conflicts with Conventional Wisdom

| Conventional Belief | TMT-75 Position | Evidence |
|--------------------|-----------------|----------|
| "100% plan adherence = dedication" | Less compliance with intelligent autoregulation is preferred; coaches explicitly request less compliance | [E] Moore |
| "More structured = more progress" | Some athletes are shocked at how little structure is needed for meaningful gains; the better trained you are, the more you should trust vibes | [E] Moore |
| "Group rides are junk miles" | Never junk; can provide race-specific stimulus (stochasticity, pack dynamics, motivation); may be the most specific training for some race demands | [E] Moore |
| "If I skip a hard workout, I lose fitness" | Near-zero downside to skipping; net gain is always in recovery; "never regretted skipping a hard workout" | [E] James |
| "Vibes training means no structure" | Best vibes training still has session purpose, priority ranking, and objective progress metrics; it is structured flexibility, not chaos | [E] All |
| "You need a rigid plan to improve" | Plans are written in sand; the tighter the "forecast window" the better; day-of autoregulation beats 12-week prefab plans | [E] Moore |
| "Undercooking is wasting potential" | 5-20% undercooking is far better than 10% overcooking; the recovery hole from overcooking is disproportionately deep | [E] Moore |
| "FTP should always be done as structured intervals" | "Give me 60 min at FTP any combination" is practically equivalent to 3x20 erg mode; ride the mountain, do not turn around after 1200 seconds | [E] Minas |
| "Heart rate zone should guide threshold effort" | Hold power steady; let RPE and HR creep; do not drop power to stay in an HR zone | [E] Moore |
| "Training on vibes is for beginners" | The most experienced athletes should be the best at autoregulation; deep training history improves signal reading | [E] Moore |
