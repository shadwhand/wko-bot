## 1M: AMA (1-Million Listens Q&A)

### Key Claims

| Claim | Evidence Level |
|-------|---------------|
| Lowest viable endurance intensity is ~40% FTP; 50% FTP reliably captures all endurance benefits | Experience-based (coaching N>50) |
| Extra kJ from slightly harder endurance riding makes no meaningful fitness difference vs. total time | Experience-based |
| A sustainable fat-loss rate is 0.5--1.0 lb/week; anything faster triggers metabolic compensation | Research-backed |
| Insufficient energy availability impairs adaptation regardless of training stimulus quality | Research-backed |
| Ramp test FTP varies from 55--90% of final-minute power depending on individual anaerobic capacity | Experience-based + research |

### Actionable Guidelines

- **Endurance floor**: riding as low as 40% FTP still yields normal aerobic progress when combined with intervals on top
- **Weight loss ceiling**: ~0.5--1.0 lb/week maximum for athletes; larger deficits impair adaptation
- **Ramp test**: never use a single fixed percentage (e.g., 75%) -- individual anaerobic capacity creates a 55--90% range

## Perspectives #37: Practical Performance Psychology with Billy Ryan

### Key Claims

| Claim | Evidence Level |
|-------|---------------|
| ACT (Acceptance & Commitment Therapy) outperforms CBT-style "positive thinking" for athletic performance | Research-backed (clinical + sport psych literature) |
| Confidence is a byproduct of competent action, not a prerequisite; chasing confidence is counterproductive | Research-backed |
| Psychological flexibility (ability to act on values despite discomfort) predicts performance better than emotional state | Research-backed |
| Suppressing negative thoughts increases their intensity ("beach ball effect") | Research-backed |
| "Rigid flexibility" -- athletes who tolerate extreme physical pain but cannot tolerate emotional pain -- leads to poor decisions like training through injury | Experience-based |

### Actionable Guidelines

- **Tandem bicycle metaphor**: mind chatters in the back seat; you steer from the front -- acknowledge thoughts without obeying them
- **TEAM framework**: Treat Emotions As Messengers -- ask what the emotion is signaling, not why it is there
- **Drop the rope**: when in a mental tug-of-war with negative thoughts, stop engaging rather than fighting harder
- **Five senses grounding**: "I am seeing/hearing/thinking/feeling" shifts from being IN the experience to OBSERVING it
- **Confidence protocol**: act AS IF confident (in line with values) -- the feeling may follow, but is not required
- **DNAV for youth**: Discoverer, Noticer, Advisor, Valuer framework for teaching psychological flexibility

### Platform Relevance

| Module | Insight |
|--------|---------|
| `clinical` | Psychological flexibility assessment could flag athletes at risk of injury from rigid-flexibility pattern |
| `training_load` | Emotional/psychological stress should be tracked alongside physical TSS |
| `gap_analysis` | "Process goals" framework: evaluate execution quality, not just outcome metrics |

## Perspectives #39: An Unexpected Balance with James McKay

### Key Claims

| Claim | Evidence Level |
|-------|---------------|
| At 30+ hrs/week volume, only 2 targeted interval sessions per week are needed for progression | Experience-based (elite case study) |
| Riding as low as 50% FTP for most volume is not junk miles -- it drives mitochondrial biogenesis, capillary density, fat oxidation | Research-backed |
| Gaining body weight (70-->76 kg) increased both absolute power AND W/kg when previously under-fueled | Experience-based |
| Speakman's Dual Intervention Point model: body defends a lower body-fat threshold (~5.5--6 kg fat for this athlete); dropping below triggers hormonal crash | Research-backed |
| kJ from power meter is NOT a 1:1 calorie estimate due to metabolic inefficiency, TEF, and NEAT | Research-backed |
| Cramp induction rides (overdressed + hard efforts + sprints until cramps occur) reset neuromuscular cramp threshold | Experience-based (novel protocol) |
| Race simulations harder than the actual race (e.g., 13x90s max efforts with tempo between) create profound adaptation but require >1 week recovery | Experience-based |

### Actionable Guidelines

- **High-volume intensity cap**: at 25--30+ hrs/week, limit to 2 interval sessions; rest is easy volume
- **Body composition**: track body fat mass (not just weight); identify personal lower intervention point
- **Energy accounting**: actual TDEE for Tour-level athletes = 1.6--2.3x predicted BMR even after subtracting on-bike work
- **Cramp prevention protocol**: overdress + hard efforts + all-out sprints until cramps induced; do 2--4 weeks before target event
- **Race simulation protocol**: match or exceed race demands (e.g., repeat count, effort duration, recovery format) in a single session; allow >7 days recovery

## TMT #42: Training Implications of Strength as a Skill

### Key Claims

| Claim | Evidence Level |
|-------|---------------|
| Strength is primarily a neurological skill, not just muscle size; "newbie gains" are skill acquisition, not hypertrophy | Research-backed |
| In-season squat 1RM decline does NOT mean loss of sport-specific force if on-bike peak power is maintained | Experience-based + research |
| Unilateral exercises (split squats, single-leg press) have more cycling carryover than bilateral squats | Research-backed (biomechanical specificity) |
| Leg press removes stability requirement, making it more analogous to pedaling than barbell squat | Experience-based |
| 3--5 lbs initial weight gain from lifting is water/glycogen/inflammation, not muscle | Research-backed |
| Interference effect is primarily a fatigue management problem, not a cellular AMPK/mTOR conflict in practice | Research-backed + experience |

### Actionable Guidelines

- **In-season maintenance (general fitness)**: 1--2x/week, 2--3 sets of 8--15 reps at 5--6/10 RPE
- **In-season maintenance (max force)**: 3--6 total sets of 1--5 reps at 7--9/10 RPE, 1--3x/week (1x usually sufficient)
- **Example session**: warm-up + 3 sets of 2 heavy reps on single-leg press
- **Separate hard lifting from hard riding by 24--48 hours**; easy ride day after lifting is fine
- **Core exercises**: squat or deadlift (bone density) + single-leg press or Bulgarian split squat (cycling-specific) + hamstring curls (balance/injury prevention) + core
- **Sprint vs. gym**: prioritize on-bike sprints first; only add gym when sprint has plateaued
- **Bodyweight option**: walking lunges with weighted backpack; reverse Nordic curls (advanced, high patellofemoral forces)

### Platform Relevance

| Module | Insight |
|--------|---------|
| `blocks` | Flag if athlete is doing heavy lifting and hard intervals on same day |
| `pdcurve` | Track peak sprint power separately from gym 1RM -- gym decline is skill decay, not strength loss |
| `training_load` | Lifting fatigue should be factored into total training load |

## TMT #46: Pitfalls of Science Interpretation and Communication

### Key Claims

| Claim | Evidence Level |
|-------|---------------|
| "The science says" is almost always an oversimplification; science is a process, not a monolith | Research-backed (epistemology) |
| Individual responses within studies range from hyper-responders to non-responders to negative responders | Research-backed |
| Statistical significance != practical importance; a 2W FTP increase may be within power meter error | Research-backed |
| Ramp test at population level uses ~75% of final-minute power, but individuals range from 55--90% | Research-backed |
| Publication bias: positive results published more than null results, skewing perceived literature | Research-backed |
| Reproducibility crisis: large percentage of exercise science studies cannot be replicated | Research-backed |
| Fallacy of division: group average does not apply to every individual | Research-backed |

### Actionable Guidelines

- **Interpret studies**: always check variance/SD, not just means; look at individual participant data
- **N=1 approach**: use scientific literature as starting point, then test on the individual athlete
- **Evidence-based practice**: collect evidence FROM the athlete; if intervention works, that is evidence; if not, also evidence
- **Beware truncated axes**: graphs can make small differences look enormous

### Platform Relevance

| Module | Insight |
|--------|---------|
| `ftp_test` | Population-based formulas (75% ramp) should show confidence interval, not single number |
| `gap_analysis` | Individual response tracking: compare athlete response to intervention against expected range |
| `clinical` | Display individual variance alongside population norms for all metrics |

## TMT #50: Fat and Carb Burning Myths

### Key Claims

| Claim | Evidence Level |
|-------|---------------|
| Body is an ENERGY accountant, not a SUBSTRATE accountant -- total energy balance matters, not fat vs. carb ratio | Research-backed |
| Replacing only carbs burned on a 3500 kJ ride leaves a 3500 kcal deficit -- 30--60x greater than sustainable hourly fat loss rate | Research-backed (calculation) |
| Adipose tissue is an active endocrine organ (leptin, ghrelin signaling), not passive storage | Research-backed |
| Acute large energy deficit triggers metabolic slowdown (thyroid hormone, testosterone, IGF-1 suppression) | Research-backed |
| Delaying post-ride meal by a few hours measurably impairs next-day performance | Research-backed |
| Tour de France riders: off-bike energy expenditure = 1.6--2.3x predicted BMR (doubly-labeled water studies) | Research-backed |
| BMR + bike kJ drastically underestimates TDEE -- ignores TEF (~10% of intake) and NEAT | Research-backed |
| Minnesota Starvation Experiment demonstrates extreme metabolic/psychological consequences of severe deficit | Research-backed |

### Actionable Guidelines

- **Fuel rides properly**: do not try to "burn fat" by under-fueling -- this creates acute deficit that impairs recovery
- **TDEE formula**: BMR + EAT + TEF + NEAT; for endurance athletes, multiply predicted BMR by 1.6--2.3x
- **Fat loss strategy**: small, consistent, sustainable deficit (0.5--1.0 lb/week) -- not massive acute deficits
- **Post-ride nutrition**: eat promptly after rides; delay impairs next-day performance
- **Stop using kJ = kcal assumption**: actual energy cost is significantly higher

### Platform Relevance

| Module | Insight |
|--------|---------|
| `nutrition` | TDEE calculator should use multiplier (1.6--2.3x BMR) not simple BMR + kJ |
| `nutrition` | Flag acute energy deficits > recommended rate (>500 kcal/day for most) |
| `durability` | Under-fueling degrades durability -- next-day performance impact is measurable |
| `training_load` | Energy availability should be a tracked metric alongside TSS/CTL |

## TMT #59: Considerations for Cramps

### Key Claims

| Claim | Evidence Level |
|-------|---------------|
| Exercise-Associated Muscle Cramps (EAMCs) are primarily neuromuscular, NOT dehydration/electrolyte | Research-backed |
| Electrolyte relief timing does not match absorption time (minutes vs. 20--30 min) -- mechanism is TRP channel activation | Research-backed |
| Central theory: fatigue causes muscle spindle overactivation + Golgi tendon organ underactivation = runaway contraction | Research-backed |
| Peripheral theory: fatigue causes abnormal excitation at neuromuscular junction | Research-backed |
| Stretching works because it manually activates GTOs, sending "relax" inhibitory signal | Research-backed |
| Hot sauce / pickle juice / mustard / Warheads work via TRP channel activation (noxious substance reflex), NOT salt content | Research-backed |
| Cramp risk factors: age, family history, high relative intensity, fatigue, insufficient stretching | Research-backed |

### Actionable Guidelines

- **Immediate relief**: sip Tabasco/hot sauce, pickle juice, or mustard -- TRP channel activation resets motor neurons within seconds
- **Prevention**: train specifically for race demands (surges, duration, heat) to raise neuromuscular fatigue threshold
- **Positional specificity**: train on your race-specific equipment -- different bike = different muscle recruitment = cramp risk
- **Heat acclimatization**: 250W at 95F humid >> 250W at 60F dry in relative intensity; heat training reduces cramp risk
- **Strength training**: target cramp-prone muscles (e.g., Copenhagen planks for adductors)
- **Cramp induction rides**: push neuromuscular system to failure in training (2--4 weeks pre-event) to raise threshold

### Platform Relevance

| Module | Insight |
|--------|---------|
| `durability` | Cramp risk model: track when athlete exceeds trained intensity x duration envelope |
| `pacing` | Alert if race demands exceed athlete trained surges/duration profile |
| `clinical` | Cramp history tracking: correlate with temperature, duration, intensity, equipment |
| `nutrition` | TRP channel intervention notes (hot sauce protocol) in race-day toolkit |

## TMT #65: Why Training/Racing Experience Should Not Determine Training Volume

### Key Claims

| Claim | Evidence Level |
|-------|---------------|
| Racing/training experience does NOT determine appropriate training volume -- individual physiology and life context do | Experience-based (strong consensus among coaches) |

| Volume makes it feel "easier" to do quality efforts -- larger aerobic base raises the floor | Experience-based |

- **Volume assessment criteria**: (1) available time, (2) recovery capacity, (3) historical response, (4) event demands
- **If struggling in stage races/long events**: volume insufficiency is the most likely limiter, not intensity
- **Longer intervals + more volume combined**: extending TTE and base volume simultaneously was transformative for multiple athletes

## TMT #71: Avoiding Panic Training (Starts Now)

### Key Claims

| Claim | Evidence Level |
|-------|---------------|
| "Panic training" = cramming fitness in final weeks before target event; almost always counterproductive | Experience-based (universal) |
| Prevention starts months before: build in buffer weeks for illness, travel, life disruptions (2--3 lost weeks/season is normal) | Experience-based |
| Breaking long-term goals into smaller milestones (project management approach) keeps training on track | Experience-based |
| If you are behind schedule, the answer is usually NOT more intensity -- it is accepting a lower ceiling and optimizing what you have | Experience-based |
| Getting sick loses 2--3 weeks -- plan assumes this will happen at least once | Experience-based |

### Actionable Guidelines

- **Plan buffer**: assume 2--3 weeks lost per 6-month season (illness, travel, life)
- **Milestone approach**: set intermediate targets every 4--6 weeks so you know if you are on track
- **If behind schedule**: do NOT pile on volume/intensity; instead reduce goal expectations and optimize recovery/taper
- **Late-season panic fix**: shift focus to race craft, nutrition execution, and tactical preparation instead of fitness cramming
- **Start early**: the best time to prevent panic training is 3--6 months before the event

### Key Claims

| Claim | Evidence Level |
|-------|---------------|
| AMPK is a cellular energy sensor AND a glycogen sensor via beta-subunit glycogen-binding domain | Research-backed (in-vitro, definitively proven) |
| High glycogen INHIBITS AMPK activity (not low glycogen activating it -- reframing) | Research-backed |
| AMPK is inhibited even in presence of AMP activator when glycogen is high -- glycogen sensing is independent of energy status | Research-backed |
| "Train low" (low glycogen) strategies increase fat oxidation but do NOT improve performance | Research-backed (in-vivo human studies) |
| Mechanism is not outcome: a molecular pathway discovery does not validate a training intervention | Research-backed (scientific epistemology) |
| Delayed feeding post-exercise impairs recovery and next-day performance even if glycogen is eventually replenished | Research-backed |
| The ice-cream-shark-attack analogy: correlation (AMPK + low glycogen) does not prove causation for training strategy | Research-backed (epistemology) |

### Actionable Guidelines

- **Do NOT train glycogen-depleted for adaptation**: performance and recovery suffer; molecular signal increase does not translate to superior outcomes
- **Fuel during training**: glycogen availability enables higher quality work, which is the primary adaptation driver
- **Post-exercise feeding**: eat promptly to avoid recovery impairment
- **Skepticism for "biohacks"**: biological systems are robust; hacking a single pathway rarely yields net positive without consequences
- **Best stimulus for adaptation**: consistent, well-fueled, progressive training -- not glycogen manipulation

## Watts Doc #56: Strength Training Strategies to Avoid Weight Gain

### Key Claims

| Claim | Evidence Level |
|-------|---------------|
| Heavy, low-rep strength training (1--5 reps) with low total volume minimizes hypertrophy while maintaining force production | Research-backed (Bayesian analysis in cited study) |
| Doing a heavy single once/week produces barely detectable strength gains -- minimum effective dose is higher | Research-backed |
| Weight gain from starting lifting is almost always water/glycogen/inflammation initially (3--5 lbs), not muscle | Research-backed |
| True muscle hypertrophy is a slow, difficult process requiring sustained caloric surplus + high volume + progressive overload | Research-backed |
| Bayesian statistical analysis is superior to frequentist for small-sample exercise studies | Research-backed (statistical methodology) |

### Actionable Guidelines

- **Avoid weight gain protocol**: heavy weight, low reps (1--5), low total sets (3--6), 1--2x per week
- **Expect initial weight gain**: 3--5 lbs in first 2--3 weeks is normal and is NOT muscle mass
- **Track body composition**: weight alone is misleading; use trends over 4+ weeks
- **For cyclists specifically**: single-leg press and split squats at heavy loads, low reps
- **Do not fear lifting**: true hypertrophy requires deliberate effort that endurance athletes typically do not produce

## Watts Doc #62: Setting Up Your N=1 Training Experiment

### Key Claims

| Claim | Evidence Level |
|-------|---------------|
| Every athlete is an N=1 experiment; group study averages may not apply to the individual | Research-backed (statistical theory) |
| Small samples can randomly violate exchangeability and produce false results | Research-backed |
| Repeated intervention design (same athlete, multiple cycles) is the most coaching-relevant experimental setup | Research-backed |
| Control conditions in N=1: use the athlete's own baseline, not a separate control group | Research-backed |
| Sampling variance in small studies means a single positive result may not replicate | Research-backed |
| The "repeated within-participant" trial design (e.g., unilateral leg training) isolates individual response | Research-backed |

### Actionable Guidelines

- **Coaching as experimentation**: form hypothesis from literature -> test on athlete -> observe response -> adjust
- **Repeated intervention**: if an approach worked once, try it again in a different block to confirm it is reproducible
- **Baseline matters**: compare athlete to their own previous performance, not to population norms
- **Track individual response**: maintain longitudinal records so patterns emerge over seasons
- **Multiple outcome measures**: do not rely on single metric; track FTP, TTE, PD curve shape, subjective feel, race results

**Episodes**: TMT 46, TMT 65, WD 62, 1M AMA

- N=1 experimental design is the gold standard for coaching

**Episodes**: Perspectives 39, TMT 59, TMT 65, TMT 71
- Cramps are neuromuscular (not electrolyte) -- train specifically for race demands
- Stage race underperformance usually = volume/durability insufficiency
- Race simulations harder than the event itself create profound adaptation
- **Platform action**: Race demands profiler that compares athlete trained envelope to event demands

### 4. Strength Training Integration
**Episodes**: TMT 42, TMT 47, WD 56

- **Platform action**: Rest week scheduling, CTL/ATL context display, "feeling worse is normal" education
