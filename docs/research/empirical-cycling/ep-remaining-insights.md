# Empirical Cycling Podcast — Remaining Episode Insights

Extracted from 21 episodes. Key for evidence levels:
- **[R]** = Research-backed (cites specific studies/mechanisms)
- **[E]** = Experience-based (coaching/racing observation, N>1)
- **[O]** = Opinion (informed speculation, single-case)

---

## 1. 1M: AMA

### Key Claims
- TTE (time to exhaustion) is not perfectly precise; athletes often feel they could have held on longer but sub-maximal cutoffs still provide useful data **[E]**
- Critical power models can underestimate below-CP power durations because the model doesn't fully account for durability loss **[R]**
- Max heart rate should be thought of as a constellation of symptoms rather than a single number; it varies day to day **[R]**
- More riding volume before VO2max blocks = better response; ultra-endurance athletes who never do VO2max efforts see massive gains from a single block **[E]**
- Well-trained athletes see less adaptation per unit of training from high-intensity work vs. untrained; endurance volume provides adaptations that intensity alone cannot replicate **[R]**
- Meta-review finding: endurance training adaptations exceeded what HIT and sprint training produced in well-trained athletes **[R]**
- 10 hours/week is tough to see giant responses unless basically untrained **[E]**

### Actionable Guidelines
- Time-crunched base: if gym interrupts interval quality, consider dropping gym to 1x/week and keep the interval session that progresses best
- Don't chase max HR as a single number; look at multiple indicators (RPE, breathing, coordination breakdown)
- For VO2max blocks in ultra-endurance athletes, build massive volume base first, then add a short VO2 block for outsized gains

### Platform Relevance
- **pdcurve**: CP model limitations acknowledged; sub-CP durations may need empirical correction
- **durability**: TTE imprecision + below-CP fatigue effects relevant to durability modeling
- **training_load**: volume base magnifies intensity response; track volume-before-intensity sequencing
- **zones**: max HR as a range, not a fixed number
- **ftp_test**: TTE not perfectly precise; use trends not single tests

## 2. Perspectives #37: Practical Performance Psychology (Billy Ryan)

### Key Claims
- ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy) framework: thoughts and emotions are indicators, not commands; assess their "workability" **[R]**
- Pre-race anxiety is normal and often not actionable; workability of a thought depends on timing (3 weeks out vs. 1 minute before start) **[E]**
- Psychological flexibility = knowing which internal signals to act on vs. let pass **[R]**
- Tire pressure light analogy: anxious thoughts are often routine indicators, not emergencies **[E]**
- Rigid attachment to outcomes creates suffering; flexible goal pursuit allows pivoting without collapse **[R]**
- Fear of losing identity if performance drops is a major psychological barrier **[E]**

### Actionable Guidelines
- Before a race: assess "is this thought workable right now?" If 3 weeks out, act on it. If 1 minute before, drop the rope
- Practice "dropping the rope" in a tug-of-war with unhelpful thoughts rather than fighting them
- Build flexibility by exposing yourself to uncomfortable scenarios in lower-stakes events first
- Differentiate between signal (undertrained) and noise (pre-race jitters) by checking if there are actionable steps remaining

### Platform Relevance
- **clinical**: mental health awareness; psychological flexibility training as performance tool
- **pacing**: pre-race anxiety management affects pacing decisions
- **gap_analysis**: psychological limiters alongside physiological ones

## 3. Perspectives #39: An Unexpected Balance (James McKay)

### Key Claims
- James McKay won Lincoln Grand Prix after 6-7 years of targeting it; long-term patience + progressive development = key **[E]**
- Heat acclimation training reduced cramping and improved hot-weather performance substantially **[R]**
- Heat training is easy to overdo; one bad session left him wiped for days **[E]**
- Cramps correlated with combination of heat + duration + intensity, especially early-season when not adapted **[E]**
- Balance between structured training and life (UK amateur) is critical; consistency > optimization **[E]**

### Actionable Guidelines
- Heat acclimation: start conservatively; err on the side of under-dosing; one bad session can cost days of recovery
- Aero equipment in warm races is viable if heat-adapted (wore aero base layer, helmet, overshoes in 25C race without issues while non-adapted riders suffered)
- For cramp-prone riders: build heat tolerance progressively, ensure adequate sodium/electrolytes, train at race-specific durations

### Platform Relevance
- **durability**: heat + duration compound fatigue; heat acclimation as durability intervention
- **pacing**: heat-adapted athletes can sustain higher %FTP in warm conditions
- **clinical**: cramping patterns correlate with training specificity gaps
- **blocks**: multi-year periodization; patience in development

## 4. TMT #42: Training Implications of Strength as a Skill

### Key Claims
- Strength is a skill (neural drive, coordination) not just muscle size; neural adaptations can be maintained with very low volume **[R]**
- Heavy singles (1-3 reps) can maintain strength with minimal systemic fatigue and minimal hypertrophy stimulus **[R]**
- Cyclists who are aerobically conditioned recover faster from heavy low-rep strength work than pure powerlifters due to lower body mass and better recovery capacity **[E]**
- Strength maintenance requires far less volume than strength building; 1x/week heavy singles sufficient for many **[R]**
- Endurance athletes doing heavy low-rep work experience less systemic fatigue than high-rep hypertrophy work **[R]**

### Actionable Guidelines
- To maintain strength in-season: 3 heavy singles at RPE 7/10, 1x/week
- Don't confuse strength maintenance (low reps, heavy) with hypertrophy (moderate reps, moderate weight)
- If bike-focused: keep gym sessions brief and heavy, not long and moderate
- Measure strength via occasional heavy singles or doubles rather than rep-based tests

### Platform Relevance
- **training_load**: strength sessions with low volume/high intensity have minimal interference with cycling training load
- **blocks**: in-season strength maintenance protocol = 1x/week heavy singles
- **clinical**: strength as injury prevention; neural drive benefits for sprinting

## 5. TMT #43: Off-Season Breaks and Massive Listener Q&A

### Key Claims
- Off-season breaks are essential for mental and physical recovery; 1-2 weeks of minimal/no riding recommended **[E]**
- Well-trained athletes see less per-unit adaptation from high-intensity training; need more volume base **[R]**
- Ultra-endurance athletes who never do VO2max work see massive gains from a single VO2 block after volume base **[E]**
- Mid-season breaks: expect 2-3 weeks to return to pre-break fitness (1 week break); fitness often rebounds higher **[E]**
- End-of-season breaks: 1-2 weeks little/no riding, then slow re-entry focused on volume and fun **[E]**
- Fall months in cold climates: make use of good weather/daylight for volume; ease back with low pressure **[E]**

### Actionable Guidelines
- Off-season break: 1-2 weeks minimal riding, then gradual re-entry
- Mid-season break recovery: ~1 week break requires ~2-3 weeks to return, structured as: week 1 = volume, weeks 2-3 = add intensity
- End-of-season: slow re-entry, prioritize fun rides and volume, defer structured intensity
- Use the off-season to address gym work, weaknesses, and mental reset

### Platform Relevance
- **blocks**: off-season periodization templates; break duration vs. detraining curves
- **training_load**: detraining/retraining kinetics after breaks
- **gap_analysis**: use off-season to target identified weaknesses

## 6. TMT #46: Pitfalls of Science Interpretation and Communication

### Key Claims
- "The science says" is a misleading phrase; science provides evidence with limitations, not absolute truths **[R]**
- Evidence-based practice = start with published protocols, then individualize; adjustment is critical **[E]**
- Small differences in interval structure (e.g., 5x5 vs. 4x8) are not meaningfully different for training adaptations **[R]**
- Critical power as a concept has limitations in application but its underlying physiology is still informative **[R]**
- Training in zones (ranges) rather than targeting exact watts is appropriate because the underlying physiology operates in ranges **[R]**
- Ecological validity matters: study protocols may not match real-world training **[R]**

### Actionable Guidelines
- Start with published protocols as a baseline, then adjust based on individual response
- Don't over-optimize interval structures; small variations in duration/number are effectively equivalent
- Read research for its physiological insights even if you disagree with the training model used
- Use zones as ranges, not exact watt targets

### Platform Relevance
- **zones**: zones represent physiological ranges, not precise watt boundaries
- **pdcurve**: CP model limitations acknowledged; use CP physiology without over-relying on exact numbers
- **training_load**: evidence-based practice = protocol + individualization
- **ftp_test**: any single test has limitations; aggregate multiple data points

## 7. TMT #47: Strength Training with Limited Equipment

### Key Claims
- Unilateral exercises (single-leg squats) effectively double the load challenge with same weight **[R]**
- Elevated heel single-leg squats are extremely challenging for quads even with light dumbbells **[E]**
- Tempo manipulation (slow eccentrics, pauses) increases time under tension and difficulty without adding weight **[R]**
- Going unilateral-only long-term can lead to stabilizer fatigue and potentially overuse issues **[E]**
- AMRAPs (as many reps as possible) at lighter weights become increasingly aerobic at higher rep ranges, which taxes cyclists differently **[R]**

### Actionable Guidelines
- Limited equipment protocol: single-leg elevated heel squats + slow eccentrics + pauses
- Use unilateral work to double effective load with limited weights
- Don't go exclusively unilateral forever; mix in bilateral when possible
- For cyclists: avoid high-rep AMRAPs on squats if goal is neural drive (prefer heavier, fewer reps)

### Platform Relevance
- **training_load**: gym session design that minimizes interference with cycling
- **blocks**: off-season gym programming with limited equipment

## 8. TMT #50: Fat and Carb Burning Myths

### Key Claims
- "Fat burning zone" is misleading; absolute fat oxidation matters less than total energy expenditure for body composition **[R]**
- Constrained energy expenditure hypothesis (Pontzer): body adjusts BMR and NEAT downward when exercise energy expenditure rises; total energy expenditure has a ceiling **[R]**
- Thermic effect of food is non-negligible but also not massive **[R]**
- No good data on actual energy multipliers for recreational endurance athletes (8-15 hrs/week); only have world-class data **[O]**
- Energy compensation (reduced NEAT, reduced BMR) is a major factor in why athletes plateau on weight loss despite high training volumes **[R]**
- Calorie calculation for cyclists is highly uncertain; intensity, volume, thermic effect of food, and energy compensation all introduce error **[R]**

### Actionable Guidelines
- Don't train specifically in a "fat burning zone" for weight loss; total caloric balance matters more
- Track energy expenditure with understanding that power meter kJ underestimates total daily energy needs
- Be aware of energy compensation: if you feel lethargic and sedentary off the bike, you may be in excessive deficit
- Fueling on the bike reduces the "calorie hole" and energy compensation effects post-ride

### Platform Relevance
- **nutrition**: fat/carb oxidation rates misunderstood; total energy balance is what matters
- **zones**: "fat burning zone" is not a meaningful training zone for weight management
- **durability**: energy compensation affects recovery capacity and next-day performance
- **training_load**: energy expenditure tracking needs contextual adjustment for compensation

## 9. TMT #53: Leveling Up Parenting and Training

### Key Claims
- Realistic time availability is the #1 constraint for parent-athletes; coach must work around it, not the other way **[E]**
- If cycling is new relative to the relationship, the partner's buy-in is critical and may be limited **[E]**
- Negotiation with partner about training time is essential; treat it as a shared resource allocation **[E]**
- Having an outlet for exercise improves mental health and makes you a better partner/parent **[E]**
- Minimum effective dose thinking is critical for time-crunched parents **[E]**

### Actionable Guidelines
- Ask: how many hours can you realistically ride this week? Build the plan around that
- If new to cycling relative to the relationship, start with modest time commitment and demonstrate it benefits the household
- 1-2 quality interval sessions + whatever volume fits is often sufficient for parent-athletes
- Weekend long rides: negotiate trade-offs (e.g., partner gets equivalent personal time)

### Platform Relevance
- **training_load**: minimum effective dose for time-crunched athletes
- **blocks**: planning around real-world time constraints

## 10. TMT #56: Resetting Goals and Motivation

### Key Claims
- Loss of motivation after a bad season or injury is normal; forced goals lead to worse outcomes **[E]**
- Finding intrinsic reasons to ride (fun, health, social) sustains motivation better than extrinsic goals (results, watts) **[E]**
- Pivoting from a failed A-race plan to "vacation mode" at the same event preserves long-term motivation **[E]**
- Accountability and motivation from a coach is one of the most common reasons people seek coaching **[E]**
- Zero riding for extended periods leads to measurably worse physical and mental health outcomes **[E]**
- Minimum exercise for mental health benefits is surprisingly low **[E]**

### Actionable Guidelines
- If training has collapsed: pivot event goals from "race" to "experience/finish" to avoid spiraling
- Shift to gravel/fun rides if road racing motivation is gone; cross-discipline pivot preserves fitness and mental health
- Don't force high-intensity when motivation is low; ride at whatever intensity feels good
- Some riding > no riding for both physical and mental health maintenance

### Platform Relevance
- **clinical**: mental health dimensions of training load management
- **gap_analysis**: flag when performance trend is declining to suggest goal reassessment
- **blocks**: goal reset protocol when mid-season plan fails

## 11. TMT #57: Our Coaches Answer Your Training Questions

### Key Claims
- High work stress doubles recovery cost when combined with hard training **[E]**
- During high-stress periods, switch goal from "improve" to "maintain" — maintenance requires surprisingly little volume **[E]**
- Travel stress (flying, time zones, exposure to illness) is a hidden stressor that should reduce training expectations **[E]**
- Maintaining fitness requires much less training than building it **[R]**
- Sometimes the best training prescription is rest/nothing **[E]**

### Actionable Guidelines
- High life-stress weeks: drop to maintenance mode (1-2 easy rides + 1 short interval session max)
- Travel weeks: reduce volume and intensity; prioritize not getting sick
- Maintenance requires far less than building; don't panic about missing sessions during stressful periods
- Monitor work/life stress as a training load input

### Platform Relevance
- **training_load**: non-cycling stress (work, travel, sleep) as training load modifier
- **blocks**: stress-adjusted block prescriptions
- **clinical**: flag athletes in high-stress periods for reduced training recommendations

## 12. TMT #59: Considerations for Cramps

### Key Claims
- Exercise-associated muscle cramps (EAMC) are distinct from other cramp categories in the literature **[R]**
- EAMC are driven by combination of duration + intensity + novelty of effort, not just electrolytes **[R]**
- Electrolyte/sodium depletion theory is not well supported for EAMC; neural fatigue theory is stronger **[R]**
- Mechanical tension on fatigued muscles is a key contributor; stretching may reduce passive tension **[R]**
- First high-intensity race of season often triggers cramp-like symptoms due to lack of race-specific conditioning **[E]**
- Heat compounds cramping risk independently of hydration status **[R]**
- Coasting while tensing muscles (not relaxing between efforts) was identified as a personal cramping trigger **[E]**

### Actionable Guidelines
- Build race-specific intensity progressively; don't jump into maximal race efforts without preparation
- Train at race-specific durations to condition muscles for sustained effort
- Incorporate stretching as potentially helpful and at worst neutral for cramp prevention
- Practice conscious muscle relaxation during coasting/recovery periods in rides
- Heat acclimation reduces cramping risk in hot conditions
- Electrolyte supplementation: may help but evidence is weak for EAMC specifically

### Platform Relevance
- **durability**: cramp risk as a durability metric; intensity + duration interaction
- **clinical**: cramping pattern analysis from ride data
- **pacing**: cramp risk increases with novel intensity/duration combinations
- **gap_analysis**: identify cramp-prone scenarios from historical ride data

## 13. TMT #61: Coach Q&A on Limiters, Low Volume, Rest Weeks, Resuming Training

### Key Claims
- Limiters are individual; two athletes at same FTP may have completely different weaknesses **[E]**
- Low-volume athletes (5-7 hrs/week) can still make significant progress with well-targeted intervals **[E]**
- Rest weeks are underused; most athletes wait too long to take them **[E]**
- After breaks: volume first, then add intensity; don't jump straight back into hard intervals **[E]**
- Under-optimized training (doing "enough" but not targeting the right things) is more common than overtraining **[E]**
- Mid-season break: 1 week off requires about 2-3 weeks to return; build volume first week, add intensity weeks 2-3 **[E]**

### Actionable Guidelines
- Resume training after break: week 1 = volume only at previous levels; weeks 2-3 = progressively add intensity
- Rest week frequency: every 3-4 weeks of progressive loading for most athletes; adjust based on response
- Low-volume training: prioritize 2 quality interval sessions/week + whatever volume fits
- Identify individual limiters through power profile analysis, not generic assumptions

### Platform Relevance
- **gap_analysis**: individual limiter identification from power profile
- **blocks**: rest week scheduling; return-from-break protocols
- **training_load**: volume-first reintroduction after breaks
- **pdcurve**: use PD curve shape to identify individual limiters

## 14. TMT #65: Why Training/Racing Experience Shouldn't Determine Training Volume

### Key Claims
- Years of experience is a poor proxy for trainability; some experienced riders respond like beginners to new stimuli **[E]**
- Training volume should be determined by recovery capacity and life constraints, not by how long you've been riding **[E]**
- Beginners can handle more volume than commonly prescribed if recovery and nutrition support it **[E]**
- Experienced riders who've never done structured training respond dramatically to it regardless of years riding **[E]**
- Social media comparisons are pure selection bias; nobody posts their bad workouts **[E]**
- Comparison is the thief of joy; progress should only be measured against yourself **[E]**

### Actionable Guidelines
- Don't prescribe volume based on "years racing"; use actual recovery metrics and life constraints
- New-to-structure athletes: expect large gains regardless of riding history
- Assess trainability by response to training, not by resume
- Block social media comparison; track only personal progression

### Platform Relevance
- **training_load**: volume prescription based on recovery capacity, not experience level
- **gap_analysis**: training history analysis should weight structured vs. unstructured
- **clinical**: social media anxiety as performance limiter

## 15. TMT #66: The Best and Worst Training Habits

### Key Claims
- Best habit: consistency over weeks and months trumps any single workout optimization **[E]**
- Worst habit: testing too often; leads to test anxiety, insufficient rest before tests, and misjudging fitness **[E]**
- Focusing on short-term progression (day-to-day, week-to-week) leads to over-testing and anxiety **[E]**
- Most people don't need formal testing; let training be the guide **[E]**
- Testing too early after a block (insufficient rest) commonly produces disappointing results **[E]**
- Insufficient rest before testing is more common than people realize **[E]**
- In-season, FTP test results may not reflect race-specific fitness (e.g., crit fitness vs. TT fitness) **[E]**

### Actionable Guidelines
- Test infrequently; use progressive workout performance as the primary fitness indicator
- If testing: ensure adequate rest (3-5 days easy) before the test
- Don't judge fitness by a single test; use rolling training performance trends
- Match test type to target event (don't use TT-style test if racing crits)

### Platform Relevance
- **ftp_test**: over-testing is counterproductive; use training trends instead
- **pdcurve**: rolling PD curve from training data > isolated test days
- **training_load**: consistency metric more valuable than peak test results

## 17. TMT #71: Avoiding Panic Training (Starts Now)

### Key Claims
- Panic training = cramming intensity because the season is approaching and you feel unprepared **[E]**
- Panic training almost always backfires: too much intensity too soon leads to fatigue, not fitness **[E]**
- If you realize you're behind in January, the answer is NOT to double the intensity immediately **[E]**
- Pivoting goals when behind schedule is better than panic training to meet unrealistic ones **[E]**
- Athletes who are behind should build volume first, then add intensity progressively; don't skip steps **[E]**
- If training hasn't gone well, treat the A-event as a "vacation" rather than a target race **[E]**

### Actionable Guidelines
- January behind schedule? Add volume first for 3-4 weeks, THEN start intensity progression
- If your event is 8+ weeks out, there's time to build properly; don't panic
- If your event is <4 weeks out and you're unprepared, pivot goals (finish, enjoy, treat as training)
- Reduce expectations rather than increase training load when behind

### Platform Relevance
- **blocks**: anti-panic-training protocol; calendar-aware block recommendations
- **gap_analysis**: detect panic training patterns (sudden intensity spikes after low-load periods)
- **training_load**: flag athletes who suddenly ramp load after extended low periods

## 18. TMT #73: Things We Wish We Knew About Training, Racing, and Coaching

### Key Claims
- Fueling on the bike is the #1 thing coaches wish they knew earlier; old paradigm of 25g carbs/hr was grossly insufficient **[E]**
- Old era: fasted rides, 1 emergency gel, come home destroyed; new era: eat enough to feel same as when you left **[R]**
- Eating enough on the bike often leads to weight loss because it prevents post-ride overeating and energy compensation **[E]**
- Erg mode prevents athletes from learning their own physiology; coaches should wean athletes off erg **[E]**
- Sub-maximal training targets (e.g., 110% FTP when capable of 130%) hide fatigue signals **[E]**
- Training plans are "written in sand" — flexibility is essential; rigid compliance is counterproductive **[E]**
- Auto-regulation is the most important coaching skill to teach athletes **[E]**
- Longer-duration intervals (extending TTE) were a breakthrough, tied to better fueling **[E]**
- Weight regulation: being too light destroyed performance for months; best racing weight was well above minimum **[E]**
- LT1 training specifically is skeptical; LT1 rises best from lots of hours well under it, not training at LT1 **[E]**
- Performance variability day-to-day: if trending down, it's a yellow/red flag; trending up = good; flat = maybe OK at late training age **[E]**
- Strength training for cyclists over 35-40 is essential for health, and benefits performance especially if they don't do very hard intervals **[E]**
- Only ~5 fundamental workout types exist; don't over-complicate programming **[E]**
- Coaching cue: "I want you to feel about the same as when you left" = proper fueling **[E]**
- Process goals in racing (learn leadouts, contest preems) > outcome goals for skill development **[E]**
- "Eating ain't cheating" — fueling well is not a shortcut, it's baseline **[E]**
- Minimum effective dose of training is often much lower than athletes think **[E]**

### Actionable Guidelines
- Fuel target: feel approximately the same energy level post-ride as pre-ride
- Wean off erg mode; self-selected power reveals true daily readiness
- Use hard intervals (not sub-maximal) to reveal true fitness ceiling and detect fatigue
- Test infrequently; use day-to-day performance trends as the primary signal
- After 35, prioritize strength training for health even if bike performance isn't the primary goal
- LT1: don't train specifically at LT1; do lots of hours well below it instead
- Hours > miles for training prescription (distance is terrain-dependent; time is universal)
- If daily performance is trending down, investigate immediately (nutrition, sleep, stress, overreaching)

### Platform Relevance
- **nutrition**: fueling revolution; 25g/hr was insufficient; modern targets 60-90g/hr
- **ftp_test**: erg mode hides true readiness; prefer free-ride mode for key sessions
- **durability**: TTE extension tied to better fueling
- **training_load**: day-to-day performance variability as overreaching indicator
- **zones**: LT1 training zone not especially beneficial; volume below LT1 elevates LT1
- **clinical**: weight management — racing too light destroys performance for weeks/months
- **gap_analysis**: daily performance trend tracking as key diagnostic tool

## 19. Watts Doc #54: Glycogen's Effects on AMPK

### Key Claims
- AMPK (AMP-activated protein kinase) is a master metabolic regulator activated by low energy states **[R]**
- Glycogen directly inhibits AMPK activity in vitro; higher glycogen = more AMPK inhibition **[R]**
- The relationship is an S-shaped curve: AMPK activity is high at low glycogen, drops as glycogen increases **[R]**
- Cow vs. rat glycogen showed different magnitudes of AMPK inhibition, suggesting glycogen structure matters **[R]**
- Glycogen binds to the beta subunit of AMPK via a carbohydrate-binding module (CBM) **[R]**
- This provides a molecular mechanism for "train low" approaches: low glycogen = higher AMPK = greater mitochondrial biogenesis signaling **[R]**
- However, practical implications are complex: training with chronically low glycogen impairs performance and may increase illness/injury risk **[R]**
- The glycogen-AMPK interaction is one signal among many; it doesn't justify blanket "train low" protocols **[R]**

### Actionable Guidelines
- "Train low" has a molecular basis but practical execution requires nuance
- Periodize glycogen availability rather than chronically depleting: some sessions low, most sessions adequately fueled
- Sleep-low protocols (deplete glycogen in PM session, sleep without carbs, do AM easy session low) are one practical approach
- Do NOT do high-intensity sessions glycogen-depleted; reserve "train low" for easy/moderate sessions only
- Glycogen depletion increases RPE and reduces work capacity; performance quality matters more than molecular signaling

### Platform Relevance
- **nutrition**: glycogen periodization framework; "train low" only for easy sessions
- **training_load**: glycogen state as a modifier of training stimulus
- **durability**: glycogen depletion effects on late-ride performance
- **zones**: low-glycogen sessions should be in zone 1-2 only

## 20. Watts Doc #56: Strength Training Strategies to Avoid Weight Gain

### Key Claims
- Main concern for cyclists adding gym work: unwanted hypertrophy increasing body mass **[E]**
- Study on powerlifters: 3 protocols over 6 weeks — (1) one heavy single 3x/week, (2) heavy single + back-off sets at 80%, (3) one AMRAP set at 70% **[R]**
- Heavy singles (protocol 1) produced strength gains with minimal body weight change **[R]**
- Back-off sets and AMRAPs produced more hypertrophy (and weight gain) **[R]**
- For cyclists wanting strength without mass: heavy singles or very low-rep sets at high intensity are optimal **[R]**
- Concurrent training (endurance + strength) naturally suppresses some hypertrophy via AMPK pathway interference with mTOR **[R]**
- Cyclists are already somewhat protected from hypertrophy due to high aerobic training volume **[R]**
- Caloric surplus is required for significant hypertrophy; cyclists in energy balance or deficit are unlikely to gain meaningful mass from gym work **[R]**

### Actionable Guidelines
- Protocol for strength without weight gain: 1-3 heavy singles at RPE 9-9.5, 2-3x/week
- Avoid back-off sets and AMRAP sets if concerned about weight gain
- Don't eat in caloric surplus if trying to gain strength without mass
- In-season: reduce to 1x/week heavy singles for maintenance
- Off-season: can push to 2-3x/week for strength building, still keeping reps very low (1-3)
- If weight starts increasing: reduce volume (sets) before reducing intensity (load)

### Platform Relevance
- **training_load**: gym session quantification; heavy singles = low training stress
- **blocks**: strength periodization — off-season build (2-3x/week) to in-season maintain (1x/week)
- **nutrition**: caloric balance determines whether gym work causes weight gain
- **clinical**: strength training for health and injury prevention in aging cyclists

## 21. Watts Doc #62: Setting Up Your n=1 Training Experiment

### Key Claims
- n=1 experiments are inherently noisy; single data points are "myth-busters level" evidence **[R]**
- Small changes in VO2max or FTP (e.g., 2-5 watts, 1-2 mL/min/kg) are within measurement error of most tools **[R]**
- Power meter error of 2% means a 300W FTP could be 294-306W; a 5W "improvement" may be noise **[R]**
- For n=1 to be meaningful: need large effect sizes, consistent testing protocols, and multiple data points **[R]**
- Ramp test peak power changes that exceed what VO2max changes can explain suggest aerobic capacity (W') or efficiency improvements **[R]**
- Confounding variables are the biggest threat to n=1 conclusions: sleep, nutrition, stress, season all change simultaneously **[R]**
- Repeated measurements across time are more reliable than single pre/post comparisons **[R]**
- Be skeptical of your own positive results; confirmation bias is real **[R]**

### Actionable Guidelines
- For meaningful self-experimentation: keep everything else constant, change ONE variable, measure over 4-6+ weeks
- Use multiple metrics (FTP, VO2max proxy, TTE, power profile, subjective RPE) rather than a single number
- Demand large changes before concluding something "worked"; small changes are likely noise
- Repeat the experiment if possible before drawing conclusions
- Log confounding variables (sleep, stress, nutrition, illness) to contextualize results
- Use rolling averages of training performance rather than single test days

### Platform Relevance
- **ftp_test**: single tests are noisy; rolling PD curve from training data is more reliable
- **pdcurve**: multiple data points across the curve > single metric changes
- **training_load**: self-experimentation framework for training plan adjustments
- **gap_analysis**: track confounding variables alongside performance changes

### Durability Module
- **Heat + duration + intensity**: triple interaction drives cramp risk (TMT-59, Persp-39)

### Training Load Module
