# WKO5 Training Methodology

This skill covers how to use WKO5 for coaching and training — the methodology, workflows, and decision-making frameworks that turn data into better performance.

## The Power Duration Model (PDM)

The foundation of WKO5 training. A mathematical model fitted to the athlete's mean-max power (MMP) curve that derives individualized physiological parameters:

- **Pmax** — neuromuscular/sprint capacity (max instantaneous power)
- **FRC** (Functional Reserve Capacity) — anaerobic energy above FTP, in kJ. The "matchbook" of energy above threshold.
- **mFTP** (Modeled FTP) — sustainable aerobic threshold power, derived from the model (not manually set)
- **TTE** (Time to Exhaustion) — the maximum duration for which a power equal to mFTP can be maintained (typically 40-55 min for trained athletes, range 30-70 min)
- **mVO2max** — estimated VO2max in L/min
- **mFiber Type** — estimated % type I (slow-twitch) muscle fiber area
- **Stamina** — fatigue resistance metric
- **Phenotype** — classification: "Sprinter", "Pursuiter", "TTer", or "All-rounder"

All parameters interconnect. The PDM provides "precise, unbiased estimates of key parameters reflective of important physiological determinants of performance" (Dr. Andrew Coggan). It is the foundation upon which individualized training is built.

## iLevels: Individualized Training Zones

WKO5's key innovation over classic Coggan zones. iLevels blend modeled FTP and power-duration data to track the athlete's *actual capabilities*.

**Why iLevels matter:**
- Classic Coggan zones use fixed interval timeframes based on population averages
- iLevels auto-update as the athlete trains and de-trains
- Training targets (both power and time-in-zone) reflect individual physiology, not generic tables

**What iLevels provide:**
- **Power vs Duration** — individualized interval durations derived from the PD curve
- **Power vs Fitness** — targets that automatically adjust with fitness changes
- **Optimized Intervals** — recommended time-in-zone ranges based on the desired physiological response

## The 6-Step Power Duration Training Process

From Tim Cusick & Kevin Williams' "Advanced Training with Power" — a systematic workflow that must be followed in order, though each step has flexibility in execution.

### Step 1: Baseline Testing

Establish the PDM with an initial 5-day max-effort testing protocol:

| Day | Protocol |
|-----|----------|
| 1 | Warm up → **Max 5 min test** → Easy 45 min endurance → Cool down |
| 2 | Warm up → **Max 20-30 min test** (TT: 20 min, All-Around/Sprinter: 25 min, Pursuiter: 30 min) → Easy 20 min endurance → Cool down |
| 3 | Warm up → **Max 1 min test** → Easy 45 min endurance → Cool down |
| 4 | **Easy recovery or rest** |
| 5 | Warm up → **2x 150m max sprint** → Easy 45 min endurance → Cool down |

After this initial baseline, no additional formal testing is needed. Use unstructured testing going forward.

**What a clean curve looks like:** After testing, check the "PD Curve Chart with Error Calculations" — it shows Pmax, FRC, mFTP with standard error (S.E.) and coefficient of variation (C.V.) for each parameter, plus overall sum of squares (S.S.) for model fit quality.

### Step 2: Determine Strengths and Limiters

Use four key charts to assess rider ability:
- **Power Duration Profile** — overall power across durations
- **Power Profile Classic** — classic Coggan-style profile
- **PD Curve Profile Strengths and Limiters** — where the athlete excels vs. falls short relative to their own profile
- **Against World Class Power Duration Comparison** — benchmarking against global standards

Consider both **external** factors (general and specific demands of the event) and **internal** factors (rider ability, psychological focus, strengths/limiters).

### Step 3: Establish Training Levels

Set up iLevels. They will drive interval prescription in the next steps.

### Step 4: Design Training Strategy

Use Dr. Coggan's trainability table:

| Ability | Short-term gain | Long-term gain | Physiological cost |
|---------|----------------|---------------|-------------------|
| VO2max | 15-25% | 0-10% (?) | ++/+++ |
| Lactate threshold | 30-45% | 20-30% (?) | +/++ |
| Efficiency | 0-5% | 0-5% (???) | ++/+++ |
| Neuromuscular power | 15-25% | 10-20% (???) | +/++ |
| Anaerobic capacity | 15-25% | 0-10% (???) | +++/++++ |

Key insight: Lactate threshold has the highest trainability (30-45% short-term) with relatively low cost. VO2max and anaerobic capacity are trainable but expensive. Efficiency and neuromuscular power have limited long-term trainability.

The training strategy defines the path to the desired adaptations.

### Step 5: Build "Smart" Workouts

Design workouts targeted to individual physiology. The workout targeting process:

**Strategic level:** Purpose → Modality
- Driven by: season plan, needs analysis, rider ability, event demands, desired outcome

**Tactical level:** Interval (Duration/Intensity) → Relief (Duration/Intensity) → Density/Series/TIZ
- Driven by: immediate demand, power vs. capacity vs. specificity, athlete fitness, athlete motivation

Tools for workout design:
- **iLevels** for individualized intensity targets
- **Optimized Intervals** from the PD curve for duration targets
- **PD Curve Targeting** for specific power/duration prescriptions

Goal: **optimal adaptation for the least fatigue**.

### Step 6: Monitor Training with WKO

Track three areas using the Training Readiness dashboard:
- **Compliance** — is the athlete following the plan?
- **Performance Management** — CTL/ATL/TSB trends over time
- **Time in Zones** — distribution across iLevels

## Ongoing/Unstructured Testing

After initial baseline, maintain the PD curve through training itself:

- Compare **PD Curve** (red modeled line) to **MMP curve** (yellow dotted line)
- Where MMP dips below the PD curve → areas needing re-testing
- Use the **Normalized Residuals for Testing** chart — points below zero indicate durations to test
- Three target charts provide specific test prescriptions:
  - **Short Duration Test Targets** (seconds range)
  - **Medium Duration Test Targets** (minutes range)
  - **Long Duration Test Targets** (extended efforts)

**Key principles:**
- "Testing is training, and training is testing" — blend tests into regular workouts
- Review the testing chart weekly
- All tests should be within the same training cycle (not necessarily the same day/week)
- Ongoing unstructured testing every 30-60 days keeps the model accurate

## Using TTE for Training (Cusick & Coggan)

TTE (Time to Exhaustion) is the maximum duration for which power equal to mFTP can be maintained. On the PD curve, it appears as a vertical line just after the "kink" — the point where sustained power begins to decline.

**Two levers to increase FTP via the PD curve:**
1. **Increase mFTP** — lift the curve by increasing the power you can sustain in the quasi-steady state (raise the flat part of the curve)
2. **Extend TTE** — push the kink point to the right by extending the duration you can hold mFTP (widen the flat part of the curve)

Tracking both simultaneously reveals whether an athlete's gains come from raw power improvement or improved endurance at threshold — and where the next gains should be targeted.

**Key insight:** FTP is not specifically a "one hour power" — MLSS can be sustained for 30-70 minutes. For many trained athletes TTE typically falls in the 40-55 minute range, which is why it's often *associated* with an hour but isn't exactly that. The 40km TT (~40 min) was originally proposed as the best practical estimate.

## Interval Prescription — Research Highlights

### Short Intervals (30/15) for Trained Athletes
Ronnestad et al. (2020) showed that 30s-on/15s-off intervals at ~94% Wmax produced superior adaptations vs effort-matched 5-min intervals in elite cyclists (VO2max ~73):
- Peak aerobic power: +3.7% vs -0.3%
- 20-min power: +4.7% vs -1.4%
- Fractional utilization at 4 mmol/L lactate: +3.0 pp vs -3.5 pp

Consider 30/15 blocks for athletes who have plateaued on traditional long-interval structures.

## Fueling Strategy for Training

### During Exercise
- 60-120 g/h CHO (1:0.8 maltodextrin:fructose ratio) for rides >2.5h at/near FTP
- Delivery form doesn't matter — drinks, gels, chews, or mix produce identical oxidation rates (~1.6 g/min peak)
- CHO during rides does NOT blunt adaptation signals (AMPK, p53) when pre-exercise glycogen is high
- Caffeine at 3 mg/kg: reduces RPE ~5%, extends TTE ~21% — use for key sessions and tests

### Train-Low / Compete-High
Periodized CHO availability has strong molecular support (AMPK amplification) but practical limits:
- **Train low (glycogen depleted):** Base/endurance sessions below FTP. Expect ~12% lower power output. Don't use for FTP calibration.
- **Compete high (fully fueled):** Key interval sessions, threshold work, races. Maximize power and session quality.
- **The catch:** Enhanced cellular adaptations do NOT consistently translate to better TT performance.

### Fasted Training Rules
- Only for low-intensity rides (Zone 1-2, well below FTP)
- Never for high-intensity workouts — costs ~3.8% performance
- Eat immediately after fasted sessions
- Flag fasted sessions in WKO5 to avoid misinterpreting lower power as fitness decline

### Keto/LCHF Diets
LCHF impairs performance at threshold+ despite increasing VO2max and fat oxidation (Burke 2017, 2020). Fat requires more O2 per ATP produced, worsening exercise economy. A drop in mFTP during a keto phase does not indicate detraining.

## Interpreting WKO5 Data — Context Flags

Power output is affected by factors beyond fitness. Flag these when reviewing data:

| Context | Effect on Power | Action |
|---------|----------------|--------|
| Fasted session | ~3.8% lower | Don't use for FTP calibration |
| LCHF/keto diet | Lower at threshold+ | Track diet phase separately |
| Heat (not acclimated) | Reduced | Log temperature |
| Heat (post-acclimation) | Up to +6% | Genuine adaptation |
| Altitude | Lower absolute watts | Relative intensity still stresses CV system |
| Caffeine (3mg/kg) | ~21% TTE extension | Note if used for key tests |
| Low glycogen | ~12% lower | Deliberate; amplifies adaptation signals |
| High RPE at normal power | Unchanged watts | Investigate overreaching/illness |
| Early training block | Possible dip | PV expansion precedes RBCV; be patient |

## Training Philosophy

- "Data science supports the art of coaching but does not replace it"
- AI helps with data analysis, but coaching judgment remains essential
- Individualization through measured physiology is the key differentiator of WKO5
- "Each athlete is unique. Though there are similarities in training intensities, individualization is the key to peak success."
- The principle of individualization: training should be adjusted according to age, gender, rate of progress, and previous skill development
- If physiological information is unknown, the coach makes the best guess — which may result in impaired performance
