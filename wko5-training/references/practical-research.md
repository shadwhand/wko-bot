## Interval Structure & Periodization

### Ronnestad et al. 2020 — Short Intervals Beat Long Intervals in Elite Cyclists
**"Superior performance improvements in elite cyclists following short-interval vs effort-matched long-interval training"**
*Scand J Med Sci Sports. 30:849-857*

- 3 weeks (9 sessions) of short intervals (3 x 13 x 30s on / 15s off) vs effort-matched long intervals (4 x 5 min)
- In elite cyclists (VO2max ~73 mL/kg/min):
  - SI: peak aerobic power +3.7%, 20-min power +4.7%, fractional utilization at 4 mmol/L lactate +3.0 pp
  - LI: peak aerobic power -0.3%, 20-min power -1.4%, fractional utilization -3.5 pp
- Mean power during SI work intervals was 441W (94% Wmax) vs LI 368W (79% Wmax) despite matched RPE

**Coaching application:** Short 30/15 intervals allow athletes to accumulate time at very high power (94% Wmax) which drives greater improvements in power at lactate threshold and 20-min power. Consider prescribing 30/15 blocks for athletes looking to improve FTP and fractional utilization, especially well-trained athletes who have plateaued on traditional 5-min interval structures.

## Nutrition & Fueling During Exercise

### Hearris et al. 2022 — 120g/h CHO Works in Any Form
**"13C-glucose-fructose labeling reveals comparable exogenous CHO oxidation during exercise when consuming 120 g/h in fluid, gel, jelly chew, or coingestion"**
*J Appl Physiol. 132:1394-1406*

- Trained cyclists (VO2max 65, PPO 438W) consuming 120 g/h (1:0.8 maltodextrin:fructose)
- Peak exogenous oxidation: ~1.56-1.66 g/min across all forms (drink, gel, chew, mix)
- Oxidation efficiency 72-75%, minimal GI symptoms
- Exercise capacity (TTE at 150% LT) was similar across all forms

**Coaching application:** Athletes can use whatever CHO delivery form they prefer — drinks, gels, chews, or a mix — with identical metabolic results. The 1:0.8 maltodextrin-to-fructose ratio is key to achieving oxidation rates above the ~60 g/h single-transporter ceiling. For rides >2.5h at or near FTP, target 60-120 g/h.

### Fell et al. 2021 — CHO During Rides Doesn't Blunt Adaptation
**"Carbohydrate improves exercise capacity but does not affect subcellular lipid droplet morphology, AMPK and p53 signalling"**
*J Physiol. 599(11):2823-2849*

- 180 min at LT + TTE at 150% LT with 0, 45, or 90 g CHO/h
- CHO improved capacity dose-dependently (90 g/h best)
- CHO did NOT alter glycogen utilization, AMPK phosphorylation (~4-fold), or p53 signaling
- Exercise-induced IMTG reduction (~50% type I, ~30% type IIa fibers) was identical regardless of CHO intake

**Coaching application:** Athletes can fuel long threshold rides with 60-90 g/h CHO without compromising training adaptation signals when pre-exercise glycogen is high. This challenges the simplistic "train low" dogma for during-exercise feeding.

### Pettersson et al. 2019 — CHO Hydrogel: Substrate Shift but No Performance Gain
**"Effects of supplementing with an 18% carbohydrate-hydrogel drink versus a placebo during whole-body exercise"**
*J Int Soc Sports Nutr. 2019*

- Exogenous CHO contributed 27.6% of total energy with hydrogel; peak exogenous oxidation 1.33 g/min
- Fat oxidation decreased 9.5%, endogenous CHO spared 18.1%
- But: time-trial performance was NOT improved (-0.8s, p=0.46)

**Coaching application:** CHO hydrogel is well-tolerated and shifts substrate partitioning, but don't expect automatic performance gains from novel delivery methods. Focus on total CHO intake rate and the glucose:fructose ratio.

### Kumar et al. 2019 — Caffeine Extends Time to Fatigue by 21%
**"Caffeine ingestion with or without low-dose carbohydrate improves exercise tolerance in sedentary adults"**
*Front Nutr. 2019;6:9*

- Caffeine at 3 mg/kg reduced RPE by ~5% and extended TTF by ~21% regardless of training status
- Low-dose CHO alone (~40g) provided no additional benefit
- Trained subjects had 28% higher total EE and lower RER during moderate exercise

**Coaching application:** Caffeine (3 mg/kg) is a reliable ergogenic aid for FTP tests, time trials, and key sessions. It extends TTE during threshold and supra-threshold work. Use strategically for performance tests and key workouts.

### Rauch et al. 2022 — Fat Oxidation Zones Map to Power Zones
**"Feeding tolerance, glucose availability, and whole-body total carbohydrate and fat oxidation"**
*Front Physiol. 12:773054*

- Fatmax (peak fat oxidation) at 63% VO2max ≈ Endurance/low Tempo zone
- Fatmin (fat oxidation ceases) at 94% VO2max ≈ near VO2max efforts
- 43% achieved MFO >1.0 g/min on a mixed diet (not keto)
- Personalized CHO intake of ~1.0 g/kgBM/h optimizes oxidation while minimizing GI distress

**Coaching application:** Zone 2/Endurance rides train fat oxidation capacity. Threshold and above relies entirely on CHO — fuel accordingly. High fat oxidation rates don't require ketogenic diets.

## Fasted Training & CHO Periodization

### Slater et al. 2023 — Fasted Training Costs ~3.8% Performance
**"Fasting before evening exercise reduces net energy intake and increases fat oxidation, but impairs performance"**
*Int J Sport Nutr Exerc Metab. 33:11-22*

- 7-hour fast vs 2-hour post-meal: fasted increased fat oxidation +3.25g/30min but reduced performance by 3.8%
- Fasted: lower VO2, lower motivation/energy/readiness/enjoyment
- Daily energy intake was ~443 kcal lower after fasted exercise (net negative energy balance)

**Coaching application:** Fasted sessions will produce lower wattages. Do NOT use fasted rides to calibrate FTP or power profiles. Reserve fasted training for low-intensity endurance/recovery rides aimed at metabolic adaptation. Flag fasted sessions in WKO5 to avoid misinterpreting lower power as fitness decline.

### Evoke Endurance — Fasted Training: A Nuanced View
*Scott Johnston*

- Do not do every session fasted
- Only fast for low-intensity sessions (Zone 1-2, well below FTP)
- Never do high-intensity workouts fasted — it impairs session quality
- Eat immediately after fasted sessions
- Women may need extra caution due to hormonal considerations
- Risk of elevated cortisol and testosterone suppression with excessive fasted training

### Zouhal et al. 2020 — Exercise Training and Fasting Review
**"Exercise training and fasting: current insights"**
*Open Access J Sports Med. 11:1-28*

- Fasted training increases fat oxidation and can improve long-term endurance
- Little evidence supports fasting-mediated benefits for high-intensity performance
- "Glucose-to-ketone switchover" occurs during fasting
- **Recommendation:** Avoid high-intensity training while fasting

### Train-Low / Compete-High Summary
The molecular evidence (Yeo 2010, Baar & McGee 2008, Hawley & Burke 2010) supports periodized CHO availability:
- **When to train low:** Base/endurance sessions below FTP where maximizing AMPK/PGC-1alpha signaling for mitochondrial adaptation is the goal
- **When to fuel fully:** Key interval sessions, threshold work, races — maximize power output and session quality
- **The catch:** Enhanced metabolic adaptations from train-low do NOT consistently translate to improved time-trial performance. The benefit is cellular adaptation, not acute performance.

## Diet & Performance

### Burke et al. 2017 & 2020 — LCHF/Keto Impairs Performance Despite VO2max Gains
**"Low carbohydrate, high fat diet impairs exercise economy and negates the performance benefit from intensified training in elite race walkers"**
*J Physiol. 595(9):2785-2807 (2017); PLOS ONE 15(6):e0234027 (2020)*

- Elite race walkers, 3 weeks intensified training + HCHO, PCHO, or LCHF diet
- All groups increased VO2peak (~3-5%)
- LCHF dramatically increased fat oxidation (peak 1.57 g/min) BUT increased O2 cost of exercise at race intensities
- Performance results:
  - HCHO: 10km time improved 4.8-6.6%
  - PCHO: improved 5.3% (2017) / trended toward improvement (2020)
  - LCHF: no improvement to 2.3% slower
- No "carryover" benefit after returning to high-CHO
- Replication study (2020) confirmed: impairment is reproducible

**Coaching application:** LCHF/keto diets impair performance at FTP and above because fat requires more O2 per ATP. In WKO5: athletes on LCHF may show decreased power at threshold+ while maintaining/improving endurance zone power. A drop in mFTP during a keto phase does not indicate detraining. Periodized CHO (PCHO) offers a valid compromise.

## Environmental Training

### Pryor et al. 2019 — Heat Acclimation Protocols
**"Application of evidence-based recommendations for heat acclimation"**
*Temperature. 6(1):37-49*

- Heat acclimation improves TTE by ~23%, TT performance by ~7% in hot conditions
- May improve VO2max ~6%, lower LT ~1.0 mmol/kg, improve economy ~2.5% even in cool conditions
- Full HA requires 10-14 consecutive days of 60-90 min in heat (≥30C WBGT); trained athletes adapt in 5-7 days
- Adaptations: lower resting/exercising HR, increased stroke volume, increased plasma volume, lower core temp, increased sweat rate, glycogen sparing, reduced blood lactate

**Coaching application:** During heat acclimation blocks, expect 2-3 weeks of depressed power numbers in WKO5. After HA, potential supercompensation — genuine FTP and VO2max improvements even in thermoneutral conditions (up to 6%). Log environmental conditions (temp, humidity) alongside ride files. TSS during HA underestimates actual physiological stress.

## Subjective Monitoring

### Gross — RPE: The Basis and Why Coaches Should Use It
*TrainingPeaks Coach Blog*

- RPE integrates multiple signals: HR, ventilation, lactate, muscle tension, core temperature
- Two scales: Borg RPE (6-20, correlates with HR 60-200) and Borg CR10 (0-10, ratio scale)
- RPE-power mismatch (high RPE at normally moderate power) signals overreaching, illness, or insufficient recovery

**Coaching application:** Use RPE alongside WKO5 power metrics. When RPE decouples from watts, investigate — accumulated fatigue, sleep debt, illness, psychological stress. RPE is especially useful during deloads, tapers, or when power data seems inconsistent with expected fitness.

## Interpreting Power Data — Context Flags

When reviewing WKO5 power data, these factors can affect power output without reflecting true fitness changes:

| Context | Effect on Power | Action |
|---------|----------------|--------|
| Fasted session | ~3.8% lower | Flag; don't use for FTP calibration |
| LCHF/keto diet | Lower at threshold+ | Track diet phase; don't conflate with detraining |
| Heat (not acclimated) | Reduced | Log temperature; expect suppressed power |
| Heat (post-acclimation) | Up to +6% | Genuine adaptation; may boost mFTP |
| Altitude | Lower absolute watts | AMPK signal may also be blunted; relative intensity still stresses cardiovascular system |
| Caffeine (3mg/kg) | ~5% higher RPE-matched power, ~21% TTE extension | Note if used for key tests |
| Low glycogen / train-low | ~12% lower power | Deliberate; amplifies adaptation signals but reduces session quality |
| High RPE at normal power | No change in watts | Investigate overreaching, fatigue, illness |
| Early training block | Possible plateau/dip | PV expansion precedes RBCV; patience needed |
